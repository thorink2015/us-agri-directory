# US FAA Regulations for Agricultural Drone (UAS) Spraying Operations

> **Current as of April 2026.** This document serves as the foundational regulatory reference for US agricultural drone operations. It covers the primary federal rules, certification processes, exemption pathways, and recent rulemaking activity that govern drone-based aerial application in the United States. Every factual claim is cited to an official source. Where a fact could not be verified from allowed primary sources, it is flagged as "unverified."

## Part 107: the small UAS rule governing drones under 55 pounds

### Applicability to agricultural operations

14 CFR Part 107, the "Small UAS Rule," governs civil operations of unmanned aircraft systems weighing less than **55 pounds at takeoff**, including everything on board or attached.[^1] Agricultural drone spraying operations using drones under 55 lb are subject to Part 107 for general flight rules, but also require compliance with Part 137 (Agricultural Aircraft Operations) when dispensing pesticides, fertilizers, or other agricultural substances.[^2] Section 107.36 prohibits carriage of hazardous material and is **not waivable**, so operators must obtain an exemption from this section before conducting spray operations.[^2]

The full regulatory text of Part 107 is codified at 14 CFR Part 107.[^3]

[^1]: https://www.faa.gov/newsroom/small-unmanned-aircraft-systems-uas-regulations-part-107
[^2]: https://www.faa.gov/uas/advanced_operations/dispensing_chemicals
[^3]: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-F/part-107

### Remote Pilot Certificate: eligibility, testing, and application

To become a certificated remote pilot under Part 107, a person must meet the following eligibility requirements:[^4]

- Be at least **16 years old**
- Be able to read, speak, write, and understand **English**
- Be in a **physical and mental condition** to safely fly a drone (self-certified on FAA Form 8710-13; no FAA medical certificate required)
- Pass the initial **Unmanned Aircraft General, Small (UAG) aeronautical knowledge test**

The UAG knowledge test covers applicable regulations, airspace classification, aviation weather, small UAS loading and performance, emergency procedures, crew resource management, radio communication procedures, physiological effects of drugs and alcohol, aeronautical decision-making, airport operations, maintenance and preflight inspection, and operation at night.[^4] The test is taken at an FAA-approved Knowledge Testing Center. Testing centers charge approximately **$175** for the exam.[^5] No documented pre-test training is required by regulation, although the FAA recommends its Remote Pilot Study Guide.[^4]

**Application process (step by step):**

1. Create an IACRA (Integrated Airman Certification and Rating Application) profile at iacra.faa.gov to obtain an FAA Tracking Number (FTN).
2. Schedule and pass the UAG knowledge test at an approved testing center.
3. Complete FAA Form 8710-13 electronically via IACRA.
4. The application is submitted for a **TSA security background check**, which typically completes within 10 business days.
5. Upon successful TSA vetting, a confirmation email provides instructions for printing a temporary certificate from IACRA.
6. The permanent certificate is sent via U.S. mail.[^4]

**Alternate path for existing Part 61 certificate holders:** Pilots who hold a certificate under 14 CFR Part 61 (not a student certificate) and have completed a flight review within the previous 24 months may instead take the free Part 107 Small UAS Initial online training course (ALC-451) on the FAASTeam website, then complete the IACRA application and validate identity at an FSDO, DPE, ACR, or CFI.[^4]

**Fees:** The knowledge test fee (approximately $175) is paid to the testing center. UAS registration costs $5 per drone and is valid for 3 years. Online training courses on FAASTeam are free. No separate FAA certificate issuance fee applies.[^5][^6]

### Recurrent training requirements

The Operations Over People and Night Operations final rule (published January 15, 2021; effective April 21, 2021; 86 FR 4314) **replaced the in-person recurrent knowledge test** with free online recurrent training.[^7][^8] Certificate holders must complete this training **every 24 calendar months** to maintain aeronautical knowledge recency.[^4]

Two courses are available on the FAASTeam website (faasafety.gov), both at no cost:[^9]

- **ALC-677:** For any Part 107 remote pilot certificate holder, regardless of Part 61 status.
- **ALC-515:** For Part 107 remote pilots who also hold a Part 61 certificate with a current flight review.

Any Part 107 certificate holder who received their certificate before April 6, 2021, must take the **updated** recurrent training (which includes night operations content) before operating at night or over people. Recurrent training completed before that date does not satisfy the updated requirements.[^7]

Note: The Recreational UAS Safety Test (TRUST) is a separate, one-time test for recreational flyers only. It is not the recurrent training required of Part 107 pilots.[^10]

### Night operations rules

Effective April 21, 2021, Part 107 permits routine small UAS operations at night **without a waiver**, provided two conditions are met:[^7]

1. The remote pilot in command has completed the **updated initial knowledge test or the updated online recurrent training** (which includes night operations knowledge areas).
2. The small unmanned aircraft is equipped with **lighted anti-collision lighting visible for at least 3 statute miles** with a flash rate sufficient to avoid a collision.

Daylight is defined as 30 minutes before official sunrise to 30 minutes after official sunset, local time. Operations during twilight also require anti-collision lighting. Night is any time outside the daylight and twilight window.[^1] All existing Part 107 night waivers were cancelled after May 17, 2021.[^11]

Agricultural spraying is often conducted during early morning or late evening hours to minimize wind and maximize chemical efficacy. Any spraying before 30 minutes prior to sunrise or after 30 minutes past sunset constitutes night operations and triggers the anti-collision lighting and updated training requirements.[^7]

[^11]: https://www.faa.gov/uas/commercial_operators/part_107_waivers/waivers_issued

### Operations over people: Categories 1 through 4

The Operations Over People final rule established four categories determining when a small UAS may fly over people who are not directly participating in the operation:[^7]

**Category 1.** The UAS must weigh **0.55 pounds or less** (including everything on board) and contain no exposed rotating parts that could cause lacerations. May operate over people but not over open-air assemblies unless compliant with Remote ID.[^7]

**Category 2.** Performance-based eligibility for UAS weighing more than 0.55 lb. The manufacturer must demonstrate (via a Declaration of Compliance or accepted Means of Compliance) that impact injury severity would not exceed defined thresholds. May not operate over open-air assemblies unless Remote ID compliant.[^7]

**Category 3.** Performance-based eligibility for UAS weighing more than 0.55 lb with additional restrictions. May **not** fly over open-air assemblies. May fly over people only if the operation is within a **closed- or restricted-access site** where all people are on notice, or if the UAS does not maintain sustained flight over any person who is not directly participating.[^7]

**Category 4.** For small UAS with an **airworthiness certificate under Part 21**. May operate over people if the approved Flight Manual permits it. Must comply with Remote ID for sustained flight over open-air assemblies. Requires ongoing maintenance and airworthiness record-keeping.[^7]

**Relevance to agricultural work:** Most agricultural spray drones weigh well over 0.55 lb, so Category 1 does not apply. **Category 3** is the most relevant for ag spraying because its restriction to closed or restricted-access sites aligns with agricultural fields where access can be controlled and workers can be notified. In practice, ag operators should design spray operations so that no non-participants are overflown. The hazardous material dimension (the exemption from Section 107.36) and Part 137 requirements must also be addressed regardless of operations-over-people category.[^2]

### Operating limitations relevant to agricultural work

**Maximum altitude (Section 107.51):** 400 feet above ground level (AGL). The UAS may fly higher than 400 ft AGL if it remains within 400 feet of a structure.[^1]

**Visual line of sight (Section 107.31):** The UAS must remain within VLOS of the remote pilot in command and the person manipulating the flight controls, or within VLOS of a designated visual observer. Vision must be unaided by any device other than corrective lenses. BVLOS operations require a waiver or exemption.[^1][^12]

**Maximum ground speed (Section 107.51):** 100 mph (87 knots).[^1]

**Weather minimums (Section 107.51):** Minimum flight visibility of **3 statute miles** from the control station. The UAS must remain below any cloud layer.[^1]

**Airspace restrictions and LAANC:** Operations in Class G airspace require no ATC permission. Operations in Class B, C, D, or surface E airspace require **ATC authorization**. The Low Altitude Authorization and Notification Capability (LAANC) provides near-real-time airspace authorization at more than 530 FAA ATC facilities covering 726+ airports. Requests at or below UAS Facility Map altitudes may be auto-approved. For non-LAANC airports, manual authorization through FAADroneZone should be submitted at least 60 days in advance.[^13][^14]

**Right of way:** Remote pilots must **always yield right of way to all manned aircraft**. This limitation cannot be waived.[^1][^12]

**Accident reporting (Section 107.9):** The remote PIC must report to the FAA within **10 calendar days** any operation resulting in serious injury, loss of consciousness, or damage to property (other than the UAS itself) exceeding **$500** in repair cost or fair market value.[^15]

## Part 137: agricultural aircraft operations and the dispensing trigger

### When Part 137 is required for drone ag ops

14 CFR Part 137 governs agricultural aircraft operations, defined in Section 137.3 as the operation of an aircraft for the purpose of:[^16]

1. Dispensing any **economic poison** (substances intended for preventing, destroying, repelling, or mitigating pests, or intended for use as plant regulators, defoliants, or desiccants).
2. Dispensing any other substance intended for **plant nourishment, soil treatment, propagation of plant life, or pest control**.
3. Engaging in **dispensing activities directly affecting agriculture, horticulture, or forest preservation** (excluding live insects).

Under Section 137.11(a), no person may conduct agricultural aircraft operations without an Agricultural Aircraft Operator Certificate (AAOC) issued under Part 137.[^16] Drones are legally "aircraft" operating in the National Airspace System, so when they dispense substances covered by Section 137.3, they trigger Part 137 requirements.[^17][^2]

[^16]: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-G/part-137
[^17]: https://www.faa.gov/hazmat/air_carriers/operations/drones

### How Part 137 interacts with Part 107

For most drone agricultural spraying operations, **both Part 107 and Part 137 certifications are required**, along with exemptions from certain sections of each.[^2]

| Requirement | Source |
|---|---|
| Remote Pilot Certificate | Part 107 |
| UAS registration | Part 107 / Part 48 |
| Operating limitations (altitude, speed, VLOS) | Part 107 |
| Exemption from Section 107.36 (hazmat carriage) | Exemption to Part 107 |
| Agricultural Aircraft Operator Certificate (AAOC) | Part 137 |
| Knowledge and skills test | Part 137 Section 137.19(e) |
| Recordkeeping | Part 137 Section 137.71 |
| Congested area restrictions | Part 137 Sections 137.51 through 137.59 |

For UAS **55 lb or heavier**, the aircraft operates under Part 91 and Part 137, requiring exemptions from Parts 61, 91, and 137, plus a Section 44807 exemption.[^2]

Key guidance documents governing this interaction include Advisory Circular AC 137-1B ("Certification Process for Agricultural Aircraft Operators"), which explicitly addresses UAS operations,[^18] and FAA Notice 8900.741 (issued June 9, 2025, active through June 9, 2026), which provides the current streamlined UAS Part 137 certification process.[^19]

[^18]: https://www.faa.gov/documentLibrary/media/Advisory_Circular/AC_137-1B.pdf
[^19]: https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/orders_notices/index.cfm/go/document.information/documentID/1043917

### Application process for the Agricultural Aircraft Operator Certificate

The FAA maintains a **streamlined certification process for UAS-only operators** under Notice 8900.741. The process is centralized through the 137 UAS Operations Office rather than local FSDOs.[^19][^2]

**Step 1: Register the UAS.** Drones under 55 lb receive an FA-number via FAADroneZone ($5). Drones 55 lb or heavier receive an N-number via Civil Aviation Registry Electronic Services (CARES) or by mail.[^2][^6]

**Step 2: Petition for exemption.** Submit a petition at least **120 days** before operations begin (per 14 CFR Section 11.63(d)). The petition seeks exemption from Section 107.36 (hazmat carriage) and from several Part 137 sections designed for manned aircraft. Submit by commenting on shell docket **FAA-2007-0001-0001** at regulations.gov.[^2]

**Step 3: Submit basic operational information.** Provide the applicant's full legal name and address (no P.O. Box), Chief Supervisor of Operations name and contact information, PIC name and remote pilot certificate number, and aircraft make, model, and registration number (FA or N number).[^2]

**Step 4: Receive the exemption.** The FAA reviews the petition and, if approved, issues a Grant of Exemption with specific conditions and limitations.

**Step 5: Apply for the AAOC.** Complete **FAA Form 8710-3** (Agricultural Aircraft Operator Certification Application) and submit it with the exemption number by email to **UAS137Certificates@faa.gov**. The applicant name on the form must match the name on the exemption.[^2][^20]

**Step 6: Complete the knowledge and skills test.** Under the streamlined UAS process, these tests **may be self-administered** by the operator. Documentation of satisfactory completion must include the date, PIC name, FAA certificate number, and legal signature, and must be provided to the FAA upon request.[^21]

**Step 7: Certificate issuance.** The 137 UAS Operations Office issues the AAOC.

[^20]: https://www.faa.gov/documentLibrary/media/Form/FAA-Form_8710-3.pdf
[^21]: https://www.faa.gov/documentLibrary/media/Notice/N_8900.659.pdf

### Required documents, knowledge test, and congested area plan

**Knowledge test topics** (per Section 137.19(e) and AC 137-1B) include: steps before starting operations (including area survey), safe handling and disposal of economic poisons, general effects of economic poisons on plants, animals, and persons, primary symptoms of poisoning and emergency measures, safe flight and application procedures, and aircraft operating limitations. UAS-specific topics include: performance within the proposed flight envelope, engine power loss mitigation, lost link and emergency recovery procedures, communication between PIC and visual observer, see-and-avoid methods, and loss-of-sight-of-UA procedures.[^18]

**Skills test requirements** (per AC 137-1B Sections 2.6.12 through 2.6.12.2) involve loading aircraft tanks or hoppers with suitable material (water, lime, or sand) to maximum weight. Evaluation covers crew coordination, takeoffs, approach to the working area, swath runs (four or more passes at consistent altitude within plus or minus 5 feet), pullups and turnarounds, cleanup swaths, load jettisoning, rapid deceleration (for rotorcraft), and landing. UAS-specific items include communication during normal and lost communications, operating per operational documents, and risk mitigation.[^18]

**Congested area plan (CAP):** Required for any dispensing over congested areas. The plan must include an aerial photograph or map of the work area with obstructions marked, altitudes and airspeeds, dispensing rate and material type, dates and hours of operation, ATC coordination, emergency landing areas, and applicable exemptions or waivers. Prior written approval from the appropriate elected official or governing body is required. Public notice must be given at least **48 hours** before operations begin.[^18][^22]

**Insurance:** Part 137 does **not** impose a federal insurance requirement. The FAA does not mandate proof of insurance or financial responsibility under this rule. However, commercial clients, landowners, and many state pesticide licensing programs require proof of liability insurance. State laws vary.[^18]

[^22]: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/part-137/section-137.53

### Costs and timeline

**FAA fees:** There is **no FAA application fee** for a Part 137 AAOC. The FAA does not charge for the AAOC application, knowledge test, or skills test. Associated costs include drone registration ($5 per aircraft) and the Part 107 knowledge test (approximately $175 at a testing center).[^5][^2]

**Preparation costs:** Third-party consulting firms offer Part 137 application preparation packages (regulatory consulting, manuals, exemption drafting) at varying prices, typically starting around $600. The NAAA publishes a free guide to starting a Part 137 operation.[^23]

**Timeline:** The exemption petition must be filed at least **120 days** before the desired effective date.[^2] The certification team reviews the application within **30 business days** of receipt (per AC 137-1B Section 2.4.2).[^18] Applicants are given **15 calendar days** to respond to any notices of corrective action.[^23] The overall realistic timeline from initial filing to certificate issuance is **4 to 8 months**, depending on the completeness of the initial submission and the speed of FAA response. The streamlined UAS process (centralized at UAS137Certificates@faa.gov rather than individual FSDOs) was specifically designed to reduce processing time.[^19]

[^23]: https://www.agaviation.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/137-Certification-Guide.pdf

### Recordkeeping and reporting requirements

**Records that must be kept (Section 137.71):** Commercial agricultural aircraft operators must maintain and keep current at their home base of operations: (1) the name and address of each person for whom services were provided, (2) the date of service, (3) the name and quantity of material dispensed for each operation, and (4) the name, address, and certificate number of each pilot used, along with the date that pilot met the knowledge and skill requirements. Records must be kept for **at least 12 months** and made available for inspection by the FAA upon request. Private operators are not required by regulation to keep these records.[^24]

**Change of address (Section 137.75):** Operators must notify the FAA in writing in advance of any change in home base address.[^25]

**Termination (Section 137.77):** When ceasing operations, the certificate holder must surrender the certificate to the responsible Flight Standards office.[^25]

**Deviation reporting (Section 137.1(c)):** Any person who deviates from Part 137 rules under the public emergency provision must send a complete report to the responsible Flight Standards office within **10 days** of the deviation.[^25]

**EPA coordination:** Part 137 does not impose EPA-specific requirements, but operators must independently comply with EPA regulations. Economic poisons used in interstate commerce must be registered with the EPA under the Federal Insecticide, Fungicide, and Rodenticide Act (FIFRA). The EPA Worker Protection Standard (WPS) applies to agricultural pesticide operations. State pesticide applicator licenses (separate from FAA certification) are typically required.[^18]

[^24]: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-G/part-137/subpart-D/section-137.71
[^25]: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-G/part-137/subpart-D

## Exemptions and waivers for drones over 55 pounds

### Section 44807 exemption process

49 U.S.C. Section 44807 ("Special Authority for Certain Unmanned Aircraft Systems") grants the FAA authority to use a **risk-based approach** to authorize UAS operations that exceed Part 107 parameters, primarily for drones weighing 55 lb or more. This authority was originally enacted as Section 333 in the FAA Modernization and Reform Act of 2012 and was replaced by Section 44807 in the FAA Reauthorization Act of 2018.[^26]

Agricultural spraying represents the **vast majority of exemptions** for UAS above 55 lb. As of mid-2025, over **1,700 operator certificates** have been issued for agricultural UAS operations under this authority.[^27]

Common heavy agricultural drones requiring Section 44807 exemptions include models such as the DJI Agras T40 and T50, XAG P100, and Hylio AG-230 and AG-272, all of which exceed 55 lb at takeoff with a full payload.

[^26]: https://www.faa.gov/uas/advanced_operations/certification/section_44807
[^27]: https://www.faa.gov/newsroom/BVLOS_NPRM_website_version.pdf

### What an exemption package must contain

The application process for a Section 44807 exemption involves multiple steps:[^2][^26]

**Petition for exemption:** File by commenting on shell docket **FAA-2007-0001-0001** at regulations.gov. The petition must comply with 14 CFR Section 11.81 and be submitted at least **120 days** before the desired effective date. Required documentation includes a Concept of Operations, Operations Manual, Emergency Procedures, Checklists, Maintenance Manual, Training Program, Flight History (hours, cycles, accidents), and a **Safety Risk Analysis** for complex operations (including BVLOS, multi-UAS, or operations over people).[^2]

**Request for Information (RFI):** The FAA sends an RFI requiring the applicant's legal name and address, Chief Supervisor of Operations contact information, PIC name and certificate number, aircraft make, model, and N-number. Failure to respond results in closure of the petition.[^2]

**Certificate of Waiver or Authorization (COA):** After receiving the exemption, operators apply for a COA through the COA Application Processing System (CAPS) at caps.faa.gov. The FAA issues a **blanket COA** for flights at or below 400 ft AGL in Class G airspace to all operators with Section 44807 exemptions. The COA is valid for **2 years** from issuance of the Grant of Exemption.[^28]

**Part 137 AAOC:** Operators must then complete FAA Form 8710-3 and submit it with their exemption number to UAS137Certificates@faa.gov to obtain the AAOC before conducting agricultural spraying.[^2]

Over-55-lb drones must be registered with an **N-number** through the Civil Aviation Registry (CARES at cares.faa.gov or by mail), and they operate under **Part 91 and Part 137**, requiring exemptions from regulations in Parts 61, 91, and 137.[^2]

[^28]: https://www.faa.gov/uas/advanced_operations/certification/section_44807/49-USC-44807-Blanket-COA-Template

### Processing times and conditions

The FAA specifies only the **120-day minimum** filing requirement as a formal temporal guideline. No specific published guidance on exact processing timelines exists. The FAA has noted that processing delays are "often caused due to illegible submissions" and incomplete information. Requests for non-routine operations "may take longer to review."[^2]

Standard blanket COA conditions include: maximum groundspeed of **30 mph** (or manufacturer maximum, whichever is less), maximum altitude of **400 ft AGL**, VLOS requirement (unless additional waiver obtained), Remote ID compliance, Remote Pilot Certificate for PIC, and Part 137 AAOC.[^28][^21]

### Examples of granted exemptions

**Hylio AG-230 (February-March 2024):** On February 28, 2024, Hylio received a Section 44807 exemption for agricultural operations with the AG-230 (over 55 lb). On March 3, 2024, the FAA issued a blanket COA allowing operation of **up to three Hylio AG-230 drones simultaneously by a single PIC**, without a visual observer, during night VLOS, in VMC, for commercial agricultural services throughout the United States. The FAA stated this blanket COA "will be used throughout industry as the standard template for all 44807 blanket COAs."[^29]

**Precision Drone LLC (April 2015):** Exemption No. 11316 (Docket FAA-2014-1063) was granted under the predecessor Section 333 authority for agricultural crop monitoring with the Precision Drone Pacesetter.[^30]

The full list of approved drones under Section 44807 is available on regulatory docket FAA-2023-1271 at regulations.gov.[^26]

[^29]: https://www.faa.gov/uas/programs_partnerships/beyond
[^30]: https://www.faa.gov/media/38976

### Section 927 waiver process (new as of April 2026)

Section 927 of the FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 created a **new waiver authority** complementing (not replacing) the existing exemption process. Published April 1, 2026, in the Federal Register (91 FR 16276, Docket FAA-2026-1100), it allows the FAA to grant regulatory relief via waivers without requiring the "public interest" showing needed for exemptions and without notice-and-comment periods. Waiver requests are submitted via email to **927waivers@faa.gov**. Four eligibility categories apply: minimal-impact operations, high-value limited use cases, emerging technology demonstrations, and other safety considerations. This pathway is not agriculture-specific but is available to all UAS operators seeking regulatory relief.[^31]

[^31]: https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/04/01/2026-06297/implementing-section-927-waiver-process-for-certain-unmanned-aircraft-operations

## BVLOS: beyond visual line of sight operations

### Current waiver process under Section 107.31

Operators seeking BVLOS authorization for small UAS (under 55 lb) apply through FAA DroneZone for a Part 107 waiver to Section 107.31. The application must demonstrate **Detect and Avoid (DAA) capability** and show the UAS "does not endanger life or property of another." The FAA evaluates applications on a risk-based tier system: low-risk operations (remote areas) may require only applicant data, medium-risk operations need third-party DAA validation, and high-risk operations may require FAA certification of the DAA system.[^32]

For large UAS or Part 91 operations, operators submit FAA Form 7711-2 to **9-UAS-91.113Waivers@faa.gov**, processed by the UAS Policy Team (AJV-P22) at FAA Headquarters.[^33]

The FAA has not published specific BVLOS waiver approval rates. BVLOS waivers remain difficult to obtain and are granted on a case-by-case basis.[^32]

[^32]: https://www.faa.gov/sites/faa.gov/files/uas/commercial_operators/part_107_waivers/Part-107-Waiver-Section-Specific-Evaluation-Information.pdf
[^33]: https://www.faa.gov/uas/advanced_operations/instructions-drone-operators-completing-faa-form-7711-2

### The proposed Part 108 BVLOS rule

The FAA published an NPRM titled "Normalizing Unmanned Aircraft Systems Beyond Visual Line of Sight Operations" on **August 7, 2025** (90 FR 38212, Document No. 2025-14992, Docket FAA-2025-1908). This 180-page joint rulemaking by the FAA and TSA proposes creating a new **14 CFR Part 108** for BVLOS operations.[^34]

Key provisions of the proposed Part 108:

- Creates performance-based regulations for UAS operations at low altitudes (generally 400 ft AGL or below)
- Establishes two tiers: **Permitted Operations** (simpler, lower-risk) and **Certificated Operations** (more complex)
- Includes specific agricultural provisions at proposed Sections 108.445 (permitted) and 108.575 (certificated)
- Proposes a maximum UAS weight limit of **1,320 lb** under Part 108
- Creates new right-of-way rules: UAS under Part 108 would have right-of-way over aircraft not broadcasting ADS-B Out or electronic conspicuity
- Introduces "Shielded Areas" (within 50 feet of powerlines) where UAS would have right-of-way regardless
- Proposes that agricultural UAS operating under Part 108 would be **exempt from Part 137 requirements** (by amending Section 137.1 to read "Except for aircraft subject to part 108 of this chapter")
- No airman certificates required under Part 108; instead, operators would need an "operations supervisor" and a "qualified flight coordinator"
- Also creates 14 CFR Part 146 for Automated Data Service Providers (UAS Traffic Management)
- Would amend Parts 36, 43, 45, 48, 89, 91, 107, 119, 133, 135, and 137[^34]

**Timeline and current status as of April 2026:**

|------|-------|
| June 6, 2025 | Executive Order 14307 "Unleashing American Drone Dominance" directs NPRM within 30 days, final rule within 240 days[^35] |
| August 7, 2025 | NPRM published in Federal Register[^34] |
| September 29, 2025 | FAA denied requests to extend comment period[^36] |
| October 6, 2025 | Initial comment period closed; approximately 3,100 comments received[^34] |
| January 28, 2026 | FAA reopened comment period on right-of-way, ADS-B Out, and electronic conspicuity provisions[^37] |

As of April 2026, the NPRM is in **post-comment review**. The EO's 240-day target for a final rule (approximately February 2026) has passed without publication. The reopened comment period and the volume of comments suggest the final rule timeline has slipped. **No final rule has been published.**

### MOSAIC is not a UAS rule

MOSAIC (Modernization of Special Airworthiness Certification) is a **manned aircraft rule** governing Light-Sport Aircraft (LSA) and Sport Pilot privileges. It was published as a final rule on July 24, 2025, with effective dates of October 22, 2025 (pilot and maintenance rules) and July 24, 2026 (LSA certification rules). MOSAIC removes weight limits for LSA, adds new aircraft types, and enables certain aerial work with light-sport aircraft. It has **no relevance to UAS operations**. The correct current FAA UAS rulemaking activity is the Part 108 BVLOS NPRM described above.[^39][^40]

[^39]: https://www.faa.gov/aircraft/MOSAIC
[^40]: https://www.faa.gov/newsroom/modernization-special-airworthiness-certification-mosaic-fact-sheet

### Practical implications for ag operators today

**What ag operators can do now:** Apply for individual BVLOS waivers or exemptions on a case-by-case basis, though these are resource-intensive and have uncertain timelines. For drones 55 lb or heavier under Section 44807, operators can obtain exemptions and blanket COAs for VLOS agricultural spraying through a well-established pathway. The Hylio blanket COA (March 2024) expanded operating authority to include multi-UAS night VLOS operations but did **not** authorize BVLOS.[^29]

**What is not yet available:** Routine BVLOS for agriculture is not authorized under any existing rule. The proposed Part 108 would create this pathway, but the final rule has not been published. No scalable BVLOS permits exist for agricultural operations as of April 2026.

## Swarm and multi-drone operations

### Current rules under Section 107.35

14 CFR Section 107.35 states: "A person may not manipulate flight controls or act as a remote pilot in command or visual observer in the operation of more than one unmanned aircraft at the same time."[^41] Under the baseline Part 107 rule, each airborne drone requires a separate certificated remote pilot. A single person cannot simultaneously control multiple drones or serve as PIC or visual observer for more than one drone at a time.

[^41]: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-F/part-107/subpart-B/section-107.35

### Waiver requirements and granted swarm waivers

Waivers of Section 107.35 are submitted through FAA DroneZone (transitioning to the Aviation Safety Hub). Applications must demonstrate: system capability for simultaneous multi-aircraft control, containment measures (geo-fencing, pre-programmed paths), see-and-avoid capability while operating multiple aircraft, flight termination capability for each individual aircraft, failure response procedures for single and multiple simultaneous failures, command and control link redundancy, buffer zones and standoff distances, minimum pilot qualifications and experience (including multi-UAS simulation training), and minimum personnel (typically one RPIC plus two or more visual observers).[^42][^43]

The FAA has published a representative sample of an approved Section 107.35 waiver application showing approval for operating **4 octocopters simultaneously** for vineyard monitoring over a 40-acre agricultural property. Conditions included Class G airspace, below 400 ft AGL, daylight operations, 3+ miles visibility, geo-fencing, pre-programmed flight paths, 50-foot separation between aircraft, minimum 1 RPIC plus 2 visual observers, and flight termination capability per aircraft.[^42]

The Hylio AG-230 blanket COA (March 2024) represents a separate, significant multi-drone precedent for ag operations under the Section 44807 pathway: **up to three heavy ag drones operated by a single PIC** without visual observers for commercial agricultural services.[^29]

The FAA generally responds to waiver requests within **90 days**, depending on complexity.[^1]

[^42]: https://www.faa.gov/sites/faa.gov/files/uas/resources/policy_library/section_352_responses/107_35_for_section_352.pdf
[^43]: https://www.faa.gov/sites/faa.gov/files/uas/commercial_operators/part_107_waivers/Part-107-Waiver-Section-Specific-Evaluation-Information.pdf

### Practical relevance to large ag operations

The one-pilot-one-drone rule is a major operational bottleneck for large-scale agricultural spraying. Agricultural drones with 10-gallon hopper capacity can treat only about 5 acres per load at typical application rates, meaning multiple drones must run simultaneously to cover large farms efficiently.[^44] The Hylio blanket COA template is expected to make multi-drone approvals more accessible for over-55-lb ag operations. The FAA's May 2025 Drone Integration Concept of Operations envisions normalization of multi-drone operations as part of broader BVLOS and advanced operations rulemaking.[^45]

[^44]: https://www.agaviation.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/07/202307-faa-letter-uas-137-certification.pdf
[^45]: https://www.faa.gov/uas/resources/policy_library/Drone-Integration-Concept-of-Operations-May-2025.pdf

## Weight class rules and registration

### Under 55 pounds vs. over 55 pounds

**Under 55 lb:** Part 107 governs operations. Requires a Remote Pilot Certificate, FAA registration, and compliance with Part 107 operating rules. Waivers available for specific operational limitations under Section 107.205. For ag spraying, also requires an exemption from Section 107.36 (hazmat) and Part 137 certification.[^1][^2]

**Over 55 lb:** Part 107 does **not** apply. Civil operators must obtain authorization via Section 44807 Grant of Exemption (the most common path for ag drones), a type certificate or special airworthiness certificate, or public aircraft operations authority. These aircraft operate under Part 91 and Part 137 for agricultural work, with exemptions from Parts 61, 91, and 137.[^26][^46][^2]

The 55-lb threshold includes the total takeoff weight: aircraft plus payload (including the substance being dispensed).[^2]

[^46]: https://www.faa.gov/faq/what-are-requirements-operation-uas-weigh-excess-55-pounds

### Registration requirements by weight tier

**Under 0.55 lb (250g):** Recreational flyers are not required to register. Part 107 (commercial) operators must register **all drones regardless of weight**.[^47]

**0.55 lb to 55 lb:** Registration required for all operators. Part 107 registration costs **$5 per drone**, valid for 3 years, done through FAADroneZone. Each drone receives a unique registration number. The registration number must be displayed on the exterior of the aircraft. Operators must carry registration certificate (paper or digital) when flying. Failure to register carries civil penalties up to **$27,500** and potential criminal penalties up to **$250,000** and/or 3 years imprisonment.[^6]

**Over 55 lb:** Must register for an **N-number** through the FAA's Civil Aviation Registry Electronic Services (CARES) or by mail using the Part 47 paper-based process. Fee is $5.[^48][^2]

[^47]: https://www.faa.gov/faq/do-i-need-register-my-drone-and-if-so-how-do-i-register
[^48]: https://www.faa.gov/faq/if-my-uas-or-drone-weighs-more-55-lbs-what-are-registration-requirements

### Remote ID compliance

Remote ID (14 CFR Part 89) requires drones in flight to broadcast identification and location information via RF (Wi-Fi or Bluetooth). Required broadcast elements include UAS identification (serial number or session ID), latitude, longitude, geometric altitude, velocity, control station or takeoff location, emergency status, and time mark.[^49][^50]

**Three compliance methods:**

1. **Standard Remote ID:** Built-in broadcast capability. Must be listed on an FAA-accepted Declaration of Compliance. Operators may choose to broadcast serial number or session ID.[^49]
2. **Remote ID Broadcast Module:** A retrofit device broadcasting identification, location, and takeoff location. Operators using a broadcast module **must maintain VLOS at all times**.[^49]
3. **FAA-Recognized Identification Area (FRIA):** Drones without Remote ID may fly only within FRIAs, requested by community-based organizations and educational institutions. Operations must remain within VLOS and FRIA boundaries.[^49]

**Compliance timeline:** The manufacturer production deadline was September 16, 2022. The operational compliance deadline was extended from September 16, 2023, to **March 16, 2024**.[^51] On that date, the FAA **ended its discretionary enforcement policy** and stated: "Operators who do not comply after that date could face fines and suspension or revocation of their drone pilot certificates."[^52]

All registered agricultural drones must comply with Remote ID. Recently issued waivers (e.g., December 2025) explicitly require that the UAS "must be a standard remote identification unmanned aircraft listed on the FAA-accepted declaration of compliance list."[^53] The FAA maintains a searchable Declaration of Compliance system at uasdoc.faa.gov.[^54]

[^50]: https://www.faa.gov/sites/faa.gov/files/uas/getting_started/remote_id/RemoteID_Executive_Summary.pdf

## What changed in 2025 and 2026

### Executive Order 14307 and the BVLOS NPRM

The single most consequential regulatory development in 2025 was the publication of the BVLOS NPRM. Executive Order 14307, "Unleashing American Drone Dominance" (June 6, 2025), directed the FAA to publish the proposed rule within 30 days and finalize it within 240 days.[^35] The NPRM was published August 7, 2025, proposing a new Part 108 framework for BVLOS operations including agriculture-specific provisions. Over **1 million comments** were received. The FAA reopened the comment period in January 2026 on electronic conspicuity and right-of-way provisions.[^37] As of April 2026, the final rule has not been published. If finalized as proposed, Part 108 would be the most significant change to agricultural drone regulation since Part 107, potentially eliminating the need for Part 137 certification for UAS operating under the new rule.[^34]

### FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 implementation

The FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 (Public Law 118-63), signed May 16, 2024, authorized FAA operations through FY2028.[^55] Key UAS provisions and their implementation status:

- **Section 930** (49 U.S.C. Section 44811): Requires FAA to establish performance-based regulations for BVLOS operations. The BVLOS NPRM directly implements this section.[^34]
- **Section 927:** New waiver authority for UAS operations. Implemented via final rule published April 1, 2026 (91 FR 16276), creating a faster alternative to the exemption process.[^31]
- **Section 925:** Establishes $6 million per year for FY2025-2028 for UAS Test Ranges. Active through the FAA's Broad Agency Announcement (BAA) program.[^56]
- **Extension of Section 44807 authority** for risk-based UAS authorizations.[^26]
- No specific agricultural drone provisions were found in the Reauthorization Act itself, though agriculture is listed as an intended use case in the BVLOS NPRM.

[^55]: https://www.faa.gov/about/reauthorization
[^56]: https://www.faa.gov/uas/programspartnerships/BAA

### Other notable 2025 and 2026 developments

**Notice 8900.741 (June 9, 2025):** Updated and reissued guidance on the streamlined Part 137 UAS certification process, replacing Notice 8900.704. Confirms the FAA's determination that "part 137 UAS agricultural operations present a lower risk than other certificated operations." Active through June 9, 2026.[^19]

**Drone Integration Concept of Operations (May 2025):** The FAA published a conceptual vision for the next decade of drone integration, outlining regulatory frameworks for routine, scalable BVLOS operations at low altitudes, with agriculture explicitly listed among enabled operations.[^45]

**Detect and Avoid criteria update (March 6, 2025):** The FAA updated criteria for substantiating DAA solutions for BVLOS operations, primarily for public safety organizations.[^57]

**FCC UAS spectrum rules (effective February 7, 2025):** New Part 88 rules provide UAS operators direct frequency assignments in the 5030-5091 MHz band for non-networked control link communications.[^58]

**FAA compliance enforcement:** The FAA disclosed that it has "become aware of exemption holders conducting commercial agricultural operations in a manner noncompliant with the conditions and limitations of their exemption" and began sending Requests for Information to verify compliance with basic requirements including Remote ID, Remote Pilot Certificate, and UAS registration.[^2]

**NAAA UAS Issue Brief (revised January 2025):** NAAA updated its core policy positions on UAS integration, advocating for mandatory ADS-B In on all UAS, FAA-certified DAA technology, visible strobe lighting, pilot certificates and medical certificates for UAS pilots, 48-72 hour advance NOTAM filing, and preservation of manned aircraft right-of-way.[^59]

[^57]: https://www.faa.gov/uas/public_safety_gov/public_safety_toolkit/CMD-DAA_March_2025_Final.pdf
[^58]: https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/01/08/2024-29967/spectrum-rules-and-policies-for-the-operation-of-unmanned-aircraft-systems
[^59]: https://www.agaviation.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/2025-01-Issue-Brief-UAS.pdf

### NAAA advocacy positions and tracked rule changes

NAAA (representing approximately **1,560 manned aerial application operators** and **2,028 agricultural pilots**) has been the most vocal industry group on agricultural drone regulation.[^44] As of October 2025, NAAA reported **1,082 registered Part 137 drone operations** in the United States.[^60]

NAAA's core positions on the BVLOS NPRM include: opposition to the proposed right-of-way reversal (giving UAS priority over aircraft not broadcasting ADS-B Out or electronic conspicuity), adamant opposition to "Shielded Areas" where drones would have right-of-way over all aircraft, concern that proposed Part 108 would exempt agricultural UAS from Part 137 requirements, and alarm that UAS up to 1,320 lb would operate without airman certificates. NAAA has stated that permitting BVLOS without certified DAA and without maintaining manned aircraft right-of-way "virtually guarantees an eventual fatal mid-air collision between UAS and crewed agricultural aircraft."[^61][^62]

NAAA also operates a UAV Safety Campaign (since 2015) and formed a dedicated Uncrewed Aerial Application System (UAAS) Committee in May 2024.[^63][^64]

## Common operator misconceptions

The following misconceptions are drawn from FAA compliance findings, NAAA publications, and Advisory Circular guidance. Each entry states the misconception, the correct rule, and the source.

**1. "Part 107 is the only certificate I need to spray with a drone."**
Incorrect. Any drone dispensing pesticides, fertilizers, or other agricultural substances triggers Part 137 (Agricultural Aircraft Operations), requiring an AAOC in addition to the Part 107 Remote Pilot Certificate. An exemption from Section 107.36 (hazmat carriage) is also required. Both certifications plus the exemption must be in place before operations begin.[^2]

**2. "I can skip Part 137 if my drone weighs under 55 pounds."**
Incorrect. Part 137 applies to any aircraft dispensing agricultural substances, regardless of weight. The 55-lb threshold determines whether operations fall under Part 107 (under 55 lb) or Part 91 with Section 44807 (55 lb and over), but Part 137 applies to both weight classes when dispensing is involved.[^2][^16]

**3. "I can start spraying while my exemption petition is being processed."**
Incorrect. The exemption must be granted before any operations begin. Petitions must be filed at least 120 days before the desired start date. After receiving the exemption, the operator must still apply for and receive the Part 137 AAOC before spraying.[^2]

**4. "Remote ID is optional or not yet enforced."**
Incorrect. Remote ID has been fully enforceable since March 16, 2024. The FAA ended its discretionary enforcement policy on that date. Operators who do not comply face fines and suspension or revocation of their remote pilot certificates. The FAA has specifically flagged agricultural exemption holders for non-compliance with Remote ID.[^52][^2]

**5. "I do not need to register my agricultural drone because it is used for farming."**
Incorrect. All drones operated under Part 107 must be registered regardless of use. Drones under 55 lb register via FAADroneZone ($5). Drones 55 lb or heavier register for an N-number through the Civil Aviation Registry. The FAA has flagged operators failing to meet "basic requirements such as obtaining a Remote Pilot Certificate and registering their UAS."[^6][^2]

**6. "The recurrent knowledge test must be retaken in person every two years."**
Incorrect. Since April 21, 2021, the in-person recurrent test has been replaced by free online recurrent training available on the FAASTeam website. Certificate holders must complete this training every 24 calendar months but no longer need to visit a testing center.[^7][^9]

**7. "BVLOS is available under the new Part 108 rule."**
Incorrect as of April 2026. The Part 108 BVLOS NPRM was published August 7, 2025, but it remains a **proposed rule**. No final rule has been published. BVLOS operations currently require a case-by-case waiver (Section 107.31 for small UAS) or exemption (for large UAS). Routine BVLOS for agriculture is not yet authorized under any existing rule.[^34]

**8. "One pilot can legally operate multiple spray drones simultaneously under Part 107."**
Incorrect under baseline Part 107. Section 107.35 prohibits any person from acting as PIC or visual observer for more than one UAS at a time. Multi-drone operations require a Part 107 waiver (for under 55 lb) or a blanket COA (for over 55 lb under Section 44807). Without this authorization, each airborne drone requires a separate certificated pilot.[^41]

**9. "MOSAIC is the new FAA rule that will allow agricultural drone BVLOS."**
Incorrect. MOSAIC (Modernization of Special Airworthiness Certification) is exclusively a manned Light-Sport Aircraft rule. It has no relevance to UAS operations. The proposed BVLOS rule for drones is the Part 108 NPRM, which is a separate rulemaking.[^39]

**10. "Part 137 requires liability insurance."**
Incorrect at the federal level. Part 137 does **not** contain a federal insurance requirement. The FAA does not mandate proof of insurance or financial responsibility under Part 137. However, commercial clients, landowners, and many state pesticide applicator licensing programs do require proof of liability insurance. State requirements vary, and insurance is a practical necessity for commercial operations even where not federally mandated.[^18]
