# US Northeast Agricultural Drone Operator Directory (Batch 1)

## Summary

**Total entities found: 46** (39 based in Northeast states; 7 national dealers explicitly serving the region)

**Per-state count (NE-based only):**
Connecticut: 1 | Delaware: 0 | DC: 0 | Maine: 2 | Maryland: 11 | Massachusetts: 2 | New Hampshire: 1 | New Jersey: 5 | New York: 7 | Pennsylvania: 6 | Rhode Island: 0 | Vermont: 1 | West Virginia: 3

**Per-category breakdown (primary category):**
Operators (active, NE-based): 24 | Dealers (NE-based): 3 | Training/Research (NE-based): 9 | Manufacturers (NE-based): 2 (1 defunct) | National dealers serving NE: 7

**Key findings:** The Northeast agricultural drone spraying market is nascent compared to the Midwest and South. **DJI Agras dominates** every identified operator's fleet. Most operators launched in 2023 or 2024. New York and Pennsylvania have the highest operator density. Maryland's Eastern Shore is the Mid-Atlantic hotspot. **Three states have zero identified operators** (Delaware, Rhode Island, DC). Connecticut only legalized drone spraying in 2025 via Public Act 25-152. The 170% tariff on Chinese drones (effective late 2024) is a significant barrier to new entrants across New England.

**Data gaps and notes:**
- Delaware has no standalone operators; served entirely by MD/regional operators (Scout Applicators, Chesapeake Drone Ag, Accurate Ag Drones).
- Rhode Island has no identified operators or dealers.
- DC has no operators; training schools only (PGCC, UMGC in MD suburbs).
- Connecticut's sole entry (Almanax) is pre-commercial with a spring 2026 pilot planned.
- FAA does not maintain a public searchable Part 137 UAS database; certificates must be verified through FSDO offices.
- Rantizo's AcreConnect operator map (map.acreconnect.io) was inaccessible; additional NE operators may be listed there.
- NAAA member directory is membership-gated and primarily lists manned aircraft operators.
- No Hylio, ABZ Innovation, or Pyka dealers/operators were found in the NE region.
- XAG presence is limited to AG Drones East (likely VA-based, serving NE).
- Guardian Agriculture (Woburn, MA) ceased operations August 2025 after failing to secure additional funding.
- Several thin entries (A-1 Aerials, K Drone Services) have minimal public information and may be inactive.
- UPASS Foundation (upass.foundation) is a key industry association connecting ag drone pilots; board includes Scout Applicators (MD/DE/VA) and partners with Agri Spray Drones.
- Manned aerial applicators (Triple F Flying, Tallman Aerial Spraying, Summit Helicopters) were excluded from this directory.
- nuWay Ag maintains "Find a Pilot" pages for most NE states but pilot listings appear to be password-protected.

## Regulatory notes by state

**Connecticut:** Public Act 25-152 (signed 2025) authorizes drone use for crop spraying, seeding, and surveillance. Requires both FAA certification and CT Department of Agriculture pesticide applicator license. Rules to be finalized by March 2026. 170% tariffs on Chinese ag drones are a major barrier.

**Maryland:** Maryland Cover Crop Program explicitly approves drone seeding. University of Maryland Extension has been active in drone ag education since 2020.

**New Jersey:** NJ EPA pesticide business license required in addition to FAA certifications. Influential Drones was the first NJ drone company with Part 137 (Jan 2021).

**New York:** NYS DEC pesticide applicator certification required (Categories 1A and/or 11) in addition to FAA Part 107 and Part 137. Multiple operators active in Western NY and Finger Lakes.

**Pennsylvania:** PA Department of Agriculture requires Commercial Applicator Category 25 (Aerial Applicator) certification. PA DCNR maintains a public Aerial Applicators Licensed to Spray list that includes drone operators (Stone Valley Drones and CNY Drone Services confirmed on this list). PA awarded $52K Agriculture Innovation Grant to Swift Aeroseed for drone cover crop deployment.

**West Virginia:** WVU Extension is actively promoting drone spraying adoption through county demonstrations. Terrain challenges make drones especially valuable for steep pasture management.
