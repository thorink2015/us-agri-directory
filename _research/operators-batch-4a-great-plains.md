# Great Plains Agricultural Drone Directory: Batch 4a

**Six states surveyed: Kansas, Nebraska, North Dakota, Oklahoma, South Dakota, Texas.** This directory identifies **126 unique entities** across the agricultural drone spraying ecosystem in the Great Plains, including operators, dealers, training programs, and manufacturers. Texas leads with 34 entries, followed by Nebraska (32), Kansas (24), North Dakota (12), Oklahoma (9), and South Dakota (8). Seven multi-state companies headquartered outside these states but with significant Great Plains operations are listed separately. The industry is growing explosively: U.S. drone-sprayed acreage jumped from 4 million to **10.3 million acres** between 2023 and 2024, and over 1,700 FAA Part 137 UAS certificates have been issued nationwide. North Dakota alone saw a **600% increase** in drone-sprayed acres in one year (11,671 to 69,032 acres). DJI Agras remains the dominant platform (~70% market share), though NDAA-compliant alternatives from Hylio (Richmond, TX), XAG (via Pegasus Robotics), and EAVision are gaining ground amid proposed Chinese drone restrictions.

**Data gaps**: Many small operators lack websites and operate only via Facebook or word-of-mouth. Interactive maps (Rantizo AcreConnect, nuWay Ag pilot finder) contain additional operators not scrapeable. Nebraska PSC grant recipients are included but most lack full contact details. ND has 21 licensed unmanned aerial applicator owner-operators per state records, but only 3 have public-facing business profiles. Expect the true total operator count across these states to exceed 200 when private farmer-operators are included.

## State-by-state regulatory requirements

### North Dakota
**Agency**: ND Aeronautics Commission + NDSU Extension Pesticide Certification. Required: FAA Part 107, Part 137 AAOC, ND Pesticide Certificate, Unmanned Aerial Applicator License ($200), workers' comp, annual safety meeting (PAASS), aircraft registration with NDAC. Max UAS weight 500 lbs. Annual acreage report due December 1. Licensed since Spring 2022. As of 2024: 21 licensed owner-operators, 46 pilots, 37 aircraft, 69,032 acres. **Contact**: (701) 328-9650; aero.nd.gov.

### South Dakota
**Agency**: SD DANR. Required: FAA Part 107, Part 137, SD Commercial Pesticide Applicator License with General + Aerial (Category 17) exams ($35, valid 2 years, renew by March 1), Class 3 Medical, 44807 exemption for 55+ lb drones, N-number registration, Remote ID (since Sept 2023). Reciprocity available. **Contact**: (605) 773-4432; danr.sd.gov.

### Nebraska
**Agency**: Nebraska Dept. of Agriculture (NDA). NDA classifies ALL drone pesticide application as aerial, regardless of size. Required: FAA Part 107, Part 137, Category 12 (Aerial Pest Control) on applicator license, Nebraska Aerial Pesticide Business License. Heavy drones need 44807 approval. Reciprocity with CO, IN, IA, KS, MN, MS, MO, MT, ND, OK, SD, TX, WA, WY. **Contact**: nda.nebraska.gov/pesticide/aerial_license. **Note**: NE PSC Precision Ag Grant Program funds drone acquisition for farmers.

### Kansas
**Agency**: KDA Pesticide and Fertilizer Program. UAS policy implemented July 2022, expanded January 2023, revised April 7, 2025. Only certified commercial applicators may spray via UAS. Must submit drone details, FAA registration, pilot license, Part 137 cert to KDA before spraying. Pesticide Business License required for commercial ops. Permitted categories: 1A/1B/1C/1D, 2, 3A/3B, 5, 6, 7C/7D, 10. **Contact**: Kelly Navinsky-Wenzl, (785) 564-6692; KDA.PestFert@ks.gov.

### Oklahoma
**Agency**: ODAFF. FAA Part 137 must be filed with the Board before aerial license is issued (OK Statutes 2-3-82). Must pass Core + category exams (70% pass). License types: Commercial, Noncommercial, Private. Annual cycle (Oct 1 or Jan 1 depending on company name). Reciprocity available. Late renewal doubles fees + $100 penalty after 30 days. 5-year recertification per category. **Contact**: (405) 522-5984 ext 3; pesticide@ag.ok.gov.

### Texas
**Agency**: Texas Department of Agriculture (TDA). Commercial Applicator License (PA-401) requires General Standards + at least one category exam. Aerial category must be accompanied by at least one additional category. $64/exam, $200/year license. 70% pass. Testing through Metro Institute. Must record product, EPA reg, application rate, N-number, weather, spray permit for regulated herbicides. Must notify TDA of changes within 30 days. **Contact**: (800) 835-5832; pesticides@TexasAgriculture.gov.

## Key industry observations

The Great Plains spray drone market is in a period of rapid expansion. **10.3 million acres** were sprayed by drone nationally in 2024, more than doubling from 2023. Over **1,700 Part 137 UAS certificates** have been issued. The region's dominant use cases vary by geography: Texas operators focus heavily on **brush control** (mesquite, prickly pear, huisache) alongside cotton and row crop work; the Dakotas and Nebraska focus on **row crop fungicide, cover crop seeding, and noxious weed control**; Kansas and Oklahoma bridge both with significant wheat, pasture, and rangeland applications.

DJI Agras (T50, T100) commands roughly 70% of the U.S. market, but the proposed Countering CCP Drones Act has accelerated demand for NDAA-compliant alternatives. **Hylio** (Richmond, TX) is the leading domestic manufacturer, with plans to scale from 1,000 to 5,000 drones per year. **XAG** (distributed by Pegasus Robotics, Lewistown, MO) and **EAVision** (distributed by Agri Spray Drones, Boonville, MO) are the main Chinese competitors to DJI. The **GTEEX Revolution** (via Terraplex Ag) and **Guardian Agriculture SC1** are emerging NDAA-compliant options.

Kansas made a notable $3M state investment in **Kelly Hills Unmanned Systems** (Seneca, KS) for BVLOS drone development, establishing the largest contiguous FAA-approved UAS test range (49,000 sq mi). Nebraska's PSC Precision Ag Grant Program has funded dozens of farmers acquiring spray drones. North Dakota's Vantis BVLOS radar network may enable the most advanced autonomous agricultural drone operations in the country.

The dealer landscape is consolidating around a few major networks: **Agri Spray Drones** (Boonville, MO), **nuWay Ag** (Dundee, OH), **Volitant Technologies** (Dunbar, NE), **Bestway Ag** (Hiawatha, KS location), **Osprey Agri Drones** (Waxahachie, TX), and **TJ Aerosense** (Huron, SD) serve as key regional hubs. The **Rantizo/AcreConnect** platform operates the largest operator network but recently sold its spraying operations business to focus on software under the American Autonomy brand.

Executive Order 14307 ("Unleashing American Drone Dominance," June 2025) and the FAA's BVLOS NPRM signal regulatory tailwinds that should further accelerate adoption across these states.
