## 1. FAA Part 107 Remote Pilot Certificate

### 1.1 Knowledge test cost (2026)

The **"Unmanned Aircraft General – Small" (UAG)** initial knowledge test costs **$175 per attempt**, non-refundable, paid to PSI Services at booking. FAA's own FAQ page states testing centers charge "approximately $175." The figure is consistent across FAA.gov, UAV Coach, Pilot Institute, Drone Pilot Ground School, and Drone U's 2026 pricing page. The fee has been stable since roughly 2021.

- FAA FAQ ("How much does it cost to get a remote pilot certificate?"): https://www.faa.gov/faq/how-much-does-it-cost-get-remote-pilot-certificate
- PSI test booking portal: https://faa.psiexams.com/faa/login
- Drone Pilot Ground School: https://www.dronepilotgroundschool.com/kb/how-do-i-schedule-my-faa-part-107-exam-2/
- Drone U (2026): https://www.thedroneu.com/blog/drone-license-cost/

**Flag:** One outlier source (TheLegalDrone) described a possible $96 exam fee + $64 convenience fee ($160 total) at some PSI-owned centers; this is not reflected on FAA.gov or the dominant industry sources.

### 1.2 Test format: passing score, questions, time

Per **FAA-S-ACS-10B, Remote Pilot – Small UAS ACS, April 2021 (current version)**, Appendix 1:
- **60 multiple-choice questions**
- **2 hours (120 minutes)** to complete
- **70% passing score**
- **14-day waiting period** after a failed attempt (14 CFR 107.71)

Source: FAA-S-ACS-10B PDF — https://www.faa.gov/sites/faa.gov/files/training_testing/testing/acs/uas_acs.pdf

### 1.3 Topics covered on the exam

The ACS defines five Areas of Operation with the following weights:

| Area | Topic | Item weight |
|---|---|---|
| I | Regulations | 15–25% |
| II | Airspace & operating requirements | 15–25% |
| III | Weather | 11–16% |
| IV | Loading & performance | 7–11% |
| V | Operations | 35–45% |

Tasks under each area (per FAA-S-ACS-10B) include: **Regulations** (14 CFR Parts 89 and 107 applicability, operating rules, waivers, operations over people Categories 1–4, Remote Identification — the last two added in the 2021 revision); **Airspace** (Class B/C/D/E/G, special-use airspace, TFRs, NOTAMs, weather minimums, sUAS lighting); **Weather** (METAR/TAF, ASOS/AWOS, density altitude, stability, thunderstorms, fog, icing, ceiling/visibility); **Loading/Performance** (CG, balance, performance data); **Operations** (radio comms, airport ops, emergency procedures including lithium battery hazards, aeronautical decision-making, physiology, maintenance and preflight inspection).

Source: FAA-S-ACS-10B — https://www.faa.gov/sites/faa.gov/files/training_testing/testing/acs/uas_acs.pdf

### 1.4 Recurrent training requirements (current rule)

Effective **April 6, 2021** (Amdt. 107-8, 86 FR 4382, Jan. 15, 2021), the old **UGR recurrent knowledge test was eliminated** and replaced with a **free online training course** hosted on **FAASafety.gov**. Under **14 CFR 107.65**, a person cannot exercise Remote PIC privileges unless, within the previous **24 calendar months**, they have either: (a) passed the initial knowledge test covering § 107.73, or (b) completed recurrent training covering § 107.73, or (c) if holding a current Part 61 certificate with flight review, completed training covering § 107.74.

The courses are:
- **ALC-677** — "Part 107 Small UAS Recurrent" (for Part 107-only pilots). Free; ~2 hours of material followed by a 45-question exam with a 90-minute limit; 100% completion required (material can be reviewed). Course link: https://www.faasafety.gov/gslac/ALC/CourseLanding.aspx?cID=677
- **ALC-515** — "Part 107 Small UAS Recurrent – Part 61 Pilots." https://www.faasafety.gov/gslac/ALC/course_content.aspx?cID=515
- **ALC-451** — "Part 107 Small UAS Initial" (alternative initial path for Part 61 pilots).

FAA confirmation: "Certificate holders must complete an online recurrent training every 24 calendar months to maintain aeronautical knowledge recency." — https://www.faa.gov/uas/commercial_operators/become_a_drone_pilot

If recency lapses, the pilot simply cannot exercise RPIC privileges until the training is re-completed; the certificate itself does not expire and re-testing is not required.

- 14 CFR 107.65: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-F/part-107/subpart-C/section-107.65
- FAA newsroom announcement: https://www.faa.gov/newsroom/recurrent-training-courses-drone-pilots-available-online

### 1.5 Minimum age, language, physical condition (14 CFR 107.61)

To hold a Remote Pilot Certificate with sUAS rating, a person must:
- **(a) Be at least 16 years of age** to receive the certificate (minimum age to **take** the test is 14 per FAA-S-ACS-10B Appendix 1).
- **(b) Be able to read, speak, write, and understand English.** The FAA may place operating limitations if an applicant cannot meet this for a medical reason such as hearing impairment.
- **(c) Not know or have reason to know that he or she has a physical or mental condition** that would interfere with safe sUAS operation. **No formal medical certificate is required**; this is self-assessed. 14 CFR 107.17 separately prohibits operation with a known medical deficiency.
- **(d)** Demonstrate aeronautical knowledge by passing the initial test or, for qualified Part 61 holders, completing ALC-451.

Source: 14 CFR 107.61 — https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-F/part-107/subpart-C/section-107.61

### 1.6 IACRA application process

1. **Create IACRA account**, register as "Applicant," and obtain an **FAA Tracking Number (FTN)**, format C1234567. IACRA: https://iacra.faa.gov
2. **Schedule and take the UAG exam** at a PSI testing center (roughly 800 nationwide); $175 paid to PSI; government-issued photo ID required.
3. **Wait ~48–72 hours** for test score to post to FAA systems.
4. **Submit FAA Form 8710-13 via IACRA** using the 17-digit Knowledge Test Exam ID; sign electronically.
5. **TSA security vetting** runs automatically. FAA's preamble estimate was "about 10 business days" to issue the temporary certificate; industry sources typically report **7–14 business days** in current practice, though individual applicants have reported anywhere from a few days to four weeks.
6. **Temporary certificate** issued through IACRA/email — valid immediately for commercial operations.
7. **Permanent plastic certificate** arrives by mail in **6–8 weeks** (FAA estimate).

Part 61 pilots with a current flight review can skip the proctored exam entirely by completing ALC-451 and applying through IACRA with the completion certificate.

- FAA process page: https://www.faa.gov/uas/commercial_operators/become_a_drone_pilot
- Temporary certificate rule: 14 CFR 107.64; summary at https://jrupprechtlaw.com/section-107-64-temporary-certificate/

### 1.7 Average study time reported by test-takers

The FAA's own informal guidance, widely cited by test-prep providers, is **~20 hours** of study. Reported ranges:

| Source | Reported study time |
|---|---|
| FAA (cited by Pilot Institute, DroneLife) | ~20 hours |
| Pilot Institute | 15–20 hours; "14-day challenge" totals ~14 hours |
| Drone Pilot Ground School | 15–20 hours over 2–3 weeks |
| Ohio State Extension (2025) | 15–25 hours |
| nuWay Ag (agricultural-specific) | 8–15 hours |

Sources: https://pilotinstitute.com/part-107-time/ ; https://www.dronepilotgroundschool.com/faq/ ; https://ohioline.osu.edu/factsheet/cdfs-4121 ; https://nuwayag.com/blogs/news/agricultural-drone-licensing-guide

**Flag:** The Drone Girl reported in 2025 that the **average Part 107 test score fell to 79.31%** and the **pass rate fell to 82.96%** (vs. an overall FAA airman pass rate of 89.56%), with drone pilots being the lowest-performing FAA airman group. The underlying FAA statistical release could not be independently located on FAA.gov during this research and should be treated as unverified.

### 1.8 What changed in the last 12 months for Part 107

No change to the **$175 test fee**. The **ACS remains FAA-S-ACS-10B (April 2021)**; a newer revision has not been released despite bookseller listings for a "2025/2026 edition" that appear to be reprints. Recurrent training is unchanged. The **notable 2025 operational change** affecting agricultural Part 107 holders is a new FAA enforcement posture — Requests for Information and "operational validations" directed at Part 137 drone exemption holders (see Section 2).

## 2. FAA Part 137 Agricultural Aircraft Operator Certificate

### 2.1 What Part 137 is, and why a drone ag spray operator needs it in addition to Part 107

**14 CFR Part 137** governs agricultural aircraft operations — the **dispensing of economic poisons** (pesticides, herbicides, fungicides, plant regulators, defoliants, desiccants), plant-nourishment substances, seeds, and substances for soil treatment or forestry preservation. Per **§ 137.11(a)**, no person may conduct agricultural aircraft operations without, or in violation of, an Agricultural Aircraft Operator Certificate (AAOC).

Part 107 authorizes commercial *flight* of a small UAS; Part 137 authorizes *dispensing*. The two are independent: a Part 107 certificate does not permit chemical dispensing, and Part 137 does not by itself authorize UAS flight. A drone-based ag spray operator therefore needs:

1. **Part 107 Remote Pilot Certificate** for the pilot.
2. **A § 44807 petition for exemption** (from 14 CFR 61, 91, 107, and several 137 sub-sections that presume crewed aircraft), plus — because **14 CFR 107.36 non-waivably prohibits hazmat carriage** — an exemption covering that rule as well.
3. **Part 137 AAOC** for the operator (the business).
4. **State pesticide applicator license** (see Section 4).

Sources: 14 CFR Part 137 — https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-G/part-137 ; FAA "Dispensing Chemicals and Agricultural Products (Part 137) with UAS" (last updated May 23, 2025) — https://www.faa.gov/uas/advanced_operations/dispensing_chemicals ; AC 137-1B — https://www.faa.gov/documentLibrary/media/Advisory_Circular/AC_137-1B.pdf

### 2.2 Application process and timeline

**Streamlined UAS path (effective June 13, 2023 under Notice 8900.659, reissued as 8900.704 in 2024 and 8900.741 on June 9, 2025).** UAS applicants **no longer submit through their local FSDO**. Instead, the applicant emails **FAA Form 8710-3** and the § 44807 exemption number to **UAS137Certificates@faa.gov** at the centralized **137 UAS Operations Office** under **AFS-700**.

- FAA Notice 8900.659 (PDF): https://www.faa.gov/documentLibrary/media/Notice/N_8900.659.pdf
- FAA Notice 8900.741 (active): https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/orders_notices/index.cfm/go/document.information/documentID/1043917

The process still runs through five phases (Preapplication, Formal Application, Document Compliance, Demonstration and Inspection, Certification). **FAA Notice 8900.766** (effective Jan. 24, 2024) introduced a mandatory **Applicant Readiness Checklist** intended to prevent applicants from entering Phase 3 with incomplete documents.

**Observed timelines (2024–2025):**
- AckerSpray: AAOC issued within **2–10 weeks** if complete and accurate — https://www.ackerspray.com/blog/how-to-obtain-an-faa-part-137-certification-for-operations-with-drone-uas-over-55-lbs
- FarmTechX: **2–3 months** total, a significant improvement over the pre-streamlining 12+ month timeline — https://farmtechx.com/blog/part-137-certification-timeline-guide
- Hylio: "three months or less" typical — https://www.hyl.io/faa-information
- Maverick Drone: AAOC "90+ days"; Section 44807 exemption 60–90 days — https://www.maverickdrone.com/products/part-107-part-137-combo
- Capital Press (2023, pre-streamlining): historically "a year or longer" — https://capitalpress.com/2023/06/20/faa-streamlines-process-for-spray-drone-pilot-approval/

The FAA has not published an official service-level standard. Reports vary because different sources measure different segments (AAOC alone vs. end-to-end including § 44807 and N-number registration).

### 2.3 Cost

**The FAA does not charge a filing fee for the AAOC.** No fee is listed on Form 8710-3 or on the FAA program page. This is inferred from the absence of any codified fee; it is not explicitly stated by the FAA as "$0."

Adjacent costs (2025 industry figures):
- Part 107 knowledge test: ~$175 (Pilot Institute)
- sUAS (<55 lb) registration: $5 per drone, 3-year term
- Class 3 FAA medical (if required by an exemption condition): $70–$170 (nuWay Ag)

**Consultant/attorney fees (publicly available figures):**
- **Maverick Drone** consulting: approximately **$600** for Part 137 add-on guidance plus $600 per additional exemption (BVLOS, multi-drone).
- **Antonelli Law** flat fees: **$500–$1,500 for many Part 107 waivers**; BVLOS waivers $10,000+; legacy Section 333 summary-grant service $2,500. — https://www.antonelli-law.com/droneuas-practice-group/
- **Rupprecht Law** advertises a "Drone Spraying Exemption & Part 137 Certification Suite" (prices not publicly disclosed on the website). — https://jrupprechtlaw.com/product/drone-spraying-exemption-service/

**Flag:** A comprehensive industry survey of 44807 and Part 137 attorney fees could not be located. Quoted ranges outside Antonelli's published figures are anecdotal.

### 2.4 Required documents

Per 14 CFR § 137.19 and **NAAA's Part 137 Certification Guide (Aug 2024)** — https://www.agaviation.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/137-Certification-Guide.pdf:

- **FAA Form 8710-3** (Agricultural Aircraft Operator Certification Application)
- **Proof of pilot availability** — at least one person with a U.S. commercial or ATP certificate (replaced by Remote Pilot Certificate for UAS-only operators under the streamlined process)
- **Proof of aircraft availability** — at least one airworthy aircraft equipped for agricultural operations per § 137.19(d)
- **Recordkeeping system** — records retained ≥12 months per § 137.71
- **Letter of Competency or logbook endorsement** showing satisfactory completion of § 137.19(e) knowledge and skill tests
- **Congested Area Plan (CAP)** per § 137.51(b)(3) — required only if the operator intends to operate over congested areas; must address obstructions, emergency-landing capability, ATC coordination, prior written approval from the local political subdivision, and public notice. Per FAA Notice 8900.433, a separate LOA A503 is no longer required for the CAP.
- **Operations manual** with lost-link procedures, loss-of-sight procedures, moving-vehicle operations, and manned-aircraft avoidance — required by the § 44807 exemption Conditions & Limitations (AC 137-1B § 2.6.11)
- **Applicant information** — full legal name, physical address (no P.O. Box), Chief Supervisor contact, PIC name and certificate number, each aircraft's make/model/FA- or N-number

Sources: eCFR 14 CFR §§ 137.19, 137.51, 137.71; AC 137-1B; FAA dispensing chemicals page.

### 2.5 Knowledge and skill test components (14 CFR 137.19(e))

**Knowledge test (§ 137.19(e)(1))** covers:
(i) Pre-operation steps including survey of the work area
(ii) Safe handling of economic poisons and disposal of containers
(iii) General effects of economic poisons and agricultural chemicals on plants, animals, and persons, with precautions
(iv) Primary symptoms of human poisoning, emergency measures, and poison control locations
(v) Performance capabilities and operating limitations of the aircraft
(vi) Safe flight and application procedures

Note: Applicants whose certificate will **prohibit economic-poison dispensing** can skip items (ii)–(iv).

**Skill test (§ 137.19(e)(2))** — performed at maximum certificated takeoff weight or maximum special-purpose load, whichever is greater:
(i) Short-field and soft-field takeoffs (airplanes and gyroplanes)
(ii) Approaches to the working area
(iii) Flare-outs
(iv) Swath runs
(v) Pullups and turnarounds
(vi) Rapid deceleration / quick stops (helicopters)

For UAS applicants, per Notice 8900.659 Appendix A, the **knowledge and skill tests may be self-administered by the chief supervisor**; documentation (date, PIC name, certificate number, signature) must be retained and produced on request. AC 137-1B § 2.6.11 adds UAS-specific topics: lost-link reaction across flight phases, loss-of-sight procedures, manned-aircraft avoidance, and operating limitations including the § 91.119 altitude rule.

### 2.6 Which FSDO handles applications

- **Crewed applicants:** The local FSDO with jurisdiction over the applicant's home base of operations, per **14 CFR § 137.15** — https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-G/part-137
- **UAS-only applicants (streamlined):** The **137 UAS Operations Office under AFS-700** (central), not the local FSDO. Contact: **UAS137Certificates@faa.gov**. Some UAS certifications still require FSDO involvement (e.g., aerial ignition systems per Notice 8900.656, or when a knowledge/skill test must be observed).

**NAAA objected to the centralization** in a July 2023 letter, arguing local FSDOs have valuable area-specific knowledge — https://www.agaviation.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/07/202307-faa-letter-uas-137-certification.pdf

### 2.7 Common rejection and delay reasons from recent applicants

Drawn from the FAA dispensing chemicals page and industry accounts:

- **Failing to respond to a Request for Information.** The FAA will close the petition.
- **Using a P.O. Box instead of a physical address** — explicitly rejected.
- **Name mismatch** between Form 8710-3 and the § 44807 exemption holder name.
- **Failing to obtain the AAOC before operating**; amendment/extension requests will not be processed until a valid AAOC number is supplied.
- **Missing documentation or noncompliant operations at inspection.** A 2024 enforcement case documented by Rupprecht Law involved an operator who could not produce N-number registration, used an untrained visual observer contrary to Condition & Limitation No. 9, flew within 100 ft of nonparticipants, and failed to update the 137 operating certificate address — https://jrupprechtlaw.com/part-137-drone-operator-enforcement-action/
- **Exemption non-transferability errors** — exemptions are issued to a specific operator and cannot be transferred.
- **Incomplete § 44807 petition** after a May 2024 FAA change that required the full applicant name, Chief Supervisor, PIC certificate number, and aircraft make/model/N-number be supplied upfront.

### 2.8 What changed in 2025–2026

- **FAA Notice 8900.741** (June 9, 2025) — continues the streamlined UAS-only AAOC process under AFS-700.
- The FAA dispensing chemicals page (updated **May 23, 2025**) introduced language announcing **operational validations and RFIs** in response to observed noncompliance by some exemption holders.
- **DOT OIG report of June 30, 2025** confirmed that full aircraft type certification has been issued to only **3 drone models** to date. — https://www.oig.dot.gov/sites/default/files/library-items/FAA%20BVLOS%20Drone%20Operations%20Final%20Report_6.30.2025.pdf
- **Proposed Part 108 (BVLOS NPRM, Aug 7, 2025)** proposes amending § 137.1 to say "Except for aircraft subject to part 108 of this chapter," meaning qualifying smaller drone ag operations could be conducted under a future Part 108 permit rather than Part 137 — not yet law as of April 24, 2026.
- **Historical scale:** As of March 22, 2023, there were **122 certificated Part 137 UAS operators** (Notice 8900.659 Appendix A). A more recent total could not be verified for this file.

## 3. Section 44807 Exemption

### 3.1 What it is and when required

**49 U.S.C. § 44807** ("Special Authority for Certain Unmanned Aircraft Systems") was added by § 347(a) of the **FAA Reauthorization Act of 2018 (Pub. L. 115-254)**, replacing the former Section 333 process. It authorizes the FAA Administrator to use a risk-based approach to determine how certain UAS may operate safely in the NAS on a case-by-case basis, without full airworthiness certification.

The authority was set to sunset Sept. 30, 2023 but was extended to **September 30, 2033** by **§ 927 of the FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 (Pub. L. 118-63)**. Section 927(c) also continues existing exemptions for up to 3 years beyond their stated termination date.

- 49 U.S.C. § 44807 text: https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/49/44807
- FAA program page: https://www.faa.gov/uas/advanced_operations/certification/section_44807 (last updated Aug. 5, 2025)

**A § 44807 exemption is required when the operation involves:**
- A UAS with maximum takeoff weight (including payload) **≥ 55 lb** (outside Part 107 scope)
- Relief from a non-waivable Part 107 provision (e.g., § 107.36 hazardous-materials carriage for chemical dispensing, Part 137 provisions designed for crewed aircraft)
- Operations Part 107 does not cover (swarming/multi-UAS without VO, BVLOS beyond Part 107 waiver, certain night operations, etc.)

**Typical Part 14 CFR sections waived in ag grants** (e.g., Agri Spray Drone Docket FAA-2024-1754; Hylio Exemption 22003, Docket FAA-2023-1833; Haley Flying Service, FAA-2024-2646; Guardian Agriculture 19866, FAA-2022-0077): 61.3(a)(1)(i), 61.3(c)(1), 61.23(a)(2), 91.7(a), 91.119(c), 91.121, 91.151(b), 91.209(a)(1), 91.403(b), 91.405(a), 91.407(a)(1), 91.409(a)(1)–(2), 91.417(a)–(b), 137.19(c), 137.19(d), 137.19(e)(2)(ii), (iii), (v), 137.31(a)–(b), 137.33(a)–(b), 137.41(c), 137.42.

**"List of Approved Agricultural UAS under Section 44807"** (Docket FAA-2023-1271). Since 2023, ag grants reference this list so an operator can add new approved airframes without filing a fresh petition. Latest list update **Dec. 3, 2025**. Selected entries: DJI Agras T50 (227.07 lb), Agras T70P (286.60 lb), Agras T100 (390.21 lb), Hylio AG-172/AG-272 (400 lb), Hylio H-600 (432 lb), Guardian Agriculture SC1 (645 lb), DMR Field Ranger X-50 (330.69 lb), Event 38 E500 (630 lb), BROUAV D-72L-8 (324.08 lb). — https://downloads.regulations.gov/FAA-2023-1271-0023/attachment_1.pdf

### 3.2 Application process

Petitions are filed on the **public docket at regulations.gov** under **14 CFR Part 11** (§§ 11.63, 11.71, 11.81). COA applications for airspace authorizations are filed separately through the FAA CAPS system at https://caps.faa.gov.

**Timing rule:** 14 CFR § 11.63(d) requires petitions be submitted at least **120 days before** the needed effective date (or 120 days before an existing exemption expires).

**Required contents** (per FAA 44807 page) generally include a Concept of Operations, Operations Manual, Emergency Procedures, Checklists, Maintenance Manual, Training Program, Flight History (hours, cycles, accidents), and Safety Risk Analysis (always required for complex operations such as BVLOS, multi-UAS, night, over-people, high-speed, package delivery, and Part 135).

After an exemption is granted, the operator must (1) register the aircraft under Part 47 and obtain an N-number via FAA CARES, (2) obtain a Certificate of Waiver or Authorization (COA) through CAPS — the FAA now issues a blanket COA at ≤ 400 ft AGL in Class G airspace to 44807 holders — and (3) obtain the Part 137 AAOC if dispensing.

### 3.3 Typical approval timeline

The FAA does not publish statutory timelines for 44807 decisions beyond the 120-day filing lead time.

Industry-reported observations:
- AckerSpray: "up to 120 days; we've seen 30–90 days on average" — https://www.ackerspray.com/blog/how-to-petition-for-a-44807-exemption-for-drones-uas-over-55-lbs
- Hylio: "about two months from filing to approval" — https://www.hyl.io/faa-information
- Novel operations (BVLOS, swarming) take substantially longer.

**Flag:** The DOT OIG's **June 30, 2025 report** (AV2025034) noted that FAA's UAS Integration Office "cannot conduct comprehensive analysis of FAA drone data" because it was not systematically receiving 44807 exemption data, meaning official processing-time statistics are not tracked centrally. — https://www.oig.dot.gov/sites/default/files/library-items/FAA%20BVLOS%20Drone%20Operations%20Final%20Report_6.30.2025.pdf

Recent exemplar grants (on regulations.gov): Hylio (Exemption 22003, Docket FAA-2023-1833); Guardian Agriculture (19866, FAA-2022-0077); Agri Spray Drone (FAA-2024-1754); American Drone (FAA-2022-0319); Haley Flying Service (FAA-2024-2646).

**FAA filing fee for a § 44807 petition: none.** 14 CFR Part 11 does not impose a petition fee. Hylio states that for customers purchasing one of its drones, it assists with the 44807 submission "at no additional cost."

### 3.5 Recent changes and pending rulemaking (2024–2026)

**FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 (Pub. L. 118-63, signed May 16, 2024):**
- **§ 927** — Extended § 44807 to Sept. 30, 2033; transferred administrative authority from the Secretary of Transportation to the FAA Administrator; **added new § 44807(e) allowing the Administrator to waive parts of 14 CFR "without initiating a rulemaking or imposing the requirements of 14 CFR part 11, to the extent consistent with aviation safety."** This is a new waiver pathway distinct from the traditional petition process. FAA implementation notice published April 1, 2026 (91 FR 16276) — https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/04/01/2026-06297/implementing-section-927-waiver-process-for-certain-unmanned-aircraft-operations
- **§ 930** — Directed the FAA to issue a BVLOS NPRM within 4 months and a final rule within 16 months of NPRM publication; added new 49 U.S.C. § 44811.
- **§ 920** — Extended/expanded the BEYOND program.
- **§ 931** — Risk-assessment methodology (180 days).
- **§ 932** — UTM third-party service supplier approval.

**Executive Order 14307, "Unleashing American Drone Dominance" (June 6, 2025):** Directed FAA to fast-track the BVLOS NPRM within 30 days and finalize within **240 days**; prioritized U.S.-manufactured drones for federal procurement; called for AI tools for Part 107 waiver review. — https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/06/11/2025-10814/unleashing-american-drone-dominance

**Part 108 BVLOS NPRM:** Published in the Federal Register on **August 7, 2025** (90 FR 38212; Docket FAA-2025-1908; RIN 2120-AL82). Covers 14 CFR Parts 36, 43, 45, 48, 89, 91, 107, 108, 119, 133, 135, 137, and 146. Framework for BVLOS at ≤ 400 ft AGL, aircraft up to **1,320 lb MTOW, wingspan < 25 ft, speeds ≤ 87 knots**, with two approval tiers (Permitted Operations and Operational Certificate) and five population-density risk categories. Comment period **closed October 6, 2025** with **3,000+ comments**; FAA denied extension requests. — https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/08/07/2025-14992/normalizing-unmanned-aircraft-systems-beyond-visual-line-of-sight-operations

**Final rule status:** The EO 14307 240-day deadline expired on or about **February 1, 2026**. **As of April 24, 2026, no final Part 108 rule has been published** in the Federal Register, per House T&I oversight hearing records and DLA Piper/Pillsbury analyses. Industry analysts expect Q1–Q2 2026 publication with implementation 6–12 months later. **Flag: uncertain timing.**

## 4. State pesticide applicator license

### 4.1 Why state licensing is separate from FAA rules

**Federal Insecticide, Fungicide, and Rodenticide Act (FIFRA), 7 U.S.C. §§ 136 et seq.** (originally 1947, substantially amended 1972). Under **7 U.S.C. § 136i**, state applicator certification programs are administered pursuant to state plans approved by the EPA Administrator. **40 CFR Part 171** (revised Jan. 4, 2017, eff. March 6, 2017) sets federal minimum standards. All 50 states, DC, 4 tribes, 6 territories, and 5 federal agencies operate EPA-approved programs.

- 7 U.S.C. § 136i: https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title7-section136i
- EPA Certification Standards: https://www.epa.gov/pesticide-worker-safety/certification-standards-pesticide-applicators
- 40 CFR Part 171: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-40/chapter-I/subchapter-E/part-171
- 2017 Final Rule (82 FR 952): https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2017/01/04/2016-30332/pesticides-certification-of-pesticide-applicators

**The FAA regulates the aircraft and pilot (14 CFR Parts 107, 137; 49 U.S.C. § 44807). FIFRA + state law regulate the pesticide applicator.** A drone ag operator needs both. The 2017 EPA rule added at **40 CFR § 171.105** a dedicated **aerial specialization** that all states must offer for commercial applicators using restricted-use pesticides (RUPs).

### 4.2 General structure

- **Private applicator** — applies RUPs on land owned or rented by the applicator or the applicator's employer, for production of agricultural commodities.
- **Commercial applicator** — applies pesticides on another's property, for compensation.
- **Noncommercial applicator** — some states use this for employees applying RUPs as part of job duties rather than for hire.
- **Core ("general standards") exam** — labels, safety, environment, laws, calibration, IPM, drift (40 CFR § 171.103).
- **Category-specific exams** — required for each method/site. **Aerial** is a mandatory federal category under § 171.105.
- **Recertification cycle** — federal maximum is 5 years (40 CFR § 171.107); many states use 3-year cycles.

### 4.5 Reciprocity between states

FIFRA permits but does not mandate reciprocity. Each state sets its own conditions; typical prerequisites are proof of residency in a different state, an active home-state license, a state-specific laws exam in some cases, and a fee (roughly $25–$200).

Verified examples:
- **Kansas:** Reciprocity for commercial applicators (not noncommercial) with KDA-agreement states.
- **Nebraska:** Reciprocates private with MO, KS, CO, IA, WY, SD; commercial/noncommercial with WY, WA, ND, TX, SD, MT, MO, KS, MN, MS. — https://nda.nebraska.gov/pesticide/reciprocal
- **Wisconsin:** Formal reciprocity under Wis. Stat. § 94.704 and ATCP 29.25/29.26. — https://datcp.wi.gov/Documents2/ComPestAppCertRecip.pdf
- **Washington:** Publishes a Reciprocal Status Chart; requires letter of good standing. — https://agr.wa.gov/services/licenses-permits-and-certificates/pesticide-license-and-recertification/pesticide-and-spi-licensing/reciprocal-licenses
- **North Dakota:** Accepts non-resident aerial applicators from neighboring states but requires NDSU chemical-knowledge certification regardless.
- **Iowa:** Requires the written Iowa Reciprocal Category 11 test for reciprocal aerial applicators.
- **Georgia:** Reciprocates commercial licenses with states that have an EPA program and matching categories; a specific aerial reciprocal agreement exists with Mississippi.
- **Virginia:** $25 reciprocity application fee; home-state license must remain active.

### 4.6 2024–2026 state regulatory updates

- **Kansas (April 7, 2025):** KDA expanded permitted UAS categories to include Categories 1, 2, 3(3A/3B), 5, 6, 7(7C), 8, 9, and 10, and created a new UAS policy for government agencies.
- **Washington:** SB 5330 (2023) mandated a new standalone Aerial Pest Control exam covering drones; the 2025 WAC rulemaking finalization date (Oct. 31, 2025) and Dec. 31, 2030 compliance deadline come from a third-party pest-control company blog and should be confirmed with WSDA.
- **Nebraska:** Updated fact sheet "2025 Drones and the Nebraska Pesticide Act," posted Dec. 5, 2024.
- **Iowa (IDALS):** The unproctored private applicator exam option sunsets Dec. 31, 2025, replaced by an online proctored option. A new Iowa Cat 11 Aerial manual is required as of June 2025.
- **Texas (TDA):** Effective May 19, 2025, Metro Institute replaced PSI for administering TDA pesticide applicator exams.
- **Federal:** EO 14307 (June 6, 2025) did not change FIFRA applicator certification requirements directly but is referenced by some state extension sources.

## 5. Registration and marking

### 5.1 Part 47 vs. Part 48 for agricultural drones

**14 CFR Part 48** governs small UAS (<55 lb) registration and identification. **14 CFR Part 47** is traditional aircraft registration and is mandatory for drones **55 lb or greater**, and also for sUAS intended to operate outside U.S. territorial airspace or held in trust/voting trust arrangements (14 CFR § 48.1(c)). Any sUAS owner may voluntarily elect Part 47.

FAA AIM Ch. 11 § 2: "UAS weighing 55 pounds MGOW or more must be registered under 14 CFR part 47... while UAS less than 55 pounds may be registered under the FAA's newer 14 CFR part 48 online system." — https://www.faa.gov/air_traffic/publications/atpubs/aim_html/chap11_section_2.html

Agricultural drones exceeding 55 lb MGOW with payload — **DJI Agras T50 (227.07 lb), Agras T70P, Agras T100, Hylio AG-172/AG-272, Hylio H-600, Guardian SC1** — must be registered under **Part 47, not DroneZone**.

- 14 CFR Part 48: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-C/part-48
- Rupprecht Law heavy-lift guide: https://jrupprechtlaw.com/ultimate-guide-to-large-drones-heavy-lift-drones-55lbs/

### 5.2 Cost

**Part 48:** $5.00 per certificate (14 CFR § 48.30). For non-recreational/Part 107 use, each aircraft requires its own $5 certificate. For recreational use, a single $5 certificate covers all drones owned.

**Part 47 fees** (14 CFR § 47.17, amended by Amdt. 47-33, 87 FR 71217, Nov. 22, 2022):
- Certificate of Aircraft Registration (each aircraft): **$5.00**
- Special/reserved registration number: $10.00
- Change/reassign/reserve registration number: $10.00
- Replacement Certificate: $2.00
- Renewal: $5.00
- Fees waived for federal, state, or local government applicants.

Source: https://law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/14/47.17

### 5.3 Duration / renewal

- **Part 48:** **3 years** (§§ 48.100(c), 48.105(c)); renewal window is the 6 months preceding expiration.
- **Part 47:** **7 years** as of Jan. 23, 2023 (§ 47.40, amended by Amdt. 47-33, 87 FR 71210). Renewal via AC Form 8050-1B within the 6 months preceding expiration. Certificates in effect on Jan. 23, 2023 were extended retroactively to 7 years from issuance.

Federal Register: https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2022/11/22/2022-25289/increase-the-duration-of-aircraft-registration ; FAA InFO 23002 — https://www.faa.gov/sites/faa.gov/files/InFO23002_0.pdf

### 5.4 N-number and identifiers

- **Part 47 registration results in an N-number** of the type assigned to manned aircraft (AIM 11-2-2). Application is via **AC Form 8050-1** plus evidence of ownership (AC Form 8050-2 Bill of Sale or equivalent; notarized ownership affidavit for corporate/LLC owners), filed by mail to the **FAA Aircraft Registration Branch, Oklahoma City, OK**. Typical processing is several weeks to 2–3 months; Rupprecht Law reported a 20-calendar-day issuance on one recent application (June 23, 2025).
- **Part 48 registration issues an FAA alphanumeric registration number (often prefixed "FA"), NOT an N-number.** FAA Notice N 8900.338: "The registration number will be composed of multiple alphanumeric characters and will not be a traditional N-number." — https://www.faa.gov/documentlibrary/media/notice/n_8900.338.pdf

### 5.5 Display and marking requirements

**Part 48 marking — 14 CFR § 48.205** (amended by 84 FR 3669, Feb. 13, 2019, effective Feb. 25, 2019):
- Unique identifier must be **legibly maintained**, **affixed so it stays in place during each operation**, and **legibly displayed on an external surface of the small unmanned aircraft**.
- The 2019 amendment closed the loophole that had allowed placement inside a battery compartment accessible without tools.
- No minimum character size is specified; application by engraving, permanent label, or permanent marker is acceptable.

Federal Register: https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2019/02/13/2019-00765/external-marking-requirement-for-small-unmanned-aircraft

**Part 47 marking — 14 CFR Part 45 Subparts A and C** (per § 48.1(b)(2)). **§ 45.29** character size requirements:
- Fixed-wing: at least **12 inches** high (grandfathered/experimental/glider/LSA/powered-parachute/weight-shift exceptions can permit 3 inches — not applicable to ag multirotors).
- Rotorcraft: at least **12 inches** high.
- **§ 45.29(f):** If no authorized surface is large enough for full-size marks, "marks as large as practicable shall be placed on the largest of the authorized surfaces."

**Uncertainty flag:** The FAA has not issued a drone-specific amendment to § 45.29 providing a numeric minimum for Part 47–registered drones. The "3-inch or 12-inch" framing has no drone-specific codified answer; in practice, § 45.29(f)'s "as large as practicable" rule governs, and operators should coordinate with the FAA Aircraft Registration Branch. No change to § 45.29 for drones was identified in 2025–2026.

eCFR: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-C/part-45/subpart-C/section-45.29

### 5.6 Remote ID (14 CFR Part 89)

**Rule:** Remote Identification of Unmanned Aircraft, final rule published Jan. 15, 2021 (RIN 2120-AL31), codified at 14 CFR Part 89.

**Applicability (§ 89.101):** Any UAS required to be registered under Part 47 or Part 48 — including agricultural drones registered under Part 47.

**Three compliance paths (§ 89.105):**
1. Operate a Standard Remote ID drone (built-in broadcast of RID message elements).
2. Operate with a Remote ID Broadcast Module (visual line of sight only, § 89.115).
3. Operate within an FAA-Recognized Identification Area (FRIA).

**Enforcement:** Original operator deadline Sept. 16, 2023; discretionary enforcement extended to **March 16, 2024** (88 FR 63183). Since **March 16, 2024, Remote ID is fully enforced nationwide**; noncompliant operators face civil penalties and possible certificate suspension or revocation. No further extensions.

- FAA announcement: https://www.faa.gov/newsroom/faa-ends-discretionary-enforcement-policy-drone-remote-identification
- 14 CFR Part 89: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-F/part-89
- Manufacturer Declarations of Compliance: https://uasdoc.faa.gov/listDocs

**Ag drone RID status sampled (Rupprecht Law, as of June 23, 2025):**
- Standard RID Declarations exist for **DJI Agras T20P, T25, T30, T40, T50**, and **Hylio AG-116/216, AG-130/230, AG-172/272**.
- **No** Standard RID Declaration for **DJI Agras T16, T20, Heavy Lift, Storm**, or **Hylio AG-152/252, H-450, H-600, HYL-150 HYBRID, HYL-300** — a broadcast module must be used (VLOS only).

Source: https://jrupprechtlaw.com/ultimate-guide-to-large-drones-heavy-lift-drones-55lbs/

### 5.7 What changed in 2025–2026 for registration and marking

No material regulatory changes to **Parts 47, 48, 45, or 89** for agricultural drones in 2025–2026 beyond those already in effect: the 7-year Part 47 duration (since Jan. 23, 2023), the external marking rule (since Feb. 25, 2019), and full Remote ID enforcement (since March 16, 2024). The proposed **Part 108** would affect parts of these regimes but had not been finalized as of April 24, 2026.
