## Hub: `/regulations`

**Title:** `Drone Spraying Regulations: FAA, State & Federal Requirements | Ag Drone Directory`
**Meta:** `Complete guide to FAA Part 107, Part 137, state pesticide licensing, and NDAA compliance for agricultural drone operators in 2026.`
**H1:** `US Agricultural Drone Spraying Regulations`
**Primary keyword:** drone spraying regulations

### AEO Block
Commercial drone spraying in the United States requires three credentials: FAA Part 107 remote pilot certificate, FAA Part 137 agricultural aircraft operator certificate (with Section 44807 exemption for drones over 55 lbs), and a state commercial pesticide applicator license with aerial category endorsement. NDAA compliance adds a fourth layer for operators working federal or state-funded contracts.

### Body (hub overview, 3 to 4 paragraphs)
Agricultural drone spraying is legal in all 50 states but operates under a layered regulatory framework with no single "drone spray license." Three separate agencies govern the activity. The FAA controls airspace access and aircraft certification through Part 107 (pilot) and Part 137 (agricultural aircraft operations). The EPA governs pesticide product labels, which dictate carrier volume, droplet size, buffer zones, and wind limits. State departments of agriculture issue commercial pesticide applicator licenses with aerial category endorsements that vary significantly from state to state.

Understanding where these layers overlap is critical. A valid Part 107 alone does not authorize commercial spraying. A state pesticide license alone does not authorize flight. Only the complete stack (Part 107 + Part 137 + state license) makes a commercial drone spray operation legal. This guide covers each layer with links to the primary source for every requirement.

### FAQ (3 questions)
Q: Can I spray crops with just a Part 107 license?
A: No. Part 107 authorizes you to fly a drone commercially but does not authorize aerial application of pesticides. You also need Part 137 and a state commercial pesticide applicator license with aerial endorsement.

Q: Is drone crop spraying legal in all 50 states?
A: Yes. Every state has a pathway for licensed commercial drone pesticide application. The specific aerial category, exam requirements, fees, and unique rules vary significantly by state.

Q: Which agency do I contact first?
A: Start with your state department of agriculture for the pesticide applicator license, then FAA for Part 107 (2 to 4 weeks) and Part 137 (90 to 180 days). The state license is often the fastest to obtain and informs your Part 137 application.

## Sub-page 1: `/regulations/faa-part-107`

**Title:** `FAA Part 107 for Drone Spraying | Remote Pilot Certificate Guide`
**Meta:** `How to get FAA Part 107 for agricultural drone spraying. Study time, exam format, cost, renewal, and what Part 107 does and does not authorize.`
**H1:** `FAA Part 107: Remote Pilot Certificate for Ag Drone Operators`

### AEO Block
FAA Part 107 is the baseline remote pilot certificate for all commercial drone operations in the US. The exam is 60 multiple-choice questions, 70 percent passing, proctored at PSI testing centers for $175. Study time is 2 to 4 weeks. Part 107 alone does not authorize pesticide application; Part 137 and a state pesticide license are also required.

### Content sections
**H2: What Part 107 covers** (airspace rules, flight restrictions, weather minimums, crew requirements, waiver process)

**H2: How to get Part 107** (study materials, exam format, 60 questions, 70% passing, $175, PSI testing centers, 2 to 4 weeks typical study, no flight test)

**H2: What Part 107 does NOT authorize** (pesticide application, flights over 55 lbs without 44807, BVLOS without waiver, night ops without waiver pre-2021 rule change)

**H2: Part 107 renewal** (every 24 months, online recurrent knowledge test, free, takes about 1 hour)

**H2: Part 107 waivers for ag drone operators** (night operations, BVLOS, operations over people, altitude above 400 ft AGL)

### FAQ (4 questions)
Q: How hard is the Part 107 exam?
A: Pass rate is approximately 80 to 90 percent with 2 to 4 weeks of study. The hardest sections are airspace classification, sectional chart reading, and weather theory. Free study materials from FAA, plus paid courses from Drone Pilot Ground School and others.

Q: Do I need Part 107 if I only spray my own farm?
A: Yes for drones over 0.55 lbs (250g) used in any commercial activity. Even spraying your own crops for commercial agriculture qualifies. The recreational exemption does not apply to farming operations.

Q: Can I fly at night with Part 107?
A: Yes, since the April 2021 rule change. Anti-collision lighting visible for 3 statute miles is required. No waiver needed for night ops under the updated Part 107.

Q: What happens if my Part 107 lapses?
A: You must retake the full initial knowledge exam (not just the recurrent). Keep your 24-month renewal current to avoid the full exam.

## Sub-page 2: `/regulations/faa-part-137`

**Title:** `FAA Part 137 for Drone Spraying | Agricultural Aircraft Certificate Guide`
**Meta:** `How to get FAA Part 137 and Section 44807 exemption for agricultural drone spraying. Timeline, operations manual, and what it covers.`
**H1:** `FAA Part 137: Agricultural Aircraft Operator Certificate for Drones`

### AEO Block
FAA Part 137 is the agricultural aircraft operator certificate required for any commercial aerial application of pesticides, including by drone. The certification process takes 90 to 180 days from submission to FAA approval. Drones over 55 lbs require an additional Section 44807 exemption as part of the Part 137 application. Most commercial ag drone operators hold both Part 137 and a 44807 exemption.

### Content sections
**H2: What Part 137 is** (agricultural aircraft operator certificate, originally written for manned aircraft, interpreted for sUAS)

**H2: Part 137 commercial vs private** (commercial = spraying for hire; private = farmer spraying only own crops. Different requirements.)

**H2: The Section 44807 exemption** (required for drones over 55 lbs, petition to FAA, operations manual, training records, maintenance protocols. Most T50/AG-272 class drones exceed 55 lbs loaded.)

**H2: How to get Part 137** (application process, operations manual drafting, training record templates, 90 to 180 day approval timeline, consultant packages at $2,500 to $4,500)

**H2: What the operations manual must include** (crew training, maintenance schedule, chemical handling procedures, emergency procedures, recordkeeping, congested area plans)

**H2: Common mistakes that delay approval** (incomplete operations manual, incorrect 44807 petition format, missing training documentation, not having Part 107 first)

### FAQ (4 questions)
Q: How long does Part 137 approval take?
A: 90 to 180 days from complete submission to FAA approval. Incomplete operations manuals are the #1 cause of delays. Consultants typically shorten this by 60 to 120 days.

Q: Do I need Part 137 to spray my own farm?
A: If you are spraying only your own crops with your own drone, you may qualify for the simplified Part 137 private applicator path. If you charge anyone else or spray land you do not own, you need the full commercial Part 137.

Q: What is the Section 44807 exemption?
A: An FAA authorization required for any drone over 55 lbs operating commercially. Since most ag spray drones (T50, AG-272, T100) exceed 55 lbs loaded, the 44807 petition is filed as part of the Part 137 application package.

Q: Should I hire a consultant for Part 137?
A: If you have no previous aviation certification experience, yes. Consultant packages ($2,500 to $4,500) include operations manual drafting, 44807 petition, and coaching through approval. DIY attempts average 30 to 90 days longer due to revision cycles.

## Sub-page 3: `/regulations/state-licensing`

**Title:** `State Pesticide Applicator License for Drone Spraying | 50-State Guide`
**Meta:** `Every state requires a commercial pesticide applicator license for drone spraying. See aerial categories, exams, fees, and unique rules for all 50 states.`
**H1:** `State Pesticide Applicator Licensing for Drone Operators: All 50 States`

### AEO Block
Every US state requires a commercial pesticide applicator license with an aerial category endorsement for drone pesticide application. Requirements vary significantly: California requires a separate Unmanned Pest Control Aircraft Pilot Certificate, Iowa mandates an in-state aerial applicator consultant, North Dakota issues a standalone Unmanned Aerial Applicator License, and Ohio requires even private applicators to hold a commercial license for drone use.

### Content sections
**H2: The three-credential stack** (Part 107 + Part 137 + state license. State license is the most variable.)

**H2: What varies by state** (aerial category name, number of exams, fees, CE requirements, reciprocity, unique drone rules, experience requirements, insurance minimums)

**H2: States with drone-specific credentials** (California CDPR Unmanned Pest Control Aircraft Pilot Certificate, North Dakota Unmanned Aerial Applicator License, Arizona Drone Pilot License, Michigan MDARD-approved UAV training, Louisiana mandatory LSU AgCenter Drone Safety Program, Minnesota MnDOT registration)

**H2: States with unique restrictions** (Ohio: private applicators need commercial license. Iowa: aerial applicator consultant. Oregon: 50 hrs flight experience. Arkansas: no CAT license for drones. Tennessee: $150 aircraft decal per drone. Vermont: 30-day public comment aerial permit.)

**H2: Reciprocity overview** (Minnesota has 18 reciprocal states, broadest nationally. Many states require additional exams even with reciprocity. Some states have no reciprocity: Arkansas, Hawaii, Maine.)

**H2: Find your state** (link grid to all 50 /states/[slug] pages)

### FAQ (4 questions)
Q: Which state has the simplest drone licensing?
A: Washington. No standalone aerial category; just Laws and Safety plus a use category exam (2 exams minimum). $250/yr commercial license. WSDA published drone-specific guidance confirming drones are legal wherever airblast is legal.

Q: Which state has the hardest drone licensing?
A: Oregon. 50 hours flight experience required before applying for the Aerial Pesticide Applicator license, and it is illegal to spray by drone without APA even under supervision. California is the most complex with a separate Unmanned Pest Control Aircraft Pilot Certificate.

Q: Can I use one state license in another state?
A: Depends on reciprocity agreements. Minnesota recognizes 18 states. Pennsylvania recognizes 25+. Arkansas and Hawaii have no reciprocity. Even with reciprocity, many states require their own laws and regulations exam.

Q: Do private applicators need a commercial license for drones?
A: In Ohio, yes (unique nationally). In most states, private applicators can spray their own land with a private applicator license plus aerial endorsement. Check your specific state.

## Sub-page 4: `/regulations/ndaa-compliance`

**Title:** `NDAA Compliance for Ag Drones | DJI Ban, Blue UAS List & What It Means`
**Meta:** `What drone operators need to know about NDAA restrictions on Chinese drones. DJI status, Blue UAS list, Hylio compliance, and who is affected.`
**H1:** `NDAA Compliance: What Ag Drone Operators Need to Know`

### AEO Block
The National Defense Authorization Act restricts federal and many state agencies from purchasing or using drones with components from designated foreign manufacturers, which includes DJI and XAG. As of April 2026, DJI drones remain legal to purchase and operate in the US for private commercial use, but cumulative tariffs of 170 percent and pending legislation (Countering CCP Drones Act) create uncertainty. NDAA-compliant alternatives include Hylio AG-272 ($55,000 to $75,000), Pyka Pelican 2 ($550,000), and platforms on the DoD Blue UAS list.

### Content sections
**H2: What NDAA actually restricts** (federal procurement. Sections 889 and 848. Does NOT ban private commercial use of DJI.)

**H2: Who is affected** (federal agencies, state agencies using federal funds, university researchers on federal grants, operators bidding USDA or state-funded work. Private farm contracts are NOT affected.)

**H2: Current DJI status in the US** (legal to buy and operate. FY2025 NDAA Section 1709 required security review by Dec 2025. Countering CCP Drones Act excluded from FY2025 NDAA but active in subsequent cycles. 170% tariffs. UFLPA import holds.)

**H2: NDAA-compliant alternatives** (Hylio AG-272 and AG-230: US-made, Richmond TX, FY2019 Sec 889 and FY2020 Sec 848 compliant. Pyka Pelican 2: US-made, Alameda CA. DoD Blue UAS list for reference.)

**H2: Mixed fleet strategy** (run DJI for private farm contracts, Hylio for federal/state work. Common among operators who serve both markets.)

**H2: What to watch** (Countering CCP Drones Act in future legislative cycles. State-level NDAA mirror laws. Tariff trajectory.)

### FAQ (4 questions)
Q: Are DJI drones banned in the US?
A: No, as of April 2026. DJI products remain legal to purchase and operate for private commercial use. NDAA restricts federal agency procurement, not private commercial operations. Tariffs of 170 percent have increased costs significantly.

Q: Do I need an NDAA-compliant drone to spray crops?
A: Only if you bid on federal, state, or university-funded contracts. Private farm contracts have no NDAA requirement. Most US commercial ag drone operators still fly DJI.

Q: What is the Blue UAS list?
A: The Department of Defense Defense Innovation Unit maintains a list of drones cleared for government use. Inclusion means the platform passed a security review. Hylio, Skydio, and Parrot are on the list. DJI and XAG are not.

Q: Should I buy Hylio or DJI in 2026?
A: If you work or plan to work federal/state contracts: Hylio. If you work only private farms and want the lowest per-gallon cost: DJI T50. Many operators run both for different customer segments.
