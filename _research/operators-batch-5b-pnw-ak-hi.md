# US Agricultural Drone Directory: Oregon, Washington, Alaska, Hawaii

**Oregon and Washington dominate the Pacific Northwest ag drone landscape with roughly 25 confirmed operators, dealers, and training programs between them, while Hawaii has a small but growing sector and Alaska has essentially zero dedicated agricultural drone activity.** This batch identified **36 entities** across the four states: 12 in Oregon, 14 in Washington, 7 in Hawaii, and 3 in Alaska. The PNW's specialty crop mix (tree fruit, hops, wine grapes, hazelnuts, berries) and active forestry sector drive demand, but the market remains young. Most operators launched after 2021 and rely on DJI Agras platforms. Confirmed Part 137 certificate holders number only about 8 across all four states. Key data gaps persist in phone numbers, emails, social media, and founding dates for many operators.

**Totals:** 36 entities (20 operators, 5 dealers, 5 training/research, 3 manufacturers, 3 multi-category)

**Per-state count:** Oregon 12 | Washington 14 | Hawaii 7 | Alaska 3

**Category breakdown:** Operator 20 | Dealer 5 | Training/Research 5 | Manufacturer 3 | Multi 3

**Key data gaps:** No tribal ag drone programs confirmed (Yakama Nation, CTUIR, Native Hawaiian). No DJI Agras authorized dealers physically located in HI or AK. FAA does not publish a searchable public database of Part 137 UAS certificate holders. Several newer operators lack public contact details. Alaska has zero confirmed agricultural drone spraying operators.

## Regulatory notes by state

### Oregon

Oregon Department of Agriculture requires an **Aerial Pesticide Applicator (APA) license** for all drone pesticide application. Operators must hold an active ODA commercial, public, or private applicator license plus a valid FAA certificate plus an FAA medical certificate plus **50 hours of pesticide application flight time**. Drones weighing 55 lbs or more at takeoff require FAA Part 137 certification; drones under 55 lbs may operate under Part 107 with appropriate exemptions. ODA requires **10 aerial continuing education credits** per certification period. Oregon also hosts the **Pendleton UAS Test Range**, a key FAA-certified facility managed by ArgenTech Solutions, and the **Oregon UAS Accelerator** program providing $165K in benefits plus $40K grants per cohort company. The **Oregon Agricultural Aviation Association** (OAAA, contact Jesse McCormmach, 541-377-8004) represents aerial applicators including drone operators. ODA Pesticide Division contact: (503) 986-4635. Guidance document: oregon.gov/oda/Documents/Publications/PesticidesPARC/Drones-Pesticide-Licensing.pdf.

### Washington

Washington State Department of Agriculture requires a **Commercial Applicator License** for drone pesticide application on others' property. Operators must pass Laws & Safety exam plus applicable category exams and obtain a WA business license (UBI number). **WSDA requires an apparatus plate for each drone** used for pesticide application. WSDA drone guidance page: agr.wa.gov/departments/pesticides-and-fertilizers/pesticides/compliance/drone-guidance. WDFW contracted its first drone herbicide spraying project in 2023 (Skagit River delta via Altitude Agri Services) and has since purchased its own spraying drones. WSDOT conducted its first drone herbicide application along US-12 near Walla Walla in May 2023 (approximately 300 acres). The **Pacific Northwest Aerial Applicators Alliance** (PNWAAA, Tara Lea Brown, 509-989-9098) covers OR, WA, ID, and MT.

### Hawaii

Hawaii Department of Agriculture (HDOA) oversees pesticide applicator licensing. Aerial application of **Restricted Use Pesticides requires 24-hour advance notification** to HDOA. Aloha Aina Drones reports being "one of the few businesses in Hawaii" fully licensed by both FAA and HDOA for aerial application, indicating very limited Part 137 certification in the state. UH CTAHR has produced Part 137 petition templates and aerial pesticide operations manuals to help local farmers navigate the certification process. A 2019 USDA survey found only **182 drones in use** in Hawaii agriculture with **872 more wanted**, indicating high unmet demand. Invasive species work (coqui frogs, little fire ants, coconut rhinoceros beetles, invasive plants) is a major demand driver alongside coffee, macadamia, and tropical fruit crop protection. No DJI Agras authorized dealers are physically located in Hawaii. HDOA pesticide regulations: hdoa.hawaii.gov/wp-content/uploads/2019/09/HAR-4-66-2019-Pesticides.pdf.

### Alaska

Alaska has **no confirmed dedicated agricultural drone spraying operators**. The state's agricultural sector is small and concentrated in the Matanuska-Susitna Valley and Delta Junction. Alaska does not have reciprocal pesticide applicator licensing with other states. UAF's ACUASI is the most advanced drone program in the state but focuses on research, environmental monitoring, pipeline inspection, and cargo delivery rather than agriculture. The USDA Forest Service has used drones in Alaska for budworm damage assessment near Juneau but not for spraying. Alaska is part of the **Pan Pacific UAS Test Range Complex** alongside Oregon and Hawaii, providing infrastructure for future agricultural drone development. The Alaska Division of Agriculture has not published drone-specific pesticide applicator guidance comparable to Oregon or Washington.

## Conclusion

The Pacific Northwest agricultural drone sector is concentrated in **eastern Washington's tree fruit belt** (Yakima, Wenatchee, Chelan) and **Oregon's Willamette Valley** (hazelnuts, berries, wine grapes). Washington leads with the most confirmed spray operators and a robust ecosystem anchored by WSU research and active county weed board contracting. Oregon's strength lies in its UAS infrastructure (Pendleton test range, two FAA-approved training colleges, the UAS Accelerator) and Pratum Co-op's high-volume hazelnut spraying program at **19,000 acres in 2024**, which makes it one of the largest single-entity drone spray operations in the Pacific Northwest. Hawaii's market is embryonic but growing, driven by invasive species treatment and tropical crop needs, with Aloha Aina Drones as the sole confirmed Part 137 operator. Alaska remains a blank slate for ag drone services. Across all four states, the **DJI Agras platform dominates**, though XAG P150 (used by Pratum Co-op and sold by Raptor Dynamic in Portland) and the Talos T60X are gaining traction. The biggest structural gap is the absence of any DJI Agras authorized dealers physically located in Oregon, Hawaii, or Alaska, forcing operators to purchase through national distributors.
