# Mountain West Agricultural Drone Directory (Batch 4b)

**Eight states researched: Arizona, Colorado, Idaho, Montana, Nevada, New Mexico, Utah, Wyoming.** Idaho leads the region with 13 confirmed operators, followed by Montana (12), Colorado (10), and Wyoming (8, including 5 county government programs). Arizona, Utah, New Mexico, and Nevada remain severely underserved. The Mountain West's ag drone market is driven by invasive weed control on rangeland (cheatgrass, leafy spurge, noxious weeds) more than row crop spraying, distinguishing it from the Midwest. A total of **55 entities** were identified: 33 operators (including 7 government programs), 9 dealers/resellers, 7 training/education institutions, and 6 multi-category businesses.

## Regulatory notes by state

**Arizona.** The Arizona Department of Agriculture Environmental Services Division (contact: Jack Paterson) oversees pesticide applicator licensing. Tribal lands in AZ create significant regulatory complexity. Drone flights on Gila River Indian Community land require tribal council approval. The Tohono O'odham Nation and Salt River Pima-Maricopa Indian Community have separate governance. No state-specific drone spraying legislation was identified beyond standard pesticide applicator requirements.

**Colorado.** Licensed drone applicators must appear on county commercial spraying applicator lists. Weld County and Larimer County maintain specific UAV/Drone Applicator categories on their lists. Colorado Agricultural Aviation Association (CoAAA) hosts PAASS recertification covering CO, ID, KS, NM, OK, TX, and WY. Boulder County contracted certified drone operators for herbicide application at Red Hill Open Space Area in December 2025, signaling growing municipal adoption.

**Idaho.** Idaho Department of Agriculture handles pesticide applicator licensing. Idaho has the densest concentration of ag drone operators in the Mountain West, driven by the potato, sugar beet, and grain industries. The state has active cooperation between county weed departments (e.g., Fremont County) and private drone operators. Simplot Grower Solutions uses Rantizo drones on irrigated acres through Idaho operators. Fremont County Weed Department treats over 6,000 acres annually with drones.

**Montana.** Montana Department of Agriculture oversees commercial applicator licensing. Multiple county weed districts (Flathead, Hill) have adopted drones. The Association of Montana Aerial Applicators is active. Montana State University is building a formal precision agriculture program with drone research. The University of Montana's Autonomous Aerial Systems Office is described as "the main hub for all drones throughout the state."

**Nevada.** No dedicated ag drone spray operators were identified. Nevada's agriculture is concentrated in a few counties (Churchill, Lyon, Humboldt, Pershing, Elko) with roughly 484,000 acres of cropland, 90%+ used for hay and alfalfa. The University of Nevada Reno Extension offers UAV training and pesticide certification. This state represents a significant gap in the market.

**New Mexico.** No in-state ag drone spray operators were confirmed. Operators from Texas (e.g., Tex-Air Drone) serve parts of NM. NMSU Extension provides educational resources on drone use for ranches. The Navajo Nation (which spans NM, AZ, UT) has a formal UAV policy (RDCD-107-18, adopted December 2018) requiring permits from Navajo DOT Department of Airports. Drones are strictly prohibited in all Navajo Tribal Parks. Navajo Agricultural Products Industry (NAPI) manages significant farmland but has not adopted drone spraying. Chile, pecan, and cotton growers represent untapped markets.

**Utah.** Utah Department of Agriculture and Food oversees pesticide licensing. Utah State University Extension conducts drone spraying research for weed management (Dr. Earl Creech) and trains drone pilots through its Drones Aviation Technology program. The Ute Tribe has agricultural operations but no drone spraying programs were identified. Davis County Mosquito Abatement District uses drones for larvicide application.

**Wyoming.** Wyoming has no standalone aerial applicator association but operators can recertify through Colorado's CoAAA. The most notable development is the rapid adoption by county Weed and Pest Districts: at least 5 of Wyoming's 23 Weed and Pest Districts (Sublette, Fremont, Teton, Big Horn, Carbon) have purchased or are developing drone programs. Cheatgrass is the primary target. Federal lands managed by USFS (Medicine Bow NF) and BLM (Lander Field Office) still use helicopters for large-scale treatments but represent future drone markets. The FAA granted Wyoming Drone Spraying LLC a Part 137 exemption (Docket FAA-2025-0056) in 2025.

**Tribal land considerations (region-wide).** Tribal sovereignty creates additional regulatory layers across the Mountain West. The Navajo Nation (largest reservation in the US, spanning AZ, NM, UT) requires separate UAV permits through Navajo DOT. The Gila River Indian Community in AZ requires tribal council approval for any drone flights. No tribal agriculture programs were found to be actively using drone spraying. GRIC operates 37,000+ acres of farmland (cotton, wheat, alfalfa, citrus). Colorado River Indian Tribes and Shoshone-Bannock Tribes have farming operations but no drone adoption was confirmed. Tribal lands represent a large, currently unserved market with unique regulatory requirements.

**Federal agency context.** The USFS and BLM conduct extensive aerial herbicide programs across Mountain West rangeland (cheatgrass, leafy spurge) using helicopters. USFS Medicine Bow NF treated 41,000+ acres over 4 years. BLM Lander Field Office partners with county Weed and Pest districts. USFS Salmon-Challis NF sprayed 7,500 acres for cheatgrass. These programs represent large-scale future opportunities as agencies explore drone technology, which several Wyoming county programs are already demonstrating at smaller scales.
