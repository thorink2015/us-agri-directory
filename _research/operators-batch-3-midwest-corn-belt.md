# Batch 3: US Midwest / Corn Belt Agricultural Drone Directory

## Summary

**Total entities found: 97** across 8 Midwest states. This includes operators, dealers, training schools, manufacturers, and multi-category entities actively involved in agricultural drone spraying in Illinois, Indiana, Iowa, Michigan, Minnesota, Missouri, Ohio, and Wisconsin.

**Per-state count:** Illinois 16 | Indiana 10 | Iowa 16 | Michigan 14 | Minnesota 11 | Missouri 8 | Ohio 12 | Wisconsin 4 | Cross-state serving Midwest 6

**Per-category breakdown:** Operators 48 | Dealers 14 | Multi (operator/dealer/training combo) 20 | Training/Research 7 | Manufacturers 3 | Franchise operators 4 | Consulting 1

**Key findings:** Iowa is the epicenter of the US spray drone industry, home to Rantizo/American Autonomy (largest operator network, ~200,000 acres sprayed in 2023) and Terraplex Ag (largest dealer west of the Mississippi). Ohio hosts nuWay Ag (Dundee), the leading DJI Agras dealer with a nationwide FastPass Part 137 program. Missouri hosts Agri Spray Drones (Boonville), the largest US ag spray drone distributor (4.6M+ acres facilitated, 41 states). Michigan has Exedy Drones (Belleville), an emerging US-made drone manufacturer. The Midwest likely has 300-400+ total operators, but many small or seasonal operators have minimal web presence (Facebook-only or word of mouth). The 97 entities below represent the most findable and verifiable operators through public sources.

**Data gaps:** Many operators lack publicly listed phone numbers, emails, or social media. FAA Part 137 certificate numbers are rarely published publicly. Year founded data is missing for roughly 40% of entries. Wisconsin has the fewest confirmed spray drone operators despite significant agricultural activity. Facebook-only operators are underrepresented as Facebook business pages are difficult to systematically enumerate.

## State-by-state regulatory notes

**Illinois:** The Illinois Department of Agriculture requires an Aerial Commercial Applicator License for drone pesticide application. Operators must hold FAA Part 107 and Part 137 certificates plus state licensure. The Illinois Agricultural Aviation Association (IAAA) requires members to hold valid Part 137 Operating Certificates. University of Illinois Extension PSEP provides guidance on UAS-based licensing at extension.illinois.edu/psep/special-licensing.

**Indiana:** Regulated by the Office of Indiana State Chemist (OISC). Drone use is classified as aerial application. Operators need FAA Part 107, Part 137, and state pesticide applicator certification. Purdue published PPP-156 (Using Spray Drones in Indiana) with detailed certification guidance. Albertson Drone Service was the first licensed drone spraying contractor in the state (2020). Contact: Jill Davis at OISC, 765-494-1594.

**Iowa:** Regulated by Iowa Department of Agriculture and Land Stewardship (IDALS) Pesticide Bureau (515-281-8591). Requires Category 11 Aerial Application certification (35-question closed-book exam, 80% pass for commercial). Aerial applicators on ag land must consult an aerial applicator consultant (must be Iowa resident). Also requires Pesticide Company License. Fee: $75 for 3-year commercial certification. Iowa was the first Midwest state to approve drone spraying (Rantizo, July 2019). IDALS guidance document available at iowaagriculture.gov.

**Michigan:** Regulated by Michigan Department of Agriculture and Rural Development (MDARD). Has stringent requirements: Commercial Pesticide UAV Applicator certification needed in core plus Aerial Standard (AE) plus crop categories. Requires at least 3 years experience with no fewer than 200 hours of ag aerial application under supervision (or completion of approved training program). Michigan approved drone crop spraying in July 2021. MDARD license application (PABL) and flowchart available at michigan.gov/mdard. Annual mix/load pad location reports required.

**Minnesota:** Regulated by Minnesota Department of Agriculture (MDA). Private applicators using drones for restricted-use pesticides need General Aerial Endorsement (Category B) plus Private Applicator Certification. Commercial/noncommercial applicators need Categories A (Core), B (General Aerial), and crop-specific categories. Must be at least 18. 3rd class medical certificate required for drones over 55 lbs. Closed-book proctored exam, 70% to pass. Reciprocity with all Midwest states. MDA administers Private Applicator General Aerial Endorsement Program.

**Missouri:** Regulated by Missouri Department of Agriculture (MDA) Pesticide Program (573-751-5504). Aerial application category covers fixed wing, rotary, and drone aircraft. Private applicator license required for restricted-use pesticide purchase/application ($75 online via MU Extension). Application by unlicensed workers under supervision no longer allowed beginning 2025. Must recertify every 5 years. Missouri is home to Agri Spray Drones (Boonville), the largest US ag spray drone distributor.

**Ohio:** Regulated by Ohio Department of Agriculture (ODA). Requires FAA Part 107 plus Part 137 certificates. Ohio Commercial Pesticide Category 1 exam required (application of pesticides except fumigants by aircraft). ODA has specific guidance document for UAV pesticide applications. OSU Extension provides Aerial Pesticide Applicator (Category 1) Exam Study Guide. Ohio is home to nuWay Ag (Dundee), whose FastPass program lets new operators spray under nuWay's Part 137 while their own is pending.

**Wisconsin:** Regulated by Wisconsin Department of Agriculture, Trade and Consumer Protection (DATCP). Category 9.9 Aerial Applicator covers airplanes, helicopters, and UAS/drones. Private applicators must certify in a base category (General Farm, Fruit, or Greenhouse/Nursery) before adding Aerial Sub-Category. Must pass certification exam; certified for 5 years. ATCP 29 code governs aerial applicator certification. DNR NR 107 permits required for aquatic vegetation management. UW Extension PAT Program provides study manuals at fyi.extension.wisc.edu/pat/.

**Federal (all states):** FAA Part 107 Remote Pilot Certificate required for all commercial drone operations. Part 137 Agricultural Aircraft Operator Certificate required for dispensing any economic poison or agricultural substance. Drones under 55 lbs need Part 107 plus exemption from 107.36 (hazardous material carriage). Drones 55 lbs and over (most spray drones) need Part 91/137 plus 44807 approval and N-number registration via CARES. Submit petition via regulations.gov docket FAA-2007-0001-0001. As of late 2024, 3rd class medical no longer required for daytime autonomous operations. FAA estimates approximately 1000 pilots authorized for agricultural drone spraying as of 2025. Total US ag drone treated acreage reached 16.4 million acres in 2025 (up 58.7% year-over-year per American Spray Drone Coalition). New DJI import restrictions (FCC ruling Dec 2025) have caused new spray drone sales to drop 59%, spurring US manufacturers like Exedy Drones (MI) and Revolution Drones to enter the market.
