# Mountain West Agricultural Drone Operator Directory (Batch 4b)

## Summary

**Total entries: 60** across 8 Mountain West states plus 12 national companies serving the region. Idaho leads with 14 local entries, followed by Wyoming (8), Colorado (6), Montana (6), Utah (6), Arizona (3), New Mexico (3), and Nevada (2). The directory includes **22 operators** (3 government), **15 dealers**, **8 training/extension programs**, **2 manufacturers**, **5 multi-category companies**, and **12 national platforms/dealers**. Arizona, Nevada, and New Mexico represent major data gaps with almost no locally based spray operators found despite significant irrigated acreage. Tribal agricultural drone programs (Navajo Nation, Crow, Wind River, Ute Mountain Ute, Tohono O'odham, Salt River Pima) returned zero confirmed results. The FAA Part 137 UAS registry is not publicly searchable, limiting verification. Dynamic dealer locator maps (AcreConnect, Agri Spray Drones, Raptor Dynamic) could not be scraped and may contain additional operators.

**Per-state counts:** AZ: 3 | CO: 6 | ID: 14 | MT: 6 | NV: 2 | NM: 3 | UT: 6 | WY: 8 | National: 12

## Regulatory notes by state

**Arizona.** Arizona Department of Agriculture Environmental Services Division (contact: Jack Peterson) oversees pesticide applicator licensing. Commercial applicator license required for drone spraying. FAA Part 137 and Part 107 required. No state-specific drone pesticide regulations beyond federal requirements. Reference: rotor.ai/spray-laws/arizona.

**Colorado.** Colorado Department of Agriculture Pesticide Programs (John Scott, 303-869-9056). Commercial Applicator License required. At least one pilot must hold Part 137. Part 107 required for all drone operators. Minimum $400,000 liability insurance. Licensed Qualified Supervisor required per pest management category. $350 annual licensure fee. Certificate of Good Standing from CO Secretary of State for LLCs. Reference: ag.colorado.gov/commercial-applicators.

**Idaho.** Idaho State Department of Agriculture manages pesticide applicator licensing. Part 107 and Part 137 required for commercial drone spraying. Idaho recognizes reciprocal licensing from some states. RC Agriculture publishes detailed Idaho-specific Part 137 compliance guides. Multiple counties (Fremont, Bonneville) operate their own drone weed control programs. Reference: rotor.ai/spray-laws/idaho.

**Montana.** Montana Department of Agriculture manages pesticide applicator licensing (agr.mt.gov). Part 107 and Part 137 required. Montana has seen regulatory barriers impact operators: Cahoon Aerial Systems (Seeley Lake) closed after inability to obtain FAA exemption for drones over 55 lbs. Reciprocal licensing info available at agr.mt.gov. Reference: rotor.ai/spray-laws/montana.

**Nevada.** Nevada Department of Agriculture oversees applicator licensing. Part 107 and Part 137 required. Nevada has the smallest agricultural sector among the 8 states (primarily alfalfa/hay in Churchill, Pershing, Humboldt, and Lyon Counties). No dedicated local ag drone spray operators were confirmed. Market likely served by operators from Utah, Idaho, and California. Reference: rotor.ai/spray-laws/nevada.

**New Mexico.** New Mexico Department of Agriculture AES Division (Brad Lewis) oversees applicator licensing. Part 107 and Part 137 required. NMSU Extension has published drone ranch management guidance (Publication B-828). No locally based drone spray operators were confirmed. Market primarily served by Texas-based operators (Tex-Air Drone) and national platforms. Navajo Nation requires UAV registration with Navajo DOT and permits from Navajo Film Office (RDCD-107-18). Reference: rotor.ai/spray-laws/new-mexico.

**Utah.** Utah Department of Agriculture and Food (UDAF) oversees applicator licensing. Part 107 and Part 137 required. FieldWing Precision (North Ogden) holds full state licensing stack: Part 107, Part 137, state pesticide applicator license, over-55-lbs UAS exemption, and spray drift insurance. USU Extension Aggie Drone Academy (Price, UT) provides Part 107 prep and ag drone education. Reference: rotor.ai/spray-laws/utah.

**Wyoming.** Wyoming Department of Agriculture manages commercial pesticide applicator licensing ($25/license, $25/aircraft). Part 107 and Part 137 required. County Weed and Pest districts are early adopters: Sublette County (Hylio AG-130), Fremont County, and Carbon County (developing). Wyoming Drone Spraying (Lander) received FAA 44807 Exemption (FAA-2025-0056) in 2025. Wyoming Weed and Pest Council actively promotes drone technology. Reference: rotor.ai/spray-laws/wyoming.

## Key data gaps and limitations

The FAA Part 137 UAS certificate registry is not publicly searchable, so Part 137 status could only be confirmed where operators self-report or where FAA exemption dockets appear on regulations.gov. Interactive dealer locator maps for Agri Spray Drones, Raptor Dynamic (60+ locations), and AcreConnect (map.acreconnect.io) render dynamically and could not be scraped; these likely contain additional Mountain West operators not captured here. Arizona, Nevada, and New Mexico have almost no confirmed locally based spray operators despite significant irrigated acreage, suggesting either very early market stage or operators without web presence who work through word-of-mouth and platform networks like nuWay Ag's pilot finder. No tribal agricultural drone programs were confirmed across any of the 8 states. State pesticide applicator databases (MDA, WDA, CDA, ISDA, UDAF, NMDA, AZDA, NDOA) are not publicly searchable online in a way that isolates drone applicators from conventional applicators. Sugar beet co-ops (Western Sugar, Amalgamated, Snake River Sugar), the Idaho Mint Commission, Idaho Wheat Commission, Colorado Wheat, and NM Pecan Growers showed no public drone-specific programs. The Cahoon Aerial Systems closure in Montana (regulatory barriers for drones over 55 lbs) illustrates ongoing FAA certification friction that likely suppresses operator counts across all 8 states.
