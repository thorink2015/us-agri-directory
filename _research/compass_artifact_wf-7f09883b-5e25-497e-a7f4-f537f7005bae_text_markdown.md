# What separates US ag-drone spray operators who build a business from those who quit (2024–2026 research file)

**Scope and caveats.** US market only, 2024–2026 sources prioritized. All named operators, dollar figures, and quotes below come from publicly published trade press, podcast episodes, company press releases, extension publications, or FAA/state documents. Sections flagged "RESEARCH THIN" are where public documentation does not support firm claims — this is noted explicitly rather than filled with generic advice. Two subagent runs on customer-acquisition and named-operator research were blocked during execution, so those sections were completed via direct web search by the lead researcher and may be less exhaustive than the failure/success and regional sections.

**Industry baseline (ASDC 2025 Impact Survey, released January 2026, https://americanspraydronecoalition.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/2025-ASDC-Impact-Survey-Results.pdf):** US drone-treated acreage reached **16.4 million in 2025**, up 58.7% from 10.3M in 2024 and ~4M in 2023. Part 137-authorized UAS operators: **1,710 (+58.3% YoY)**. Average acres/operator: 9,584 (flat). New spray-drone unit sales dropped 59% (8,950 → 3,711) after UFLPA enforcement blocked DJI imports. **Average price per acre fell 38%, from $21 in 2024 to $13 in 2025.** 69% of Part 137 operators have fleets of ≤2 drones; ~86% of drones sold are owned by NON-Part 137 operators. ASDC member companies (≈80% of market): Agri Spray Drones, Bestway Ag, Drone Nerds, HSE-UAV, Pegasus Robotics, Rantizo.

## 1. How new operators find their first paying work

### Word of mouth: the single biggest differentiator between ag-rooted and outsider operators

The ag-roots advantage is documented most clearly in the **Crum brothers (Midwest Air, Peebles, OH)**. Hayden Crum (20, co-founder 2021) launched the business as a high-school freshman on the back of his family's existing Crum Farm Supply operation — nearly 50 years selling fertilizer, seed, and chemicals to the same Adams County farmers. Midwest Air has now sprayed ~100,000 acres and is scaling to 25,000–30,000 acres per season with 4 crews (https://the-droneon-show.castos.com/episodes/ohios-young-gun-blazing-drone-spraying-trails-in-family-farming-the-droneon-show-episode-22; https://www.midwestairdrones.com/about; https://www.informerpress.com/2025/05/10251/). The Crums also openly describe age as a headwind that forced them to lead with results: **"If you can have an intelligent conversation about how this will make a farmer money, you'll gain their trust"** (Hayden Crum, https://dronespraypro.com/blogs/news/build-profitable-agricultural-drone-business).

**Jonathan Cottingham (Southern Drone OPS, Jonesboro, AR)** is a third-generation aerial applicator — the family airplane-crop-dusting history was his referral network (https://arfb.com/news/2024/jun/03/out-old-new-southern-drone-ops/; https://www.farmprogress.com/technology/opting-for-on-farm-drones). **Kam Harper (Macon Ridge Specialty Drone Service, Wisner, LA)** was a former farm manager whose first customers were his former employer's neighbors: **"I bought the drone, and about three months into it, I was so busy that I couldn't keep up"** (Delta Farm Press, Oct 17 2025, https://editions.mydigitalpublication.com/article/Louisiana+farmer+grows+drone+spraying+business+as+tech+gains+acceptance/5055941/854594/article.html). Harper has sprayed 120,000+ acres in three years.

For outsiders, trade-press guidance from established operators converges on starting with seed and chemical dealers rather than farmers directly. Ackerspray's founder guide: **"Talk to local seed and chemical dealers to find out who they are, and identify how much they charge and how much business they get per year"** (https://www.ackerspray.com/blog/the-ultimate-guide-to-starting-an-ag-drone-spraying-business). Paige Browning (Nebraska rancher) is the best-documented outsider-style operator; her public quote on the slog: **"It's a great idea and all of that, but it's not easy"** (Investigate Midwest, Jan 8 2025, https://investigatemidwest.org/2025/01/08/as-the-agricultural-drone-industry-takes-off-federal-regulators-struggle-to-keep-up/).

### Direct outreach, co-ops, and channel partnerships

The **farm cooperative channel** is documented most clearly by **Knox Farmers Cooperative (East Tennessee)**, which built its own drone spray service under co-op manager Lewis Jones and agronomy manager Kevin Martin: **"In our area and across into the North Carolina mountains, it's either difficult or impractical to use a helicopter or plane to spray fungicides"** (https://www.ourcoop.com/news/agronomy/taking-flight). Knox also contracts with surrounding co-ops — a template for outsider operators: become the co-op's flying contractor.

**Soil Service, Inc.** (Midwest ag retailer) markets corn VT/soybean R3 fungicide programs to its own customer base using company-licensed pilots (https://soilserviceinc.com/crop-choice-starter-fertilizer/spray-drone-application/). **Beck's Hybrids' Practical Farm Research (Clayton Stufflebeam)** demonstrates drone-vs-ground trials on-farm, giving partnered applicators credibility: **"The pilot matters. One study will show ground applications performing better than drones, while another shows the opposite"** (https://www.farmprogress.com/crops/data-s-in-drone-vs-ground-sprayer). **Tom Hermann (Altitude Ag LLC)**, an AgriGold seed dealer, runs fungicide trials for the seed brand — the seed-dealer-as-applicator model (https://agrigold.com/resources/agronomy-library/agrigold-drone-trials-vet-fungicides-and-how-they-best-pair-with-hybrids). Rantizo reports its network operators **"fly and apply with more than 20% of the Top 50 ag retailers"** (https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/rantizo-announces-over-6m-series-a-funding-expansion-301944686.html).

### Subcontracting networks: the fastest path for day-one revenue

**nuWay FastPass (Dundee, OH)** is the most explicit documented subcontracting program: new operators who have bought equipment but are waiting on their own Part 137 approval "legally spray as a contractor under nuWay Ag's existing Part 137 certificate." nuWay handles billing and files monthly FAA reports. Membership fee **$800**; operators must carry $1M liability naming nuWay Ag Application LLC as additional insured (https://nuwayag.com/products/part-137-pilot-program). nuWay's pitch: "FastPass lets you start booking jobs and generating revenue in as little as 1–2 weeks." Mike Yoder's homepage testimonial: **"Mike and nuWay Ag helped me get started in Ag business — in less than a month we've sprayed over a thousand acres"** (https://nuwayag.com/).

**Rantizo's operator network** connected supply and demand across 30 states before the company divested services in 2025 and rebranded as American Autonomy to focus on AcreConnect software (https://www.rantizo.com/our-network; https://agfundernews.com/dji-faces-uncertain-future-in-us-as-ag-spray-drone-industry-readies-for-realignment). **SweetWater Technologies (Wyanet, IL)** — Chad and TeNeille Gripp — launched a franchise model explicitly targeting young ag professionals: **"Sweetwater Powered by Gripp is here to create a business… for our partners while providing exceptional service to our farmer clients"** (https://www.croplife.com/smart-tech/calling-all-young-ag-professionals-start-your-agricultural-drone-business-today/). SweetWater grew from 32,000 acres in 2022 to a projected 200,000 acres in 2025 with a 25-drone fleet and franchising in IL, IN, MO.

### Directories and content marketing

**agdronedirectory.com** lists operators by region, fleet size, and specialty. **nuWay Ag's YouTube channel** (Mike Yoder, https://www.youtube.com/@nuWayAg) produces weekly content and sells a 90+ page startup eBook priced at the customer acquisition problem directly: **"I Made $65k,000 in Only 8 Days!"** as the landing-page hook (https://nuwayag.com/). **The DroneOn Show** (Yoder, host) has profiled ~30 operators 2024–2026 as a de facto trust-building network. **Drone to 1K Podcast** (Drone Launch Academy) features Brandon Beal of Elevation Aerial (VA) going from $50K year-1 to $170–180K year-2 (https://podtail.com/en/podcast/drone-to-1k-podcast-by-drone-launch-academy/s7-ep-3-brandon-beal-of-elevation-aerial-applicati/). **Ag Drone Zone Podcast** (Brittney Truman / U-PASS, launched 2025, https://podcasts.apple.com/us/podcast/ag-drone-zone/id1800879006) covers an audience of aspiring operators.

## 2. Skills new operators underestimate

### Agronomy knowledge — the credibility gate

Beck's agronomy lead Matt Montgomery and researcher Clayton Stufflebeam frame the gap: **"The pilot matters"** — meaning product/timing/growth-stage decisions, not drone stick skills, explain most of the efficacy variance (https://www.farmprogress.com/crops/data-s-in-drone-vs-ground-sprayer). University of Arkansas extension's **Jason Davis** on why label literacy is non-negotiable: **"You've got to follow the label, no matter what"** — and **"Some of the more recent label interpretations have created some gray areas or some complete no-go zones for some of our drone applications"** (https://www.farmprogress.com/crop-protection/lost-in-translation-drone-spraying-hits-label-limits). Kam Harper's operational philosophy on the Delta: **"In herbicide, if it's not done properly… in seven days, you are going to be able to tell me every mistake made"** (Delta Farm Press, Oct 17 2025).

### Chemical handling and tank mixing — the technical gate most new operators fail on first

Extension and industry sources converge on a standard **A.P.P.L.E.S. mixing order**: Agitate, Powder solubles, Powders dry, Liquid flowables, Emulsifiable concentrates, Solutions — with adjuvants added in the same-phase group (https://www.chsagronomy.com/insights/adjuvant-101-proper-tank-mixing-order). Drone-specific gotchas documented by Erdal Ozkan (Ohio State) and Farm Progress: **"None of the spray drones available today have any sort of agitation in the solution tank"** — so suspension-sensitive chemistries can clog pumps or nozzles (https://www.farmprogress.com/technology/what-to-know-before-making-a-spray-drone-pesticide-application; https://ohioline.osu.edu/factsheet/fabe-540).

Low gallons-per-acre (GPA) amplifies errors: drones typically run **1–5 GPA vs 10–20 GPA for ground rigs**, which concentrates the tank and magnifies compatibility problems (https://iandbagsupply.com/agricultural-drone-adjuvants). A **jar test is mandatory** when tank-mixing any new drift-reducing adjuvant (DRA) because some oil-based DRAs foam or break compatibility (https://www.farmprogress.com/technology/what-to-know-before-making-a-spray-drone-pesticide-application). Jason Deveau (OMAFA's application technology specialist, co-administrator of Sprayers101): biological vs chemical incompatibility can cause crop damage or reduced efficacy; physical can cause clogs, foaming, settling, or phase separation (https://sprayers101.com/tankmix/).

### Drift management — the regulatory gate

Best-practice thresholds repeatedly documented: **avoid spraying above 10 mph wind**, avoid temperature inversions, monitor sensitive downwind crops (https://www.farmprogress.com/technology/what-to-know-before-making-a-spray-drone-pesticide-application). **Drone-specific drift physics** differ from planes: rotor downwash interacts with droplets and fine droplet spectra increase drift potential relative to ground rigs (https://jinghongdrone.com/chemical-drift-and-applicator-liability-in-drone-spraying). LSU AgCenter on drone operations: **"If mixing tank loads near the drone take-off and landing area, a large amount of debris can be kicked up during the take-off and landing procedure, contaminating the tank loads. Lids may be necessary"** (https://www.lsuagcenter.com/articles/page1704405979376).

### Equipment maintenance and in-field repair — learned the hard way

The Midwest Air (Crum) DroneOn Show episode is the canonical documentation of year-1 equipment pain points. Crum on a charger/generator mismatch: **"They told us, okay, one charger and three batteries… we thought the charger would charge four at a time. Not the case. It charges one at a time. And we had a generator that was not big enough. Went out for our first custom job… it took four days to do 100 acre field… we were such idiots."** Battery-cutoff crash on a T20: **"we had no idea that when this drone gets to 10% battery… the drone lands wherever it's at. It doesn't care… this thing comes in like a rocket, bounces off the trailer and hits my hand."** Power-line strike on first flight: **"we took off underneath the power line… it shredded one of the strands in the wire."** Zach and Gabe Martindale (Michigan) openly discussed a **T40 crash** on DroneOn Show Episode 9 (https://the-droneon-show.castos.com/episodes/zach-gabes-drone-grind-building-a-spray-business-in-michigan-droneon-show-ep-9). Paige Browning's summary: **"But when you get into the nitty gritty and actually doing it, it's a very dirty job"** (Investigate Midwest).

### Back-office skills — RESEARCH THIN at named-operator level

Customer communication, invoicing, and A/R management are universally acknowledged as gaps in trade-press guides and the GrabaRobot 2026 guide (https://www.grabarobot.com/guides/how-to-start-drone-spraying-business/), but **no named US operator has publicly documented specific A/R failures or late-payment disputes in 2024–2026** sources reviewed. nuWay FastPass handles subcontractor billing on behalf of operators, which itself is evidence the market views back-office as a bottleneck worth outsourcing.

## 3. Common failure modes in year 1

### Underpricing and margin compression

The hardest data point in the entire file: **national average price-per-acre fell from $21 in 2024 to $13 in 2025** — a 38% single-year collapse — per ASDC 2025 survey. Eric Ringer (American Autonomy/ASDC) on cause: **"ASDC members report substantial undercutting by non-Part 137 operators willing to accept lower rates, particularly in the corn belt"** (https://agfundernews.com/revolution-drones-and-exedy-drones-target-us-ag-spray-drone-market-as-fcc-rewrites-the-rules). University of Missouri Extension G1274 economics: small operators (500–800 acres) face costs approaching $20/acre; large custom operators (4,000+ acres) break below $7.39/acre (https://extension.missouri.edu/publications/g1274). Break-even ≈3,900 acres/yr at $7.50/acre cost basis. Rantizo regional sales manager Ben Etsinger: **"places like Indiana and further east will pay as much as around $20 per acre"** while Iowa is lower (https://www.commercialuavnews.com/forestry/how-can-you-measure-the-roi-of-your-drone-based-farming-program).

### Wrong drone for the job

The Crum brothers' T20 5-gallon tanks limited them on their first 100-acre job; they migrated to T40s by 2024. Martindale brothers in Michigan documented a T40 crash during scale-up. Operator-for-sale listing on Minneapolis Craigslist (2025) documents a full business exit: **"Selling my custom drone spraying business… EA Vision J-100 (version 2) spray drone with about 85 acres on it (yes, that's correct — just 85 acres)"** — a turn-key Minnesota C-Corp, $35K in bank, essentially never-used equipment (https://minneapolis.craigslist.org/hnp/bfs/d/minneapolis-custom-spray-drone-business/7893110633.html). **This is the single most direct public evidence of a named-entity year-1 exit in the sample.**

### Operating without Part 137 — FAA enforcement

The most detailed 2024 FAA enforcement case is documented by Rupprecht Law (https://jrupprechtlaw.com/part-137-drone-operator-enforcement-action/): a drone-seller/training-for-hire operator's exemption was **rescinded July 5, 2024** after April 8–9, 2024 in-person FAA surveillance. Violations: unregistered DJI T40 (registration signed 2 days AFTER FAA inspection); training non-employees for $999/course (Condition 21 prohibition); operating within 100 ft of a non-participant farmer (violates 500-ft limit); "multiple misleading statements to the FAA" with potential 18 USC §1001 exposure. FAA's broader posture: **"operational validations" and RFIs are being sent to exemption holders** (https://www.faa.gov/uas/advanced_operations/dispensing_chemicals). FAA 2025 enforcement sweep (Jan 2026 release) fined/suspended/revoked 18 drone operators with fines from **$1,771 to $36,770**; max raised to **$75,000** per violation (https://www.faa.gov/newsroom/faa-steps-drone-enforcement-2025).

Investigate Midwest (Jan 8, 2025, https://investigatemidwest.org/2025/01/08/as-the-agricultural-drone-industry-takes-off-federal-regulators-struggle-to-keep-up/): **"Several pilots told Investigate Midwest they had illegally flown agricultural drones or suggested they knew others who did."** 200+ pilots were waiting for Part 137 certs at the start of 2024 season; 1,200+ applications filed in H1 2024. Isaac Strubbe (IL): **"If you wait until June to start your business, it just takes longer than you think it will. July hits, and legally, you can't do anything about it."** Peter Van Ess (Washington State) publicly described an unannounced FAA field audit, passed using U-PASS audit-prep program (https://www.upass.foundation/post/how-u-pass-helps-ag-drone-pilots-pass-their-faa-audit-step-by-step).

### State pesticide-license enforcement — RESEARCH THIN in the US

No named US drone-specific state drift-fine enforcement actions 2024–2026 surfaced in searches of IA, IL, MO, AR, MS, LA, NE, OH, KS plant-board press releases. Iowa max civil fine is $500/incident (criticized as inadequate by Pesticide Action Network, https://www.panna.org/blog/iowa-needs-updated-pesticide-policies); Nebraska max $15,000; Illinois $750–$10,000 point system. The only named drift-fine against a drone operator located was **international** (Australia: Agriculture Victoria fined a drone spraying business $15,000 at Colac Magistrates' Court — not a US case).

### Insurance gaps

**BWI Fly** documents representative loss scenarios: $40K for a dicamba overspray onto a neighbor's soybeans; $85K for fungicide drift onto an organically certified vineyard (https://bwifly.com/chemical-drift-liability/). **Aeris Insurance Solutions** warns: **"If you spray the wrong field… cause drift damage to another field owned by Farmer A (Adjacent Fields) there is no coverage in the unendorsed policy"** (https://www.aerisinsurance.com/aerial-application-liability-coverages). Target-crop and adjacent-fields exclusions are the default in standard ag aviation policies. **Coverdrone launched chemical liability and cargo liability options for 2026** — an explicit admission of pre-2026 market gaps (https://www.suasnews.com/2025/12/coverdronenew-for-2026-chemical-liability-and-cargo-liability-options-for-commercial-operators/). Named US drift lawsuit judgments against drone operators: **none surfaced in 2024–2026 searches.**

### Cashflow mismatch and customer concentration

Public trade-press acknowledges spring-expense / fall-payment net-60-90 mismatch as a known risk (GrabaRobot 2026 guide) but **no named operator case study was located.** Over-concentration is documented by counter-example: Kam Harper diversified into drone sales, service, custom-built trailers, game retrieval, and custom farming — an explicit admission the single-customer risk is real (https://www.farmprogress.com/technology/delta-farmer-turns-interest-in-drones-into-thriving-business).

### Business closures: the Rantizo divestiture

The most significant US ag-drone business exit of the period is **Rantizo's 2025 divestiture of its spray-services business** to a private investment group, with the parent rebranded as American Autonomy focused on AcreConnect software. Mariah Scott: **"We predicted that DJI was going to be banned… So we decided to sell off the spray services part of the business… and focus on software"** (https://agfundernews.com/dji-faces-uncertain-future-in-us-as-ag-spray-drone-industry-readies-for-realignment). This is not a failure in the burnout sense but a strategic exit from the service layer by the network's leading operator.

## 4. What successful year-2 operators did differently

### Crop/application specialization beats generalism

**Kam Harper (Macon Ridge, LA)** on Delta specialization: **"In the Mississippi Delta, especially compared to the Midwest… the majority of what we do is herbicide. That is 90%."** 120,000+ acres in three years; 60,000 projected in 2025 alone. **Brandon Beal (Elevation Aerial Application, Galax, VA)** specialized in Christmas tree farms, corn fungicide, and pastures; Drone to 1K Podcast Ep. 3 documents **Year 1 revenue $50,000 → Year 2 $170,000–$180,000** with 3 drones (https://podtail.com/en/podcast/drone-to-1k-podcast-by-drone-launch-academy/s7-ep-3-brandon-beal-of-elevation-aerial-applicati/). **Jonathan Cottingham (Southern Drone OPS, AR)** specialized in Delta rice/soybean/cotton and expanded dealership licensure to 11 states (https://southerndroneops.com/pages/our-team). **Midwest Air (Crum, OH)** layered **H2Ohio cover-crop seeding** on top of fungicide — exploiting Ohio's ~$50/acre state program paying farmers for cover crops.

### Second revenue streams

The pattern across every successful year-2 operator in the sample is **at least two lines of revenue**:
- **Mike Yoder (nuWay Ag, OH):** spraying + DJI dealer + trailer manufacturing + FastPass subcontractor program + content/eBook
- **Kam Harper:** spraying + drone sales + custom trailers + game retrieval + custom farming
- **Hayden Crum (Midwest Air):** spraying + DJI dealer (only verified DJI Ag reseller in Ohio) + cover-crop spreading
- **Jonathan Cottingham (Southern Drone OPS):** spraying + DJI/ABZ dealer + training at universities and high schools + consulting
- **Brittney Truman (U-PASS):** Ag Drone Zone podcast + CropFlight compliance/logbook software
- **Taylor Moreland (Agri Spray Drones, MO):** distribution + EAVision partnership + Spray Drone End User Conference hosting

Crum on the self-cannibalization question: **"People tell us all the time that we're selling to our competition. I don't see it that way. I can't cover every acre, and I can't always hit perfect timing for every farmer."**

### Staffing: single-pilot swarm exemptions change the math

**Hylio (March 2024)** was first to get FAA approval for a single operator overseeing three swarming drones over 55 lbs (https://www.commercialuavnews.com/demand-for-swarming-agricultural-spray-drones-rising-in-us). **Rantizo's amended Exemption 18929B (July 3, 2024)** permits one operator to swarm 3 drones without a VO, at night. This reduces the economic argument for a second pilot and reshapes the year-2 staffing question. Midwest Air runs **4 crews/4 trailer setups** by 2025. **Southern Drone OPS** promoted Grayson Chambers to Chief Pilot of a second spray crew after apprenticeship — the documented internal-promotion path.

### Geographic radius

Paige Browning's family travels **up to 100 miles** from home for jobs. Cottingham is licensed in 11 states from his Arkansas base. Hayden Crum drove across Ohio during the 2024 drought from South Central hills to NW Ohio flat ground. **Delta Ag Drone Services** covers MS/AR/LA/TN as a four-state regional play.

### Contract structures

Per-acre pricing dominates. Iowa State's 2026 Custom Rate Survey added drone spraying as a separate line item for the first time — a commoditization signal. Seasonal 10–15% discounts for April-May and September-October booking stability are emerging (bhumicalculator 2026 survey). **Crop-share compensation: RESEARCH THIN** — no named US operator publicly discussed crop-share contracts in 2024–2026 sources.

## 5. Regional market differences

| Region | 2025 per-acre rate | Saturation | Key dynamics |
|---|---|---|---|
| Midwest corn/soy belt | $11–20 (Iowa State avg $11.85) | **Oversaturated** | 86% non-137 competition; $21→$13 national avg collapse driven here |
| Southeast cotton/peanut/rice | $16–28 | Mixed; demand still exceeds supply in MS Delta | Tree-lined fields favor drones; cotton defoliation timing |
| Mountain West rangeland/orchards | Project-based | **Underserved** | Weed-district contracts dominate; few commercial operators |
| California specialty crops | $15–21 commodity; **$100–300 pheromone/specialty** | Underserved but regulatory-locked | 8-step certification; SprayDays NOI system |

### Midwest — most crowded, most price-compressed

**SweetWater Technologies** (Wyanet, IL — Chad & TeNeille Gripp) grew 32,000 acres (2022) → ~200,000 acres (2025 projected) with a 25-drone fleet. Gripp: **"What we do is in demand. It's about building relationships with all these farmers and getting on those acres"** (https://www.farmprogress.com/crops/drone-spraying-gains-momentum-precision-cost-effectiveness-drive-adoption-in-agriculture). Other named businesses: **Sun Ag Inc.** (McNabb, IL, Mike Pack/DJI T40); **Strubbe Drone Services** (Jacksonville, IL, Isaac Strubbe); **Browning family** (NE); **Altitude Ag LLC** (Tom Hermann/AgriGold); **Midwest Drone Applications** (Kansas, founded 2023); **Midwest Aerial Applicators**; **AgriForce Drone Services** (central Iowa, 8 drones); **Precision Air Ag** (central Kansas). Iowa added drone spraying to its custom-rate survey for the first time in 2026 — a commoditization signal.

### Southeast — specialty-driven, longer season

**Macon Ridge Specialty Drone Service** (Wisner, LA — Kam Harper): 60,000-acre 2025 projection; Mississippi Delta herbicide focus. **Southern Drone OPS** (Jonesboro, AR — Jonathan Cottingham): 2021 founding, 11-state dealership. Auburn University (Steve Li) and Mississippi State (Crow & Tavares) lead academic research; Auburn's 2025 Singh dissertation is the first major US peanut-drone-fungicide study (https://etd.auburn.edu/handle/10415/10112). Rice applications concentrated in AR Grand Prairie, MS Delta, TX Coastal Prairie, SW LA (https://www.agaviation.org/?p=2415).

### Mountain West — underserved commercial market

**Wyoming Drone Spraying** (Lander, WY — Todd Wurth): completed first full season 2025, fleet of 4. Wurth on the niche: **"Some fields, they're small, have power lines in them or multiple obstacles, which makes it tough for crop dusters to get into — so there's a niche there"** (https://www.gillettenewsrecord.com/news/wyoming/article_d06b9c09-cafb-4e39-9dd1-4eee6043779f.html). **Agro Drone** (N. Colorado, Chris Varner), **Apex Weed Control** (CO, Sam Doughty), and **Rantizo Treasure Valley/PNW hub** cover the region. 12+ Wyoming Weed and Pest Districts deploy drones; Montana has 197,000 infested state acres at ~$60/acre management cost.

### California — regulatory fortress, highest margins

8-step certification per UC ANR: Part 107 → drone registration → 3rd-class medical → 44807 exemption → Part 137 → DPR QAC ($320) → DPR Apprentice Pilot Cert ($265) → **annual county Ag Commissioner registration in EACH county** (https://ucanr.edu/blog/salinas-valley-agriculture/article/california-agriculture-takes-flight-guide-drone-spray-pilot). AB 1016 (Newsom, 2024) streamlines drone pesticide licensure (https://thebusinessjournal.com/newsom-signs-bill-making-it-easier-to-use-pesticide-spray-drones/). **SprayDays California** (launched March 24, 2025) requires NOIs 24–48 hours in advance for restricted materials (https://www.cdpr.ca.gov/pesticide-use-in-california/spraydays/). Bill Coy (Yamaha, Fazer R): almond pheromone applications run **$100–$300/acre** depending on terrain (https://today.csuchico.edu/drones-versus-bugs-chico-state/). Named CA businesses: **Hein Ranch Co.** (Visalia, Martin Hein — also partner in FlyingAg growers' collective); **Bullseye Farms** (Woodland); **Guardian Agriculture** (Salinas Valley — first FAA-authorized eVTOL ag spray system, 60 ac/hr, 200-lb payload); **Jose Aguilar** (one of the first registered Monterey County drone spray pilots).

## 8. What the evidence says separates builders from quitters

Builders share four traits consistently documented across the named operators above:
- **Ag-roots credibility or a deliberate substitute** (chemical dealer channel partnership, co-op contract, or a retired-aviation mentor in Cottingham's case).
- **Specialization within 12 months** — Delta herbicide (Harper), Christmas tree fungicide (Beal), Ohio cover-crop seeding (Crum), rice/cotton Delta (Cottingham).
- **Second revenue line by year 2** — every successful named operator runs at least dealer sales, trailer sales, training, or software alongside application services.
- **Regulatory compliance from day one**, often via subcontracting under nuWay FastPass or a network while Part 137 is pending — rather than flying illegally and betting on not being caught.

Quitters' pattern, by contrast, is documented primarily by absence: the FAA's 1,200+ Part 137 applications in H1 2024 versus 1,710 authorized operators at end of 2025 imply hundreds of exits or stalled applications, but only one named US business closure (the Minneapolis Craigslist turn-key sale) and Rantizo's strategic divestiture are in the public record. The dominant quitter archetype is anonymous: a non-Part 137 operator who bought a DJI T40, sprayed locally at $10/acre, and either got shut down, got underbid in the 2025 $21→$13 price collapse, or simply could not cover net-60-90 receivables against spring-loaded equipment debt.
