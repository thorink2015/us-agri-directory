## 1. FIFRA: The Federal Statute Governing Pesticide Use

The Federal Insecticide, Fungicide, and Rodenticide Act (FIFRA) is the primary federal statute regulating the distribution, sale, and use of pesticides in the United States. EPA administers FIFRA and registers all pesticide products before they may be sold or distributed.[^1]

### "The label is the law"

FIFRA Section 12(a)(2)(G) (codified at 7 U.S.C. § 136j(a)(2)(G)) makes it unlawful for any person to "use any registered pesticide in a manner inconsistent with its labeling."[^2] This provision is the foundation of pesticide enforcement. Every direction, restriction, and limitation printed on a pesticide label carries the force of federal law. Worker Protection Standard (WPS) violations also constitute use inconsistent with labeling under Section 12(a)(2)(G).[^3]

### Civil penalty structure (inflation adjusted)

EPA adjusts FIFRA civil monetary penalties annually under the Federal Civil Penalties Inflation Adjustment Act. The most recent adjustment took effect January 8, 2025 (90 FR 1375), applying a 1.02598 multiplier.[^4]

| FIFRA Provision | Applies To | Maximum Per Violation |
|---|---|---|
| § 14(a)(1) / 7 U.S.C. 136l(a)(1) | Registrants, commercial applicators, wholesalers, dealers, retailers, distributors | **$24,885** |
| § 14(a)(2) / 7 U.S.C. 136l(a)(2) | Private applicators and others | **$3,650** (first provision), **$2,353** (second), **$3,650** (third) |

Penalties listed per 40 CFR § 19.4, Table 1.[^5]

### Criminal penalties

| Category | Fine | Imprisonment |
|---|---|---|
| Registrants, applicants, producers (knowing violation) | Up to **$50,000** | Up to 1 year |
| Commercial applicators of RUPs, distributors/sellers | Up to **$25,000** | Up to 1 year |
| Private applicators (knowing violation) | Up to **$1,000** | Up to 30 days |

Criminal fines may be increased under the Alternative Fines Act (18 U.S.C. § 3571).[^6]

### Penalty calculation framework

EPA calculates civil penalties using the FIFRA Enforcement Response Policy (ERP), issued December 2009. The ERP considers the gravity of the violation, the violator's good faith efforts to comply, economic benefit gained, and ability to pay. FIFRA § 14(a)(4) requires EPA to weigh the appropriateness of the penalty to business size, effect on ability to continue in business, and gravity of the violation.[^7]

## 2. Pesticide Labels and Drone Application

### EPA's position on drones and labels

EPA has not issued a standalone regulation or binding PR Notice addressing drone pesticide application as of April 2026. The agency's working position, documented through advisory committee processes, is that drone applications for products registered for manned aerial application are generally covered by existing FIFRA aerial application directions. However, this position has never been formally codified in a PR Notice or rule.[^8]

EPA stated explicitly in COVID-19 disinfectant guidance: "Unless the pesticide product label specifically includes disinfection directions for fogging, fumigation, wide-area or electrostatic spraying, or application via drones (i.e., unmanned aerial vehicles (UAV)), EPA does not recommend using these methods to apply disinfectants."[^9] This reinforces that application methods not on the label are not EPA-evaluated.

### PPDC Emerging Agricultural Technologies Workgroup (2021)

The most detailed EPA-hosted analysis of drone pesticide application is the Pesticide Program Dialogue Committee (PPDC) Emerging Agricultural Technologies Workgroup report (October 2021). Key recommendations:

- EPA OPP should focus on "establishing regulatory equivalency related to pesticide application, registration, exposure, spray drift, and residue for drone/UAV technology."
- "Commercial drone applications are currently operating under the manned aerial regulatory framework," but "UAV technology is sufficiently different to require that new data should be generated."
- The OECD Drone Sub-Group commissioned a literature review identifying data gaps in operator exposure, efficacy, crop residue, and offsite movement.[^8]

### 2024 "drone label" expansion effort

No EPA PR Notice or Federal Register notice addressing drone-specific label language was published in 2024. A search of EPA's PR Notices by year confirms no PR Notices were issued in 2024.[^10] The 2024 regulatory activity most relevant to drone operators was the AEZ reconsideration final rule (see Section 4). Any 2024 "drone label expansion" reports from industry sources cannot be verified at the four allowed domains and are treated as **unverified**.

### 2025-2026 status

In December 2025, EPA released Draft PR Notice 2026-NEW (to supersede PR Notice 98-10) covering "Notifications, Non-Notifications, and Minor Formulation Amendments." This PR Notice streamlines minor label modifications but does not add drone-specific language.[^11] No drone-specific PR Notice has been issued or proposed as of April 2026.

## 3. Restricted Use Pesticides (RUPs) and Applicator Certification

### When certification is required

FIFRA § 3(d)(1)(C) and 40 CFR Part 171 require that restricted use pesticides (RUPs) may be purchased and applied only by a certified applicator or someone working under a certified applicator's direct supervision.[^12] Noncertified applicators may apply RUPs only under the direct supervision of a certified applicator, subject to requirements in § 171.201 (minimum age 18, training, use-specific instructions).[^13]

### Aerial pest control certification category

40 CFR § 171.101 establishes 15 commercial applicator certification categories. Category (o), "Aerial pest control," covers "commercial applicators who use or supervise the use of restricted use pesticides applied by fixed or rotary wing aircraft."[^14] The federal regulation does not explicitly mention drones or UAS. Whether drones constitute "rotary wing aircraft" is an interpretive question. In practice, most states require drone applicators to hold aerial pest control certification.

Aerial-specific competencies under § 171.103(d) include knowledge of aerial equipment, drift, weather, and "recordkeeping requirements for aerial pesticide applications including application conditions if applicable."[^15]

### State interaction

Certification issued under a state's EPA-approved plan is valid only within that state's geographic area (§ 171.303(a)).[^16] States may impose requirements stricter than federal standards. Many states require all commercial applicators (not just RUP applicators) to hold certification. The 2017 Certification of Pesticide Applicators final rule (82 FR 1029, January 4, 2017) strengthened federal standards and required states to submit modified certification plans.[^17]

Approximately **1 million certified applicators** hold active credentials nationwide, with an estimated 930,000 additional noncertified applicators.[^18]

## 4. Worker Protection Standard (40 CFR Part 170)

40 CFR Part 170 protects workers and handlers from occupational pesticide exposure on agricultural establishments producing agricultural plants (farms, nurseries, greenhouses, forests).[^19]

### Re-entry intervals (REI)

Subpart E (§§ 170.401-170.411) governs worker protections. After application is complete, the treated area is subject to the label-specified REI (§ 170.407). Common REIs are 4, 12, 24, or 48 hours; some products require up to 72 hours.[^20] During the REI, workers may not enter the treated area except under limited exceptions in § 170.603 (short-term activities of 1 hour or less, not within the first 4 hours).[^21]

### Application Exclusion Zones (AEZ)

The AEZ is the area immediately surrounding pesticide application equipment during an outdoor application. Under § 170.405(a), AEZ distances are:

| Application Type | AEZ Distance |
|---|---|
| **Aerial** (including drones), air blast, air-propelled, fumigant, smoke, mist, fog | **100 feet** |
| Spray from nozzles producing smaller-than-medium droplets (per ASABE S572) | **100 feet** |
| Spray from height >12 inches using medium or larger droplets | **25 feet** |
| All other methods (ground spray ≤12 inches with medium+ droplets) | **No AEZ** |

During application, no worker or other person may enter or remain in the treated area or AEZ.[^22]

**2024 AEZ reconsideration rule.** The AEZ has a complex history. The 2015 WPS rule (80 FR 67567) established AEZs. The 2020 rule (85 FR 68781) weakened protections by limiting AEZ applicability to establishment boundaries only. That rule was stayed by court order. The 2024 reconsideration final rule (89 FR 80767, effective December 3, 2024) reinstated the stronger 2015 protections: the AEZ again extends beyond establishment boundaries, the easement exception was rescinded, and distance requirements use ASABE S572 droplet-size classification.[^23] This is the current rule as of April 2026.

### Notification requirements

Agricultural employers must notify workers of treated areas and entry restrictions via oral notification and/or posted warning signs as specified on the product label (§ 170.409). Signs must be posted before application (no earlier than 24 hours prior) and remain through the REI.[^24]

### Drone operators as handlers

Drone operators applying pesticides on agricultural establishments are "handlers" under the WPS. Handler employers must ensure operators complete handler training (§ 170.501) and comply with § 170.505(a): "no pesticide is applied so as to contact, directly or through drift, any worker or other person." Handlers must immediately suspend application if any person enters the AEZ (§ 170.505(b)).[^25] **Drone aerial applications trigger the 100-foot AEZ** under § 170.405(a)(1)(i)(A) ("Aerially").[^22]

## 5. Drift Management

### Standard drift label language

Draft PR Notice 2001-X establishes the framework for spray and dust drift label statements. It defines spray drift as "the physical movement of pesticide dust or droplets through the air at the time of application or soon after, to any site other than the area intended." The notice applies to all pesticide products labeled for outdoor use that can be applied as sprays or dusts, including aerial applications.[^26]

Agricultural products subject to WPS bear the required statement: "Do not apply this product in a way that will contact workers or other persons either directly or through drift."[^26]

EPA's introduction to pesticide drift states: "Pesticide spray drift is the movement of pesticide dust or droplets through the air at the time of application or soon after, to any site other than the area intended."[^27]

### Buffer zones

The 2024 AEZ rule (see Section 4) establishes the 100-foot AEZ for aerial applications. Separately, the Final Insecticide Strategy (April 2025) establishes ecological spray drift buffer distances as "a distance downwind from the edge of the application site," varying by application method (aerial, airblast, ground) and pesticide vulnerability level. Five additional spray drift mitigation measures were added in the final strategy to provide flexibility for aerial and airblast applications.[^28]

### Does "aerial" on a label include drones?

**EPA has not issued a definitive published interpretation.** An EPA OPP presentation (May 2019) posed this as an open challenge: "Does 'aerial application' incorporate UAVs?"[^29] The PPDC workgroup supports the working assumption that drone applications for products with manned aerial uses are covered, but this has not been formally codified.[^8]

A practical compliance problem exists: manned aerial label requirements (minimum gallons per acre, droplet size, boom length as a percentage of wingspan) were designed for conventional aircraft. Typical agricultural drones delivering 0.16 GPA with fine droplets may not be able to meet label directions written for manned aircraft applying 2+ GPA with medium droplet size.[^30] This gap remains unresolved.

**No drone-specific drift guidance, drift model (AgDRIFT), or PR Notice exists.** The PPDC workgroup noted that AgDRIFT models have not been updated to include UAS-specific vortex models, and "determination of appropriate AEZ" for UAS is an outstanding data need.[^8]

## 6. FIFRA Section 24(c): Special Local Need (SLN) Registrations

### How states authorize additional uses

FIFRA Section 24(c) allows states to register additional uses of federally registered pesticides to meet special local needs within the state. States may register a new end-use product for any use, or an additional use of a federally registered product, provided a special local need exists, necessary tolerances are in place for food/feed uses, the use has not been previously denied or canceled by EPA for health/environmental concerns, and the registration accords with FIFRA's purposes.[^31]

A "special local need" is defined in 40 CFR § 162.151 as "an existing or imminent pest problem within a State for which the State lead agency, based upon satisfactory supporting information, has determined that an appropriate federally registered pesticide product is not sufficiently available."[^32]

### Regulatory framework (40 CFR Part 162, Subpart D)

States must notify EPA within 10 working days of issuance. EPA has 90 days to disapprove or allow the registration to stand. If not disapproved, the state registration "shall be considered a federal registration, but shall authorize distribution and use only within that State" (§ 162.152(c)(1)).[^32]

### Application to drones

A state could issue an SLN registration adding drone/UAS application directions as supplemental labeling for a product that lacks such directions on its federal label. Because drone application parameters (spray height, droplet size, application rate, drift characteristics) differ from conventional aerial and ground applications, adding drone directions would likely constitute a "changed use pattern" under § 162.151, triggering the state's obligation to make an unreasonable adverse effects determination under § 162.153(c)(1)(ii).[^32]

### Documented examples

No drone-specific SLN registrations are highlighted or cataloged on EPA's website. EPA's Pesticide Product Label System (PPLS) database can be searched for individual SLN labels, but no curated list of drone-specific SLN registrations exists.[^33] AAPCO's Technology Workgroup published "Model guidance for licensing UAV pesticide applicators" (October 2023) and has conducted UAV/UAS surveys in 2020, 2022, and 2024 to track state regulatory approaches.[^34]

## 7. PRIA: Fee Category for Drone Label Amendments

### PRIA 5 overview

The Pesticide Registration Improvement Act of 2022 (PRIA 5) was enacted December 29, 2022 and is effective through September 30, 2027. PRIA 5 authorizes EPA to collect registration service fees and annual maintenance fees. A 5% fee increase took effect October 1, 2024 for FY 2025-2026.[^35]

### Which PRIA category covers adding drone/aerial application

The most relevant fee category is **R350** (EPA No., Congressional Record No. 71). R350 covers: "Amendment requiring data review in science divisions (e.g., changes to Restricted Entry Interval, or Personal Protective Equipment, or Preharvest Interval, or use rate, or number of applications; **or add aerial application**; or modify Ground Water/Surface Water advisory statement)."[^36]

Adding drone/UAS application directions to a label constitutes "adding aerial application" under R350. For biopesticides, the analogous category is B683, which explicitly includes "addition of aerial or chemigation application methods consistent with PR Notices 87-1 and 93-2."[^36]

### Fee and timeline

| Category | Action | Decision Review Period | FY 2025-2026 Fee |
|---|---|---|---|
| R350 | Amendment adding aerial application (science division review) | **9 months** | **$19,906** |
| R340 | Amendment requiring RD review only (e.g., precautionary label changes, up to 2 pests) | 4 months | $7,508 |

If EPA determines endangered species analysis is required, the R350 review period may be extended up to 50% (approximately 13.5 months total). Fee waivers of 50% and 75% are available for qualifying applicants.[^36]

## 8. Enforcement: Federal-State Framework and Drone Cases

### State lead agency system

FIFRA § 26 (7 U.S.C. § 136w-1) grants states primary enforcement responsibility for pesticide use violations when EPA determines the state has adopted adequate pesticide use laws, enforcement procedures, and recordkeeping requirements.[^37] FIFRA § 23(a)(1) authorizes cooperative agreements with states and tribes for enforcement cooperation and personnel training.[^37]

EPA partners with state and territorial pesticide agencies, regularly meeting with AAPCO and SFIREG (State FIFRA Issues Research and Evaluation Group).[^38] EPA's 10 regional offices negotiate and oversee cooperative agreements with states, territories, and tribes. The FY 2022-2025 FIFRA Cooperative Agreement Guidance identifies program areas that must be addressed in state and tribal cooperative agreements.[^39]

### AAPCO's role

The Association of American Pesticide Control Officials (AAPCO) was formed in 1947 in response to FIFRA's enactment. AAPCO represents state lead agencies (SLAs) in pesticide regulatory policy. Per AAPCO, "State lead agencies are responsible for implementing and enforcing the majority of pesticide regulations in the U.S." In 1978, EPA and AAPCO established SFIREG through a cooperative agreement that has been annually funded by EPA for over 47 years.[^40]

### Drone enforcement actions (2023-2026)

**No EPA enforcement actions specifically involving drone/UAS pesticide violations were found on epa.gov or federalregister.gov for the period 2023-2026.** No consent decrees or enforcement orders involving drone pesticide application were located on federalregister.gov. This does not mean no enforcement occurred at the state level; state lead agencies handle most use violation enforcement and their actions are not systematically published on federal websites.[^37]

## 9. Endangered Species Act (ESA) Pesticide Overlay

### Bulletins Live! Two (BLT)

Bulletins Live! Two is EPA's web-based system for distributing Endangered Species Protection Bulletins containing enforceable pesticide use limitations.[^41] Pesticide labels with ESA language direct users to the BLT website. Applicators must obtain applicable Bulletins within 6 months before or on the day of application, enter the EPA registration number and application month, and check their intended application area for Pesticide Use Limitation Areas (PULAs).[^42]

If the application area overlaps a PULA, the applicator must obtain and follow the Bulletin's restrictions. These may include restricted application methods, timing restrictions, required buffer zones, specific nozzle requirements, or prohibition of aerial application. Bulletins are enforceable as extensions of the pesticide label; failure to comply is a FIFRA misuse violation.[^41]

### How BLT applies to drone operations

Bulletins Live! Two applies to all pesticide applicators, including drone operators. There is no drone exemption or separate process. Because drone applications are generally treated as "aerial" under federal pesticide law, any PULA restriction on aerial application would apply to drones. No drone-specific BLT provisions exist.[^43]

### EPA's ESA workplan

EPA released its ESA Workplan in April 2022 ("Balancing Wildlife Protection and Responsible Pesticide Use"), with an update in November 2022. The workplan establishes multiple strategies: the Herbicide Strategy (draft), the Insecticide Strategy (final, April 2025), Rodenticide Strategy, Fungicide Strategy (under development), a Vulnerable Species Pilot Project, and a Hawaii Strategy. In December 2024, EPA released a process for creating refined maps used to develop PULAs in BLT.[^44]

The Final Insecticide Strategy (April 2025) specifies that applicators must "adhere to any spray drift requirements on pesticide labels and visit Bulletins Live Two! within 6 months of application." Implementation targets species with refined maps.[^45] The strategy does not separately address drones but its aerial spray drift buffers apply to drone operations by default.

## 10. What Changed in 2025-2026

No drone-specific EPA pesticide regulation, PR Notice, or binding guidance was published in 2025 or 2026. The following broader pesticide regulatory changes affect drone operators by default:

- **2024 AEZ reconsideration rule** (89 FR 80767, effective December 3, 2024): Reinstated 2015-era AEZ protections. The 100-foot AEZ for aerial applications (including drones) again extends beyond establishment boundaries. ASABE S572 droplet-size classification now determines AEZ distance. EPA released interim implementation guidance.[^23][^24]

- **Final Insecticide Strategy** (April 2025): Established ecological spray drift buffer distances for listed species protections. Buffers vary by application method and vulnerability level. Applies to drone operators making aerial applications in PULAs.[^45]

- **PALM tool** (August 2025): EPA released the Pesticide App for Label Mitigations, a tool for spray drift and runoff mitigation options. Applies generically to all application methods.[^46]

- **Draft PR Notice 2026-NEW** (December 2025): Proposed update to PR Notice 98-10 covering notifications, non-notifications, and minor formulation amendments. Streamlines minor label modifications but does not add drone-specific language. Comment period extended to March 23, 2026.[^11]

- **2026 Pesticide General Permit (PGP)** (finalized December 10, 2024; effective October 31, 2026): New NPDES permit covering mosquito control, weed/algae control, animal pest control, and forest canopy pest control. Drone operators applying pesticides to or near waters of the U.S. must evaluate PGP applicability.[^47]

- **Executive Order 14307, "Unleashing American Drone Dominance"** (June 6, 2025): Promotes the domestic drone industry broadly, addresses supply chain security, and promotes American-made drone exports. Does not address pesticide application specifically.[^48]

- **Penalty inflation adjustment** (effective January 8, 2025): FIFRA civil penalties increased by 2.598%. Maximum per-violation penalty for commercial applicators now $24,885.[^4]

- **EPA registration process optimization** (October 2025): EPA released reports on optimizing pesticide registration processes as part of Administrator Zeldin's "Powering the Great American Comeback" initiative.[^49]

No court rulings specifically affecting drone pesticide application were identified on epa.gov or federalregister.gov for 2025-2026.

## 11. Common Operator Misconceptions About EPA Rules

**Misconception 1: "FAA Part 137 is enough."**
An FAA agricultural aircraft operator certificate (Part 137) authorizes the aircraft operation. It does not satisfy EPA pesticide requirements. FIFRA Section 12(a)(2)(G) independently makes it unlawful to use any pesticide inconsistent with its labeling.[^2] WPS handler requirements (40 CFR 170), applicator certification requirements (40 CFR 171), and ESA Bulletins Live! Two obligations apply regardless of FAA status.[^22][^14][^41] A drone operator needs both FAA authorization and EPA/state pesticide compliance.

**Misconception 2: "Drones aren't aerial application."**
EPA's PPDC workgroup confirmed that "commercial drone applications are currently operating under the manned aerial regulatory framework."[^8] Under 40 CFR § 170.405, aerial applications trigger the 100-foot AEZ.[^22] The FAA requires drone spraying operations to obtain a Part 137 operating certificate, the same certificate required for manned aerial applicators. Treating drone application as something other than aerial application does not eliminate compliance obligations; it may increase them by removing the applicability of existing aerial label directions.

**Misconception 3: "The label doesn't apply to small acreage."**
FIFRA Section 12(a)(2)(G) applies to "any person" using "any registered pesticide" regardless of acreage. There is no small-acreage exemption in FIFRA or 40 CFR Part 170.[^2][^19] The WPS applies to any agricultural establishment producing agricultural plants, and the label is the law on 1 acre or 10,000 acres.

**Misconception 4: "I can use any pesticide I want as a Part 137 operator."**
Only pesticides with label directions permitting the intended use, application method, site, and rate may be applied. Restricted Use Pesticides require a certified applicator or direct supervision by one (40 CFR Part 171).[^12] Products without aerial application directions on the label may not legally be applied by any aircraft, including drones, unless a Section 24(c) SLN registration or other authorization permits the use.[^31] ESA Bulletins may further restrict or prohibit specific products in specific locations.[^41]

**Misconception 5: "EPA doesn't enforce against drone operators."**
While no federal drone-specific enforcement actions were found for 2023-2026, this reflects the enforcement model: states hold primary enforcement responsibility under FIFRA § 26.[^37] State lead agencies conduct inspections and pursue enforcement actions that may not appear on federal databases. EPA retains authority to bring federal enforcement, with civil penalties up to $24,885 per violation for commercial applicators.[^5]
