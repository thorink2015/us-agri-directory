**Key data gaps:** Alaska has zero identified operators, dealers, or training programs for ag drone spraying. Hawaii has only 2 confirmed spray operators. Several national dealers ship to all 50 states but lack brick-and-mortar West Coast locations. Tribal ag drone programs remain largely undeveloped, with Colville Confederated Tribes and Yakama Nation using drone technology primarily for forestry, not spray application.

## Regulatory notes by state

### California

**Governing body:** California Department of Pesticide Regulation (DPR), cdpr.ca.gov, (916) 445-4000.

**Key law:** AB 527 (signed Oct 2023 by Gov. Newsom) created a streamlined "unmanned pest control aircraft pilot certificate" pathway with Journeyman, Apprentice, Vector Control Technician, and Private Applicator designations. Private applicators (farmers on their own land) can now obtain certificates through a DPR-accredited program without the full commercial exam sequence. This cannot be used for spraying for hire.

**Licensing process:** FAA Part 107, then FAA Part 137, then DPR Qualified Applicator Certificate (QAC, requires Laws/Regs and Plant Agriculture exams, $320), then DPR Unmanned Apprentice Pilot Certificate ($265 application, $115 per exam, 70% minimum score). County registration with the local Agricultural Commissioner is also required.

**Buffer and drift:** California has among the strictest pesticide drift regulations in the US. Product label requirements apply; county Agricultural Commissioners enforce additional local restrictions. DPR requires annual use reporting for all pesticide applications including drone-applied products.

**Market status:** Most developed regulatory framework among the five states. Post-AB 527, operator entry is accelerating. Guardian Agriculture and Wilbur-Ellis are operating at commercial scale in Salinas Valley. Pyka is manufacturing in Oakland. UC Davis leads spray research.

### Oregon

**Governing body:** Oregon Department of Agriculture (ODA), Pesticides Division, 503-986-4635, pesticide-expert@oda.oregon.gov.

**Critical rule:** It is a violation of Oregon law to apply any pesticide by aircraft (including drones) without an Aerial Pesticide Applicator (APA) license. An unlicensed person cannot spray pesticides by drone even if supervised by a licensed APA.

**Licensing process:** Obtain base license (CPA, PPA, NPA, or PAL), then complete **50 hours of flight experience** before pursuing APA license (experience can be gained spraying water, fertilizer, or fire retardant; other states' experience counts with documentation). Then pass Oregon Aerial Applicator's exam. Must also hold FAA Part 107, Part 137, and applicable waivers. APA license requires 10 aerial continuing education credits per certification period (up to 5 years). Insurance identifying specific aircraft is required for commercial operators.

**Local restrictions:** Community opposition to aerial drone spraying exists in some coastal areas (Lincoln County). Forestry drone spraying near Yachats generated protests. ODA confirmed operators were in compliance but local measures may be proposed in some jurisdictions.

**Infrastructure:** Pendleton UAS Test Range provides 14,000 sq mi of FAA-approved airspace for testing. Pan-Pacific UAS Test Range Complex spans Pendleton, McMinnville, and Tillamook.

### Washington

**Governing body:** Washington State Department of Agriculture (WSDA), agr.wa.gov, drone guidance page at agr.wa.gov/departments/pesticides-and-fertilizers/pesticides/compliance/drone-guidance. Contact: license@agr.wa.gov.

**Requirements:** Operators must follow all FAA requirements (Part 107, registration, insurance, waivers, Part 137 AAOC). Additionally, WSDA pesticide applicator licensing is required. License types include Private Applicator ($45/year for RUPs on own land), Commercial Applicator (for hire, requires WA Laws and Safety exam plus category exams), and Limited Private Applicator (eastern WA only). Recertification every five years for all types. No additional state-specific drone buffer zone rules beyond federal and label requirements were identified.

**Market status:** Emerging market. Three primary confirmed spray operators (Ag Drones Northwest, Altitude Agri Services, Airoterra). Tree fruit sector is key growth driver; supplemental labeling for drone application on many products is still pending.

### Hawaii

**Governing body:** Hawaii Department of Agriculture, Pesticides Branch, (808) 973-9402, pest@hdoa.hawaii.gov.

**Aerial pesticide rules:** Applications of restricted-use pesticides by aircraft (including drones) require a DAB permit (Form P-23). Act 45 (2018) prohibits applying RUPs within 100 feet of school property during school hours. HB1285 (2025) proposes expanding buffer zones to half a mile around schools and public parks. EPA is developing a Hawaii-specific Endangered Species Act strategy with 50 to 320 foot buffer distances depending on species and pesticide risk.

**No reciprocal licensing:** Hawaii does not honor pesticide licenses from any other state. Operators must obtain Hawaii-specific certification.

**Environmental sensitivity:** Unique endemic species, restricted watersheds, coral reef protection, and proximity of agricultural lands to conservation areas all constrain aerial application practices. DLNR requires permits for drone flights in state parks, natural area reserves, and forest reserves.

**Market status:** Only 2 confirmed spray operators (Aloha Aina Drones in Hilo, Agricultural Spraying Solutions Hawaii on Maui). No DJI Agras authorized dealer is physically located in state. Major opportunity for coffee, macadamia, and invasive species management.

### Alaska

**Governing body:** Alaska Department of Environmental Conservation (DEC), Pesticide Control Program, dec.alaska.gov/eh/pest, (907) 269-1099. Contact: Diedra Eicher, diedra.eicher@alaska.gov.

**Pesticide rules:** Alaska requires pesticide applicator certification. A 35-foot pesticide-free zone around water bodies applies with secondary buffer zones for aerial application. Public notification is required before all aerial spraying. No reciprocal licensing with any other state.

**Operational challenges:** Short growing season (roughly May to September, shorter in interior); extreme daylight variation (midnight sun advantage in summer); remote agricultural areas; limited road access; vast distances between fields; hundreds of bush planes operating at low altitudes (under 500 ft AGL) near uncontrolled airstrips present significant safety considerations.

**Wildlife restrictions:** Drones cannot be used to locate salmon for commercial fishing or to locate game while hunting (3-hour cooling period after drone use).

**Market status:** Zero confirmed ag drone spraying operators. Market is entirely unserved. Potential applications include mosquito control in rural and bush communities, invasive species management (elodea in waterways), wildfire fuel reduction treatment, and post-fire reseeding. Mat-Su Valley and Delta Junction barley and potato operations could benefit from drone application services.

### Tribal considerations (all five states)

No specific tribal drone spraying regulations were found. Tribal sovereignty means federal, state, and tribal rules may all apply on tribal lands. Operators must coordinate with specific tribal governments for any operations on reservations. **Colville Confederated Tribes** (WA) have an established partnership with M3 Agriculture Technologies for drone-based sterile insect release in orchards. **Yakama Nation** (WA) is integrating drones and LiDAR into forestry management but has not adopted spray applications. No confirmed ag drone programs were found for Yurok Tribe, Hoopa Valley Tribe, Agua Caliente Band, or Native Hawaiian homestead programs. Relevant federally recognized tribes by state: CA (109+), OR (9), WA (29), AK (229), HI (Native Hawaiian organizations).

## Methodology and data gaps

Research relied on web search across company websites, FAA databases, state regulatory sites, LinkedIn, Google Maps business listings, ag industry publications (AgAir Update, Good Fruit Grower, Western Farm Press, Capital Press), university extension programs, drone manufacturer dealer locators, and industry network maps (Rantizo AcreConnect, nuWay Ag pilot map). Facebook groups, YouTube channels, and specialty crop association sites were also queried.

**Key gaps in this batch:**
- **FAA Part 137 UAS certificate holder lists** are not publicly accessible in bulk; individual certificates were confirmed only where operators self-report or news sources confirm
- **California DPR Unmanned Pilot Certificate records** are not searchable online; the DPR pilot registry would reveal additional operators not found through web search
- **Oregon ODA Aerial Pesticide Applicator license database** is not publicly searchable online
- **WSDA applicator license search** exists but was not systematically queried for all drone operators
- **Small or new operators** without websites, social media, or news coverage are likely missing, especially in California's Central Valley where post-AB 527 entry is accelerating
- **Social media profiles** were sparse for most small operations; Instagram, Facebook, and X/Twitter profiles were only identified for a minority of entries
- **Pricing data** was not systematically collected (Rantizo charges ~$8-12/acre; nuWay reports $14-17/acre in Midwest; Parabug charges per-acre for biocontrol)
- **Hawaii and Alaska** are likely underrepresented due to limited digital presence of any operators in these states
- **Tribal ag drone programs** remain largely undocumented online; direct outreach to tribal agricultural departments would be needed for more complete coverage

This concludes Batch 5, the final regional batch. All 50 states plus DC are now covered across Batches 1 through 5.
