## Section 44807 exemption pathway and its relationship to Part 137

> **Common misconception:** Section 44807 is sometimes described as an "alternative" to Part 137. This is incorrect. Section 44807 is an exemption pathway from airworthiness and other operating requirements that *enables* heavy UAS (55 lb or more) to fly. It does not replace Part 137. Agricultural dispensing operations still require a Part 137 Agricultural Aircraft Operator Certificate (AAOC) regardless of the exemption pathway used. [^50] [^51]

### What Section 44807 actually does

49 U.S.C. § 44807, titled "Special Authority for Certain Unmanned Aircraft Systems," grants the FAA Administrator authority to use a risk-based approach to determine whether certain UAS may operate safely in the National Airspace System without meeting all standard certification requirements. [^52] The statute was originally enacted as Section 333 of the FAA Modernization and Reform Act of 2012 (Pub. L. 112-95), then recodified as § 44807 by the FAA Reauthorization Act of 2018 (Pub. L. 115-254, § 347(a)). [^52] Its sunset date was most recently extended to **September 30, 2033** by the FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 (Pub. L. 118-63, § 927(a)). [^52]

The statute's core function is to set aside the statutory prohibition on operating aircraft without airworthiness certificates. Without § 44807, the FAA could not grant exemptions from that requirement, because airworthiness certification is a statutory mandate rather than a regulatory one. [^53] The statute also authorizes the FAA to waive applicable parts of 14 CFR without initiating a formal rulemaking, to the extent consistent with aviation safety (added by Pub. L. 118-63, § 927(b)(4)). [^52]

### When Section 44807 is required

Section 44807 is used for any civil UAS operation that cannot be conducted under Part 107. The two primary triggers are:

1. **Weight:** The UAS weighs 55 lb or more at takeoff (including payload). Part 107 applies only to small UAS under 55 lb. [^54]
2. **Non-waivable rules:** The operation requires relief from a Part 107 provision that is not listed in § 107.205 (the list of waivable rules). For example, § 107.36 (carriage of hazardous material) is not waivable, so even a sub-55 lb spraying operation could theoretically use a Part 11 exemption, though in practice the FAA has established a streamlined process for sub-55 lb ag operations that uses a Part 11 exemption specifically for § 107.36. [^55] [^56]

### Which operators use Section 44807 and why

Agricultural spraying operations comprise the vast majority of § 44807 exemptions for UAS above 55 lb. The FAA reported **over 1,700 operator certificates** issued for agricultural operations as of the publication of its BVLOS NPRM in August 2025. [^57]

The FAA maintains a publicly available List of Approved Agricultural UAS under Section 44807 at regulatory docket FAA-2023-1271. [^58] Approved aircraft include:

| Manufacturer | Notable Models | Approved MTOW Range |
|---|---|---|
| DJI | Agras T16, T20, T20P, T25, T30, T40, T50 | 92.6 to 227.07 lb |
| XAG | P40, V40, P60, P100, P100 PRO, P150 | 105.8 to 282.19 lb |
| Hylio | AG-116/216, AG-122, AG-130/230, AG-272, HYL-150, HYL-300 | 105 to 960 lb |

Operators seeking BVLOS at scale, multi-UAS (swarm) operations, or night operations beyond what Part 107 waivers cover also use § 44807 petitions. [^53]

### The petition process

All § 44807 petitions are filed on the **shell docket FAA-2007-0001-0001** via the regulations.gov public portal. [^59] Per 14 CFR § 11.63(d), petitions must be submitted **at least 120 days** before the exemption is needed, or 120 days before a current exemption expires if renewal is sought. [^50]

The petition must contain: [^54]

- Concept of Operations
- Operations Manual
- Emergency Procedures
- Checklists
- Maintenance Manual
- Training Program
- Flight History (hours, cycles, accidents)
- Safety Risk Analysis (required for complex operations such as BVLOS, multi-UAS, flight over people, or operations from a moving vehicle)

The FAA review process proceeds as follows: [^53]

1. Petition filed on the shell docket; a unique docket number is assigned.
2. FAA safety team assesses the risk of the proposed operation to the NAS and to persons and property on the ground.
3. For precedent-setting grants, a Federal Register notice is published with a 30-day public comment period.
4. If data supports safe operation, the FAA issues a grant of exemption.
5. The decision is communicated via email from 9-AVS-ARM320-Exemptions@FAA.gov and placed in the public docket per 14 CFR § 11.91.

After receiving the exemption grant, the FAA issues a **blanket COA** for flights at or below 400 feet in Class G airspace to all § 44807 exemption holders, as appropriate. [^54] The FAA also sends Requests for Information (RFI) seeking basic operational data to verify that exemption holders understand their obligations. Failure to respond results in petition closure. [^50]

### Validity period and renewal

The blanket COA associated with a § 44807 exemption is **valid for two years** from the issuance of the grant. [^60] Renewal petitions must be submitted at least 120 days before expiration. [^50] Per Pub. L. 118-63, § 927(c)(2), exemptions in effect at the expiration of § 44807 authority continue for **three years** after the termination date described in the exemption, provided the FAA does not determine a safety risk. [^52]

Exemptions for operations that can now be conducted under Part 107 will not be renewed; the FAA directs those operators to the Part 107 framework instead. [^50]

### Differences between Section 44807 exemptions and Part 107 waivers

| Feature | Part 107 Waiver (§ 107.200) | Section 44807 Exemption |
|---|---|---|
| Legal basis | 14 CFR Part 107, Subpart E | 49 U.S.C. § 44807 + 14 CFR Part 11 |
| UAS weight | Under 55 lb only | No upper weight limit |
| Scope of relief | Only rules listed in § 107.205 | Can exempt from Parts 61, 91, 137, and airworthiness requirements |
| Hazardous material (§ 107.36) | Not waivable under § 107.205 | Can be addressed in exemption package |
| Airworthiness | Part 107 waives airworthiness for sUAS by default | § 44807 provides statutory authority to waive airworthiness for larger UAS |
| Registration | FA-number via DroneZone | N-number via CARES for UAS 55 lb or more |
| Application portal | FAA DroneZone | regulations.gov (docket FAA-2007-0001) |

Source: FAA Section 44807 guidance page [^54]; AIM § 11-4-5 [^55].

The key distinction: Part 107 waivers can only waive the specific rules enumerated in § 107.205. Section 107.36 (carriage of hazardous material) is not on that list, so **a Part 107 waiver alone cannot authorize agricultural spraying**. [^56] An exemption under 14 CFR Part 11, using the statutory authority of § 44807 for heavy UAS, is required instead.

### Connection to Part 137

Even with a § 44807 exemption in hand, the **AAOC requirement under Part 137 remains independently mandatory**. [^50] [^51] The FAA's Aeronautical Information Manual states: "Civil agricultural spraying operations will also require a 14 CFR part 137 certificate." [^61]

The § 44807 grant typically includes exemption from specific Part 137 sections that do not work for unmanned aircraft (such as the manned-pilot certificate requirements in § 137.19(c), the shoulder harness requirement in § 137.31(b), and the safety belt requirement in § 137.42). [^62] But the core obligation to hold an AAOC under § 137.11 is not exempted.

The FAA process requires that the exemption be granted *before* the AAOC application: "You must complete the exemption process and receive your exemption number before applying for an Agricultural Aircraft Operator Certificate (AAOC)." [^50]

### Typical conditions and limitations in a Section 44807 grant

Based on the FAA's blanket COA template and published exemption grants: [^60] [^62]

- Operations conducted under VMC only; special VFR is not authorized.
- PIC must maintain visual line of sight unless BVLOS is specifically authorized.
- Monthly reporting is required, including negative/zero flight reports.
- NOTAM is required for UAS over 55 lb or capable of speeds over 100 mph.
- PIC may not operate multiple UA simultaneously unless specifically authorized.
- Coordination with military training routes (MTRs) and special use airspace (SUAs) required 24 hours in advance.
- Emergency/contingency procedures must include lost-link and lost-GPS protocols.
- The PIC must hold a current Remote Pilot Certificate.
- All operations must be documented regardless of airspace class.

### Typical regulations exempted for agricultural UAS (55 lb or more)

A combined § 44807 and Part 137 petition for a heavy agricultural drone typically requests exemption from: [^62]

- **Part 61:** §§ 61.3(a)(1)(i), 61.3(c)(1), 61.23(a)(2) (manned pilot certificate and medical requirements)
- **Part 91:** §§ 91.7(a), 91.119(c), 91.121, 91.151(b), 91.403(b), 91.405(a), 91.407(a)(1), 91.409(a)(1)-(2), 91.417(a)-(b) (airworthiness, maintenance, and flight rule provisions designed for manned aircraft)
- **Part 137:** §§ 137.19(c), 137.19(d), 137.19(e)(2)(ii), (iii), (v), 137.31(a), 137.31(b), 137.33(a), 137.33(b), 137.41(c), 137.42 (manned-pilot, airworthiness, and physical-presence provisions)

## How Part 137, originally written for crewed aircraft, is interpreted for UAS

### A 1965 regulation meets 2020s technology

14 CFR Part 137 was first published on June 24, 1965 (30 FR 8106), with an effective date of January 1, 1966. [^63] It was the first Federal Aviation Regulation developed specifically for agricultural aircraft operations. Prior to its adoption, agricultural flying was governed by certificates of waiver under Civil Air Regulations Parts 60 and 8. [^64]

Part 137 has been amended multiple times since 1965, but **none of those amendments addressed unmanned aircraft**. [^63] Every accommodation for UAS has been achieved through exemptions, notices, and guidance documents rather than changes to the regulatory text itself.

The FAA first issued a Part 137 Agricultural Aircraft Operator Certificate to a UAS operator on **November 20, 2015**. [^65] By March 2023, there were 122 certificated Part 137 UAS operators. [^65] By mid-2025, that number had grown to over 1,700. [^57]

### The interpretive challenge

Part 137 assumes throughout its text that a pilot is physically inside the aircraft. Multiple provisions reference in-cockpit equipment, manned-aircraft pilot certificates, and actions that only a person aboard the aircraft could perform. The FAA acknowledged this directly in AC 137-1B: "The operator of a UAS either cannot comply with several sections in part 137, or those requirements are not applicable to UAS operations." [^66]

### Specific provisions that do not work for UAS

The following Part 137 sections contain language that assumes a manned aircraft. UAS operators must receive exemption from each before certification can proceed. [^62] [^65] [^66]

**§ 137.19(b) and (c), pilot certificate requirements.** These subsections require the applicant (private operator) or the applicant's pilot (commercial operator) to hold "a current U.S. private, commercial, or airline transport pilot certificate" and be "properly rated for the aircraft to be used." [^63] UAS operators hold a Remote Pilot Certificate under Part 107, which is not a pilot certificate under Part 61. Exemption is required to substitute the Remote Pilot Certificate. [^65]

**§ 137.19(d), aircraft requirements.** This subsection requires "at least one certificated and airworthy aircraft, equipped for agricultural operation." [^63] Most UAS lack type certificates and standard airworthiness certificates. The § 44807 exemption provides relief from the airworthiness requirement; the Part 137 exemption provides relief from § 137.19(d) specifically. [^62]

**§ 137.19(e)(2), knowledge and skill test requirements.** The skill test items include maneuvers specific to manned fixed-wing or rotary-wing aircraft: [^63]

- § 137.19(e)(2)(ii): "Approaches to the working area" (as performed by a manned aircraft)
- § 137.19(e)(2)(iii): "Flare-outs" (a manned landing technique)
- § 137.19(e)(2)(v): "Pullups and turnarounds" (manned aircraft maneuvering at field edges)

These maneuvers are either inapplicable to multirotor UAS or are performed entirely differently by autonomous flight controllers. Exemption is required for these specific items. [^62]

**§ 137.31(a), aircraft meeting § 137.19(d).** Requires the aircraft to meet the certificated-and-airworthy standard. Same airworthiness issue as § 137.19(d). [^63]

**§ 137.31(b), shoulder harness.** Requires "a suitable and properly installed shoulder harness for use by each pilot." [^63] There is no pilot aboard an unmanned aircraft.

**§ 137.33(a), carrying of certificate.** Requires "a facsimile of the agricultural aircraft operator certificate" to be "carried on that aircraft." [^63] Small drones have no cockpit or document storage.

**§ 137.33(b), registration and airworthiness certificates.** References registration and airworthiness certificates that "need not be carried in the aircraft" but must be "kept available for inspection at the base." [^63] The airworthiness certificate reference does not apply to exempted UAS.

**§ 137.41(c), pilot in command requirements.** Requires the PIC to hold a pilot certificate as referenced in § 137.19(b) or (c) and to demonstrate knowledge and skill per § 137.19(e). [^63] The Remote Pilot Certificate substitution and modified skill test require exemption.

**§ 137.42, fastening of safety belts and shoulder harnesses.** Requires that "no person may operate an aircraft in operations required to be conducted under part 137 without a safety belt and shoulder harness properly secured about that person." [^63] This is impossible to comply with when the pilot is on the ground.

### The FAA's adaptive approach

The FAA has addressed the manned-aircraft assumptions in Part 137 through a series of guidance documents rather than regulatory amendments.

**Advisory Circular AC 137-1B** (August 21, 2017) was the first formal FAA guidance to explicitly include UAS within Part 137's scope. It states: "This AC also applies to agricultural aircraft operations using an Unmanned Aircraft System (UAS)." It further directs that "a part 137 operator who wishes to use a UAS should have [an] exemption with relief of the appropriate sections of part 137 before the certification process reaches the Demonstration and Inspection Phase." [^66]

**FAA Notice 8900.659** (June 13, 2023) established the first streamlined UAS-specific Part 137 certification process. [^65] It was succeeded by **N 8900.704** (June 10, 2024) and then by **N 8900.741** (June 9, 2025), which is the current active guidance. [^67] [^68] Each successive notice refined the process. N 8900.741 is titled "Title 14 CFR Part 137 Unmanned Aircraft Systems (UAS) Certification" and is maintained by the Emerging Technologies Division (AFS-700). [^68]

The notices state: "The FAA has determined that 14 CFR part 137 UAS agricultural aircraft operations present a lower risk than other certificated operations and revising the 14 CFR part 137 certification process would not adversely affect safety." [^65] [^68]

### The centralized UAS Part 137 certification process

The FAA established a centralized **137 UAS Operations Office** to handle all UAS-only Part 137 certifications, replacing the previous system where local Flight Standards District Offices (FSDOs) handled them individually. [^65] The contact email is **UAS137Certificates@faa.gov**. [^50]

The Emerging Technologies Division (AFS-700), established on September 24, 2023, oversees this office. [^69] AFS-700 handles certification, certificate management, oversight, and surveillance for Part 137 UAS-only certificate holders (as well as Part 135 UAS cargo/delivery operations). [^69] For operators holding both manned and UAS Part 137 certificates, the local FSDO remains responsible. [^69]

Key differences between the UAS and manned Part 137 certification processes: [^65]

| Feature | Manned Part 137 | UAS-Only Part 137 |
|---|---|---|
| Responsible office | Local FSDO | Centralized UAS 137 Operations Office (AFS-700) |
| Application submission | FAA Form 8710-3 to FSDO | FAA Form 8710-3 + exemption number emailed to UAS137Certificates@faa.gov |
| Pilot certificate | Commercial or ATP (Part 61) | Remote Pilot Certificate (Part 107) |
| Medical certificate | Second-class | Third-class (or BasicMed equivalent) |
| Knowledge and skill tests | FAA-administered | Self-administered |
| Surveillance | Annual 20% sampling per Order 1800.56 | Not required for UAS-only certificate holders |
| Operations manual | Not required by Part 137 text | Required as exemption condition |
| Training program | Not required by Part 137 text | Required as exemption condition |

### Certification pathway by UAS weight class

**For UAS under 55 lb:** [^50] [^65]

1. Obtain Remote Pilot Certificate (Part 107).
2. Register UAS (FA-number via FAADroneZone).
3. Petition for exemption from § 107.36 and applicable Part 137 sections via 14 CFR Part 11 on regulations.gov.
4. After exemption is granted, submit FAA Form 8710-3 and exemption number to UAS137Certificates@faa.gov.
5. Receive AAOC and blanket COA for Class G airspace at or below 400 ft AGL.

**For UAS 55 lb or more:** [^50] [^54]

1. Obtain Remote Pilot Certificate (Part 107).
2. Register UAS with N-number via the Civil Aviation Registry (CARES) or by mail.
3. Verify that the UAS model appears on the FAA's List of Approved Agricultural UAS (docket FAA-2023-1271). If not, the review may take longer. [^50]
4. Petition for § 44807 exemption covering Parts 61, 91, and 137 sections on docket FAA-2007-0001.
5. After exemption is granted, submit FAA Form 8710-3 and exemption number to UAS137Certificates@faa.gov.
6. Receive AAOC and blanket COA.

### The combined exemption package for heavy agricultural UAS

A single petition for a heavy ag drone (55 lb or more) typically bundles all needed regulatory relief into one filing: the § 44807 authority covers the airworthiness exemption, while the 14 CFR Part 11 petition process within that filing covers the specific Part 61, Part 91, and Part 137 section exemptions. [^62] [^53] The FAA reviews the entire package as one petition. AC 137-1B requires that the exemption grant be in hand before the Part 137 certification process can advance to its Demonstration and Inspection Phase. [^66]

## The relationship between Part 107, Part 137, and the Remote Pilot Certificate

> **Common misconception:** The Remote Pilot Certificate is sometimes described as "optional" for agricultural spraying. This is incorrect. **The Remote Pilot Certificate is required for all civil UAS agricultural operations.** For drones under 55 lb, it is mandated by 14 CFR § 107.12. For drones 55 lb or more, it is required as a condition of the § 44807 exemption grant. The FAA's enforcement notice on its Dispensing Chemicals page explicitly lists "obtaining a Remote Pilot Certificate" as a basic compliance requirement. [^50] The NAAA states: "Every commercial drone pilot must hold this certificate." [^70]

### The layered regulatory framework

No single certificate, exemption, or rule covers all requirements for agricultural UAS spraying. The framework is layered, with each layer addressing a different aspect of the operation:

**14 CFR Part 107 (Small Unmanned Aircraft Systems).** The operating rules for civil UAS under 55 lb. Establishes the Remote Pilot Certificate requirement (§ 107.12), operating limitations, and the waiver framework. Does not, by itself, authorize dispensing of hazardous materials. [^71]

**14 CFR Part 137 (Agricultural Aircraft Operations).** The rules governing dispensing of economic poisons and other agricultural substances from any aircraft, regardless of weight or whether it is manned. Requires an Agricultural Aircraft Operator Certificate (AAOC). Applies to all agricultural UAS operations. [^63] [^72]

**49 U.S.C. § 44807 (Special Authority for Certain Unmanned Aircraft Systems).** The statutory authorization pathway for UAS that do not fit under Part 107, primarily those weighing 55 lb or more. Provides authority to exempt operators from airworthiness and other regulatory requirements on a case-by-case basis. [^52] [^54]

**14 CFR Part 91 (General Operating and Flight Rules).** The general operating rules that apply to UAS 55 lb or more. These UAS operate under Part 91 (not Part 107), with specific sections exempted via the § 44807 process. [^50] [^73]

**14 CFR Part 61 (Certification: Pilots, Flight Instructors, and Ground Instructors).** The airman certification rules that technically apply to UAS 55 lb or more (since they operate under Part 91). Specific Part 61 sections (such as pilot certificate and medical certificate requirements designed for manned aircraft) are exempted via the § 44807 process. [^62] [^74]

### What each operator needs, by weight class

**Drones under 55 lb (total weight at takeoff, including payload):** [^50]

| Requirement | Regulatory Basis | How Obtained |
|---|---|---|
| Remote Pilot Certificate | 14 CFR § 107.12 | FAA knowledge test |
| UAS registration (FA-number) | 14 CFR § 107.13 | FAADroneZone |
| Exemption from § 107.36 | 14 CFR Part 11 petition | regulations.gov |
| Exemptions from Part 137 sections | 14 CFR Part 11 petition | regulations.gov (same petition) |
| Agricultural Aircraft Operator Certificate | 14 CFR § 137.11 | FAA Form 8710-3 to UAS137Certificates@faa.gov |
| Blanket COA | ATO issuance | Automatic with exemption grant |

**Drones 55 lb or more (total weight at takeoff, including payload):** [^50] [^54]

| Requirement | Regulatory Basis | How Obtained |
|---|---|---|
| Remote Pilot Certificate | Condition of § 44807 exemption | FAA knowledge test |
| UAS registration (N-number) | 14 CFR Part 47 | CARES or mail |
| Section 44807 exemption (covering Parts 61, 91, 137) | 49 U.S.C. § 44807 + 14 CFR Part 11 | Petition on docket FAA-2007-0001 |
| Agricultural Aircraft Operator Certificate | 14 CFR § 137.11 | FAA Form 8710-3 to UAS137Certificates@faa.gov |
| Blanket COA | ATO issuance | Issued with exemption grant |

### Why the Remote Pilot Certificate is the foundation, even for heavy drones

For UAS under 55 lb, the Remote Pilot Certificate is a statutory requirement under § 107.12: "No person may act as a remote pilot in command unless that person has a remote pilot certificate with a small UAS rating." [^71]

For UAS 55 lb or more, Part 107 does not technically apply (the UAS is too heavy). These aircraft operate under Part 91, and the applicable pilot certification rules are in Part 61, which requires manned-aircraft pilot certificates. The § 44807 exemption provides relief from those Part 61 requirements but **substitutes the Remote Pilot Certificate as the baseline competency demonstration**. [^65] [^50]

The FAA's rationale is practical: the Remote Pilot Certificate knowledge test covers airspace classification, weather minimums, radio communication, aeronautical decision-making, and regulatory compliance. These topics apply equally to manned and unmanned operations. The FAA accepts the Remote Pilot Certificate for Part 137 UAS operations in lieu of the Commercial Pilot Certificate that Part 137 normally requires. [^65]

### Common confusion points

**"Part 107 alone covers spraying."** Incorrect. Part 107 establishes the operating rules and pilot certificate for small UAS, but § 107.36 prohibits carriage of hazardous material (which includes pesticides and herbicides). Section 107.36 is **not on the list of waivable provisions** in § 107.205. [^56] An exemption (not a waiver) under 14 CFR Part 11 is required to conduct spraying, and Part 137 certification is required separately. [^50]

**"Part 137 alone covers spraying."** Incorrect. Part 137 governs the agricultural dispensing operation itself, but the operator also needs either Part 107 compliance (for UAS under 55 lb) or a § 44807 exemption (for UAS 55 lb or more) to legally operate the UAS. Part 137 does not authorize flight; it authorizes dispensing. [^61] [^50]

**"A Part 107 waiver is the same as a Section 44807 exemption."** Incorrect. A Part 107 waiver can only waive provisions listed in § 107.205. A § 44807 exemption can provide relief from Parts 61, 91, 137, and airworthiness requirements. They are different legal instruments with different scopes, filed through different processes. [^54] [^56]

**"One certificate covers everything."** Incorrect. At minimum, a sub-55 lb agricultural UAS operator needs three separate authorizations: the Remote Pilot Certificate, the exemption from § 107.36 and Part 137 sections, and the AAOC. A 55 lb or heavier operator needs: the Remote Pilot Certificate, the § 44807 exemption, and the AAOC. [^50]

**"The Remote Pilot Certificate is not needed for heavy drones."** Incorrect. Although Part 107 does not apply to UAS 55 lb or more, the FAA requires the Remote Pilot Certificate as a condition of every § 44807 agricultural exemption grant. The FAA enforcement notice explicitly identifies "obtaining a Remote Pilot Certificate" as a basic requirement that some operators have failed to meet. [^50] The NAAA warns that "employing an operator without the proper 'airman's certificate' can lead to federal criminal implications including potential fines, up to three years of imprisonment, or both (49 USC 46306(b)(8))." [^70]

### Decision tree for agricultural UAS operators

The following decision logic applies as of the current regulatory framework:

1. **Will the UAS dispense any substance (pesticide, herbicide, fertilizer, seed, etc.) from the air?**
   - If yes, Part 137 AAOC is required regardless of UAS weight. [^72]
   - If no, Part 137 does not apply.

2. **Does the UAS weigh under 55 lb at takeoff (including payload)?**
   - If yes: operate under Part 107. Obtain Remote Pilot Certificate (§ 107.12), register via DroneZone, petition for exemption from § 107.36 and applicable Part 137 sections, then apply for AAOC. [^50]
   - If no (55 lb or more): operate under Part 91 with § 44807 exemption. Register with N-number, petition for § 44807 exemption covering Parts 61, 91, and 137, then apply for AAOC. Remote Pilot Certificate required as exemption condition. [^50] [^54]

3. **Is the specific UAS model on the FAA's List of Approved Agricultural UAS (docket FAA-2023-1271)?**
   - If yes: standard review timeline. [^58]
   - If no: review may take longer; additional data may be required. [^50]

4. **Does the operation require BVLOS, multi-UAS, night operations, or flights over people?**
   - Each of these requires additional authorization, whether through Part 107 waivers (for sub-55 lb UAS where waivable) or additional conditions in the § 44807 exemption. [^54] [^60]
